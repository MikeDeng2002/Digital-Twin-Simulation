## Risk and Loss Evaluation

### Risk Aversion  
The participant’s choices in the gain lotteries reveal strong risk aversion. They consistently prefer certain smaller amounts over lotteries with a 50% chance of larger gains, only switching to the lottery when the certain amount approaches or exceeds the expected value. For example, in the $6 lottery (50% chance of $6), they prefer the lottery over certain amounts only at $3 or more, indicating a low willingness to gamble. This pattern is consistent across lotteries with increasing stakes. Thus, the participant is clearly risk-averse in the gain domain.

### Loss Aversion  
In loss lotteries, the participant consistently prefers smaller certain losses over gambles with higher expected losses, never switching to the riskier option even when the alternative loss is substantially smaller. They reject mixed gambles that involve losing $8 with a 50% chance of winning various amounts, always preferring the sure $0. This indicates very high loss aversion, with losses weighted much more heavily than equivalent gains. Cross-referencing with risk aversion, both are strongly aligned toward risk- and loss-averse behavior, indicating a consistent pattern of cautiousness and avoidance of uncertainty in both domains.

## Fairness and Social Preferences

### Ultimatum Game  
As sender, the participant offers $0 out of $5 to the other person, keeping the entire amount, representing the most selfish possible offer. As receiver, they accept all offers except the $0 offer, rejecting only the most unfair proposal. Specifically, they accept offers where the sender keeps $0 to $4 and sends $5 to $1, but reject the offer where the sender keeps all $5 and sends $0. The minimum acceptable offer is thus $1. This pattern reveals a fairness norm that is highly self-interested when proposing but enforces a minimal fairness floor when receiving. There is a stark asymmetry: the participant offers nothing but rejects nothing but the zero offer, indicating strategic selfishness combined with a minimal tolerance for unfairness.

### Trust Game  
As sender, the participant sends $0 out of $5 to the other person, keeping all the money and sending nothing to be tripled. As receiver, they return $0 for all five possible received amounts ($15, $12, $9, $6, $3), keeping the entire amount each time. The participant applies a consistent rule of returning nothing regardless of the amount received.  

Participant’s thoughts as sender: "need the money," "have to buy new furniture," "they probably have more money than me," "i dont know them," "i owe them no loyalty," "i feel kinda bad because they gave me money."  
Participant’s thoughts as receiver: "maybe i should give them a few dollars," "they arent even a real person," "they probably would do the same," "most people would do the same," "its just a survey," "its just a few dollars."

This reveals very low trust in anonymous others and a reciprocity norm of no return, justified by lack of personal connection and strategic reasoning. The participant is highly self-protective and skeptical in trust interactions.

### Dictator Game  
The participant gives $0 out of $5 to the other person, keeping the entire amount. The other person has no power to reject. Participant’s thoughts: "i need the money," "i dont know them." This allocation reveals no pure altruism or fairness motivation when strategic incentives are absent. Compared to the Ultimatum Game sender offer (also $0), the identical zero giving indicates that the participant’s giving is not strategic but reflects a genuine selfish preference.
