## Risk and Loss Evaluation

### Risk Aversion  
The participant’s choices in the gain lotteries show a pattern of risk aversion with a CRRA coefficient estimated around +0.5. They consistently prefer certain smaller amounts over lotteries with equal or higher expected value until the certain amount approaches the lottery expected value. For example, they choose the lottery over certain amounts only when the certain amount is close to or exceeds the expected value. This indicates moderate risk aversion in gains.

### Loss Aversion  
The participant exhibits strong loss aversion with a lambda coefficient well above 2.0. In loss lotteries, they consistently prefer smaller certain losses over gambles with equal or higher expected losses, rejecting mixed gambles even when the gain side is substantial. They never switch to the lottery side in loss or mixed gambles, indicating extreme aversion to losses. Cross-referencing with risk aversion shows a consistent pattern of moderate risk aversion in gains combined with very high loss aversion, a pattern typical of prevention-focused individuals.

## Fairness and Social Preferences

### Mental Accounting  
The participant’s mental accounting score is approximately 75%, indicating a strong tendency to segregate gains and losses into separate mental accounts rather than integrating them. They prefer two small wins over one combined win of equal value (World Series lottery), are more upset by separate tax letters than a combined one (IRS letters), and show mixed responses in lottery-plus-rug and car damage-plus-pool win scenarios consistent with mental accounting predictions.

### Ultimatum Game  
As sender, the participant offers $1 out of $5, keeping $4, which is an unusually low and selfish offer (20% to other). As receiver, they accept offers of $5, $4, $3, and $2 (i.e., when the sender keeps $0, $1, $2, or $3) but reject offers of $1 or $0 to themselves (sender keeps $4 or $5). The minimum acceptable offer is $2 to self (40% of the total). This reveals a fairness norm where the participant is stingy when proposing but enforces a meaningful fairness floor when receiving, rejecting very low offers. There is an asymmetry: selfish giving but moderate tolerance for fairness as receiver.

### Trust Game  
As sender, the participant sends $1 out of $5 (20%), keeping $4, indicating low trust in anonymous strangers. As receiver, the participant returns amounts that approximate about one-third of the received amount but with some variation:  
- Received $15: returns $4 (27%)  
- Received $12: returns $2 (17%)  
- Received $9: returns $1 (11%)  
- Received $6: returns $1 (17%)  
- Received $3: returns $0 (0%)  
No perfectly consistent proportional rule is applied, but the participant tends to return a small fraction, generally less than one-third, decreasing as the amount decreases.  
Participant's thoughts (sender): "Is it fair if I keep more for myself," "Would they have done the same for me," "I should be generous, but I also need to think about my own needs."  
Participant's thoughts (receiver): "They trusted me with this, I should be fair," "Should I reward them if they gave me a lot," "If they were selfish, maybe I shouldn’t send much back."  
These thoughts reveal a fairness and reciprocity frame moderated by self-interest and conditional generosity.

### Dictator Game  
The participant sends $1 out of $5 (20%), keeping $4, identical to the Ultimatum Game offer. The other person has no power to reject.  
Participant's thoughts: "How much do I need this money right now?" "Will the other person be fair if the roles were reversed?" "I don’t know who they are, should that matter?" "I want to be fair but also don’t want to regret giving too much." "What if they really need the money more than I do?"  
This allocation reveals limited pure altruism or fairness motivation absent strategic incentives. Since the Dictator and Ultimatum offers are equal, giving appears driven by fairness preferences rather than strategic considerations, but the low amount sent indicates a generally selfish fairness norm.
