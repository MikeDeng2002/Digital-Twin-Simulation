## Risk and Loss Evaluation

### Risk Aversion  
The participant consistently chooses the lottery option over certain smaller amounts until the certain amount approaches or exceeds the expected value, indicating moderate risk aversion in gains. For example, they prefer the 50% chance at $6 over certain amounts up to about $3, switching to certain amounts above that. This pattern suggests a positive CRRA coefficient, indicating risk aversion but not extreme. The participant never switches to the certain option when it is clearly less than expected value, showing rational risk evaluation.

### Loss Aversion  
In loss lotteries, the participant prefers to avoid larger losses, consistently choosing safer options when the certain loss is close to or less than the expected loss, indicating moderate to high loss aversion. The lambda coefficient is likely above 1.0, showing losses are weighted more heavily than equivalent gains. The participant rejects mixed gambles with losses unless the gain is sufficiently high, confirming loss aversion. The combination of moderate risk aversion and moderate to high loss aversion is a consistent pattern.

## Fairness and Social Preferences

### Mental Accounting  
The participant shows a strong preference for mental accounting separation, choosing scenarios consistent with segregating gains and losses in separate mental accounts in 100% of the four binary-choice scenarios. For example, they indicate that Person A (two smaller wins) is happier than Person B (one combined win), and Person A (two separate tax bills) is more upset than Person B (one combined bill). This pattern shows a strong tendency to treat gains and losses separately rather than integrating them.

### Ultimatum Game  
As sender, the participant offers $1 out of $5, keeping $4, which is an unusually low offer indicating selfishness or minimal fairness motivation. As receiver, they accept all offers except the $0 offer, rejecting only the most unfair offer. Specifically, they accept offers when the sender keeps $0, $1, $2, $3, or $4, and reject only when the sender keeps $5. The minimum acceptable offer is $1. This reveals an asymmetry: the participant is stingy as a sender but tolerant as a receiver, enforcing a minimal fairness floor but not generous in giving.

### Trust Game  
As sender, the participant sends $1 out of $5, keeping $4, indicating low trust in anonymous others. As receiver, the participant returns amounts that approximate a fixed dollar amount rather than a fixed proportion:  
- Received $15: returns $4 (27%)  
- Received $12: returns $6 (50%)  
- Received $9: returns $6 (67%)  
- Received $6: returns $5 (83%)  
- Received $3: returns $3 (100%)  

The returns do not follow a consistent proportional rule but tend to return a moderate fixed amount ($3-$6), increasing as the amount received decreases proportionally. This suggests a reciprocity norm focused on returning a reasonable fixed sum rather than proportional fairness.

Participant's thoughts (sender): "I did not want to not send anything" and "I wanted to try to keep the most money for myself."  
Participant's thoughts (receiver): "I just wanted to end up with the most money or at least close to the most."  

These thoughts reveal a reasoning frame focused on self-interest and maximizing personal payoff rather than fairness or altruism.

### Dictator Game  
The participant sends $1 out of $5, keeping $4, showing low altruism or fairness motivation when strategic incentives are absent. Participant's thoughts: "I wanted to keep most for myself," "I did not want to look too cheap," and "I did not want to give nothing so I gave a dollar." This indicates a minimal fairness concern driven by social image rather than genuine altruism. Compared to the Ultimatum Game offer ($1 sent), the Dictator and Ultimatum offers are equal, suggesting that giving is driven by fairness preferences rather than strategic considerations.

## Self-Report Reliability for This File  
The stated Values scale shows a coherent pattern with no strong acquiescence compression and aligns moderately with behavioral data. However, there is a notable stated-vs-revealed gap: the participant rates Compassion and Altruism moderately high (7 and 5 respectively) but behaves selfishly in economic games (low offers in Ultimatum and Dictator, low trust game sending). The Social Desirability score is not provided here, but the participant’s behavioral choices suggest a preference for self-interest despite stated communal values. Given this, the behavioral game data should be weighted more heavily than stated values for simulating fairness and altruism, while values can inform motivational background.
