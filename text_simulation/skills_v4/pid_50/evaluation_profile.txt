### Loss Aversion  
Lambda coefficient: Approximately 2.0 (high loss aversion).  
In loss lotteries, the participant consistently prefers smaller certain losses over larger probabilistic losses, indicating strong aversion to losses. They reject mixed gambles unless the gain is substantially higher than the loss, confirming a high weighting of losses relative to gains.

The combination of strong risk aversion in gains and high loss aversion in losses is a consistent pattern, reflecting a cautious and prevention-focused evaluation of uncertain outcomes.

### Trust Game  
Sender: Sent 0% of $5 to the other person (kept all $5, sent $0).  
Receiver returns:  
- Received $15: returned $0 (0%)  
- Received $12: returned $0 (0%)  
- Received $9: returned $0 (0%)  
- Received $6: returned $0 (0%)  
- Received $3: returned $0 (0%)  
Rule: Always returns nothing regardless of amount received.  
Participant's thoughts (sender): "I would always keep more for myself. This sounds selfish, but I was never given anything in life and am hesitant to do the same. Again the fact that they are anonymous helped me decide to keep more for myself."  
Participant's thoughts (receiver): "I would send some money back to a person because they sent something to me. I would send half of what a person sent to me as recompense. I would be less apt to send money to someone if I didn't get anything from them. The fact that they are anonymous helps with all decisions."  
Despite the stated reciprocity norm in thoughts (sending back half what was sent), actual behavior is fully non-reciprocal, returning nothing in all cases. This reveals a strong gap between stated reciprocity ideals and revealed selfishness in behavior. The participant trusts no one (sends nothing) and reciprocates nothing, despite verbalized norms of fairness and reciprocity.

However, there is a notable stated-vs.-revealed gap: the participant rates Compassion and Altruism low and Agentic values low, yet economic games reveal strong selfishness (Dictator and Trust Games) despite moderate Ultimatum offers and stated reciprocity thoughts. ## Summary  
This participant values communal principles such as harmony, honesty, autonomy, and competence, while rejecting power, status, and ambition. They are not minimalists but practice mindful curation, and seek uniqueness selectively through personal style rather than brand novelty. Socially, they show moderate self-monitoring and somewhat unstable self-concept clarity. They are strongly risk- and loss-averse, consistent with a prevention-focused orientation. Mental accounting is weak, favoring integration of gains and losses. Fairness norms reveal a moderate Ultimatum offer but full acceptance of unfair offers, indicating a lenient fairness floor. Trust and Dictator Games show extreme selfishness, with no trust sent and no altruistic giving absent strategic incentives, despite stated reciprocity ideals. The largest predictive feature for pricing behavior is the participant’s low minimalism combined with moderate uniqueness, suggesting selective but not frugal consumption. The dominant fairness norm is strategic fairness in Ultimatum but selfishness in non-strategic contexts. The major stated-vs.-revealed gap is between moderate stated altruism/reciprocity and revealed selfishness in economic games, which the simulation must navigate carefully.
