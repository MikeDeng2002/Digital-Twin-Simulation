## Risk and Loss Evaluation

### Risk Aversion  
The participant’s choices in the gain lotteries show a pattern of risk aversion. They consistently prefer the certain smaller amount over the risky lottery until the lottery value becomes sufficiently high, switching late in the list. This yields a positive CRRA coefficient indicating risk aversion. The participant never switches to the lottery at very low values, indicating consistent risk-averse behavior in gains.

### Loss Aversion  
In loss lotteries, the participant shows moderate to strong loss aversion, preferring to avoid losses and accepting smaller sure losses over risky larger losses. The lambda coefficient is above 1.0, indicating losses are weighted more heavily than equivalent gains. The participant also rejects mixed gambles that involve losing money, consistent with loss aversion. Cross-referencing with risk aversion, both are aligned in the risk-averse and loss-averse direction, indicating a consistent pattern of cautious evaluation of uncertain outcomes.

## Fairness and Social Preferences

### Mental Accounting  
The participant’s choices in the four mental accounting scenarios show a low consistency with mental accounting predictions (<25%), indicating a preference for integrating gains and losses rather than segregating them into separate mental accounts. For example, they prefer the single combined win over two smaller wins and treat combined losses and gains more holistically. This suggests weak mental account separation.

### Ultimatum Game  
As sender, the participant offers to keep $2 and send $3 of $5 (60% to other), which is a moderately generous offer, above the typical 40-50% fairness norm. As receiver, the participant accepts offers of $5, $4, $3, and $2 to themselves but rejects offers of $1 and $0. The minimum acceptable offer is $2. This indicates a fairness norm that enforces a meaningful floor at 40% of the total amount, rejecting very low offers but accepting moderately low ones. The participant is more generous as sender than the minimum they accept as receiver, showing some fairness motivation but also strategic considerations.

### Trust Game  
As sender, the participant sends $0 of $5 to the other person, keeping all the money and sending nothing to be tripled. As receiver, the participant keeps all amounts received and returns $0 in every case (received $15, $12, $9, $6, $3; returned $0 each time). The participant applies a consistent rule of zero reciprocity, returning nothing regardless of amount received.  
Participant's thoughts (sender): "People are greedy." "People like to think they're generous, but they're not. They will not send money back."  
Participant's thoughts (receiver): "I hate society so I will only look out for myself." "They should have kept all the money."  
This reveals a strong distrust of others and a norm of selfishness in trust interactions, rejecting any expectation of reciprocity.

### Dictator Game  
The participant sent $0 of $5 to the other person, keeping all the money.  
Participant's thoughts: "I don't care about anyone else." "I look out for myself only." "$5 is a lot of money."  
This allocation reveals no pure altruism or fairness motivation when strategic incentives are absent. Compared to the Ultimatum Game sender offer (sending $3), the lower Dictator offer indicates that the participant gives more in Ultimatum Game due to strategic concerns (fear of rejection), but is purely selfish when no punishment is possible.

## Self-Report Reliability for This File  
The stated Values scale shows a strong acquiescence pattern with very low ratings on communal and altruistic values but moderate ratings on autonomy and some agentic values. Behavioral game data reveal a strong selfishness pattern in economic games (Trust and Dictator Games), moderate fairness in Ultimatum Game, and consistent risk- and loss-averse preferences. There is a clear stated-vs.-revealed gap: the participant rates compassion and altruism as unimportant and behaves selfishly in games, consistent with stated values. Social Desirability is not provided here, but given the participant’s blunt self-descriptions and low social desirability indicators (e.g., disagreement with politeness and cooperation), the behavioral data should be weighted more heavily than stated values for social preferences. The Values scale is internally consistent but reflects a socially detached, agentic profile that aligns with revealed selfishness.

## Summary

They show moderate consumer minimalism without a desire for uniqueness, indicating a preference for simplicity but conventional consumption. Socially, they are moderately self-monitoring but have a very clear and stable self-concept. Their economic game behavior reveals a fairness norm that enforces a minimum acceptable offer in Ultimatum Games but a strong distrust and selfishness in Trust and Dictator Games, indicating strategic fairness rather than pure altruism. Risk and loss preferences are consistently risk- and loss-averse, reflecting cautious evaluation of uncertain outcomes. Mental accounting is weak, showing integration rather than segregation of gains and losses. The most predictive feature for pricing behavior is the moderate minimalism combined with a strong stranger-selfishness pattern in social games.
