Cross-referencing with the moderate risk aversion, this participant shows a consistent pattern of cautious evaluation of uncertainty, with both moderate risk aversion and strong loss aversion.

### Ultimatum Game  
As sender, the participant offers $2 to the other person and keeps $3 (40% offer), which is slightly below the typical 40-50% fairness norm but not extremely selfish. As receiver, the participant accepts all offers except the $0 offer, rejecting the most selfish possible offer. The minimum acceptable offer is $1 (20%), enforcing a meaningful fairness floor. This pattern shows moderate fairness motivation: the participant is willing to offer a somewhat low but not minimal amount and rejects completely unfair offers, indicating a balanced fairness norm with some tolerance for unequal splits but a clear rejection of total selfishness.

### Trust Game  
As sender, the participant sends $5 (100% of $5) to the other person, maximizing trust and generosity. As receiver, the participant returns amounts that approximate a consistent rule of returning about half of the received amount:  
- Received $15: returns $8 (53%)  
- Received $12: returns $6 (50%)  
- Received $9: returns $5 (56%)  
- Received $6: returns $3 (50%)  
- Received $3: returns $2 (67%)  

This indicates a reciprocity norm of roughly 50% return, with slight generosity at the lowest amount.  
Participant’s thoughts as sender: "I should try to split it evenly," "Try to keep things fair," "What would I want someone else to do," reflecting a fairness and reciprocity frame.  
Participant’s thoughts as receiver: "Try to keep things fair," "Try to split things evenly," "What would I want someone to do for me," "I would try to give them the extra money because I wouldn't get anything without them sending me something," showing a fairness and strategic reciprocity reasoning.  
Overall, the participant shows very high trust and a strong norm of reciprocal fairness.

### Dictator Game  
The participant sent $2 (40%) of $5 to the other person, keeping $3. The other person had no power to reject. Participant’s thoughts: "I'd like to be kind of fair in splitting the money," "I'd also like to keep a bit more for myself," "I will give myself just one dollar more than the other person," "I feel that was a good, fair thing to do," "Everyone could use a little something," "Why not be nice and give the other person something."  
This allocation reveals moderate altruism and fairness motivation absent strategic incentives. The Dictator offer ($2) is equal to the Ultimatum offer ($2), indicating that giving is driven by fairness preferences rather than strategic considerations.
