Minimum acceptable offer: $2 (40% of total).

As a receiver, they reject very low offers (below 40%), enforcing a meaningful fairness floor. There is symmetry between sender generosity and receiver tolerance, both reflecting moderate fairness concerns.

### Trust Game  
Sender: Sent 40% of $5 to the other person (sent $2, kept $3).  
Receiver returns for each received amount:  
- Received $15 (sent $5 x3): returned $7 (47%)  
- Received $12 (sent $4 x3): returned $5 (42%)  
- Received $9  (sent $3 x3): returned $4 (44%)  
- Received $6  (sent $2 x3): returned $3 (50%)  
- Received $3  (sent $1 x3): returned $1 (33%)  

The participant applies a roughly proportional return rule, returning about 40-50% of the amount received, with a slight tendency to return about half for smaller amounts and slightly less for larger amounts.  

Participant's thoughts (sender): "I want to ensure i end up with something. I want to maximize my money. I will trust the person a little bit but not fully. I will secure some amount of money for myself. hopefully they send some back."  
Participant's thoughts (receiver): "I want more than the other person. I should be nice and send some back. I'll send back less than half, but close to half."  

This reveals moderate trust in anonymous others, tempered by caution. Reciprocity norms favor fairness but with self-interest, sending back less than half but not stingily. The reasoning frame balances trust with self-protection and fairness.
