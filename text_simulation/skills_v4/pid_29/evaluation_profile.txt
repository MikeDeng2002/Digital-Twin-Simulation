## Risk and Loss Evaluation

### Risk Aversion  
The participant’s choices in the gain lotteries show a pattern of strong risk aversion. For all three gain lotteries (50% chance of winning $6, $8, or $10), they consistently prefer the certain smaller amounts over the lottery until the certain amount approaches the lottery’s expected value. For example, in the $6 lottery, they prefer the lottery over certain amounts only starting at $2.00 and above; in the $8 and $10 lotteries, they switch to the lottery at amounts closer to the expected value. This pattern indicates moderate to strong risk aversion in gains. They never switch to the lottery when the certain amount is very low, showing consistent risk-averse preferences.

### Loss Aversion  
In the loss lotteries, the participant shows a mixed pattern. For the 50% chance of losing $6, they prefer the lottery over certain losses down to about -$5.50, indicating some risk tolerance in losses. However, for the 50% chance of losing $8 or losing $10, they consistently prefer the certain loss over the lottery, indicating risk aversion in losses as well. The lambda coefficient (loss aversion) is above 1.0, indicating losses are weighted more heavily than equivalent gains. The participant also rejects mixed gambles that involve losing $8 with a 50% chance of winning various amounts, preferring the sure $0, showing strong loss aversion. Cross-referencing risk and loss aversion, the participant is consistently risk-averse and loss-averse, a coherent pattern.

### Ultimatum Game  
As sender, the participant offers $2 to the other person and keeps $3, which is 40% offered and 60% kept. This is slightly below the typical 40-50% fairness norm but not extremely low. As receiver, the participant accepts all offers except the $0 offer, which they reject. They accept offers of $1, $2, $3, $4, and $5 for themselves. The minimum acceptable offer is $1. This pattern shows a fairness norm that is moderately generous in giving and moderately tolerant in receiving, enforcing a fairness floor by rejecting zero offers but accepting low offers otherwise. There is no asymmetry between sender and receiver behavior; the participant offers a fair amount and rejects unfair zero offers.

### Dictator Game  
The participant, as sender, offers $2 to the other person and keeps $3 (40% sent). The other person has no power to reject. Participant’s thoughts: "Being fair," "Being honest."  

This allocation is equal to the Ultimatum Game offer, indicating that giving is driven by fairness preferences rather than strategic considerations. The participant is motivated by fairness and honesty even when there is no strategic incentive to give.
