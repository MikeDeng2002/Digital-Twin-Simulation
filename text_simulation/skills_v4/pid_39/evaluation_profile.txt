## Risk and Loss Evaluation

### Risk Aversion

### Loss Aversion  
Lambda coefficient: Approximately 2.0 (losses weighted about twice as much as gains). The participant prefers smaller certain losses over lotteries with 50% chance of larger losses, switching only when the certain loss is close to or exceeds the expected loss, indicating strong loss aversion. In the mixed gamble scenario (50% chance of losing $8, 50% chance of winning x), the participant prefers the sure $0 over lotteries with x = $7 or $8 but accepts when x reaches $9 or higher, consistent with a loss aversion coefficient around 2.0.

Cross-referencing risk and loss aversion shows a consistent pattern: the participant is both risk-averse in gains and strongly loss-averse, indicating a cautious and prevention-focused evaluation of uncertain outcomes.

### Ultimatum Game  
Sender: Offered 40% of $5 to the other person (kept $2, sent $3).  
Receiver: Accepted offers when the sender kept $0, $1, $2, or $3 (i.e., offers of $5, $4, $3, $2 to receiver), rejected offer when sender kept $4 (offer $1), and accepted offer when sender kept $5 (offer $0).  
Minimum acceptable offer: $2 to self (i.e., rejects offers giving only $1 to receiver).  

The participant’s fairness norm as sender is moderately fair, offering 40%, which is typical but not generous (50% or more). As receiver, they reject very low offers ($1) but accept even the most selfish offer ($0), indicating some tolerance for unfairness but a floor at very low offers. The acceptance of the $0 offer is unusual and suggests a complex fairness tolerance or strategic acceptance.

### Trust Game  
Sender: Sent 60% of $5 to the other person (sent $3, kept $2).  
Receiver returns:  
- Received $15 (sent $5 x3): returned $5 (33%)  
- Received $12 (sent $4 x3): returned $4 (33%)  
- Received $9  (sent $3 x3): returned $3 (33%)  
- Received $6  (sent $2 x3): returned $2 (33%)  
- Received $3  (sent $1 x3): returned $1 (33%)  

The participant applies a consistent reciprocity rule of returning exactly one-third of the amount received.  

Participant's thoughts (sender): "I wanted to think of others," "I wanted to be fair," "just in case they needed the money more than I," "I wanted to do the right thing."  
Participant's thoughts (receiver): "I wanted to be fair," "I wanted to think of others," "I wanted to keep an even playing field."  

This indicates moderate trust in anonymous strangers (sending 60%), a reciprocity norm based on proportional fairness (one-third return), and a fairness reasoning frame emphasizing fairness, consideration, and balance.

### Dictator Game  
Sent 40% of $5 (kept $3, sent $2).  
Participant's thoughts: "wanting to think of others," "fairness," "otherness," "selflessness," "they need it more than me."  

The Dictator offer (40%) is lower than the Ultimatum offer (40% sent, same amount), indicating giving is driven partly by fairness preferences but also strategic considerations. The participant gives less when the recipient cannot punish, suggesting some strategic giving in the Ultimatum Game.
