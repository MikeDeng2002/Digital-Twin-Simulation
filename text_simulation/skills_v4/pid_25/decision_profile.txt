## Decision Search and Closure

### Maximization vs. Satisficing  
Score: 1 / 5. This participant does not search exhaustively for the best possible option but tends to settle for good enough. Their responses strongly reject high standards for always seeking the best and indicate a preference for simpler, less cognitively demanding choices.

### Need for Cognitive Closure (NFCC)  
Score: Approximately 1.0 / 5 (based on strong disagreement with statements indicating discomfort with uncertainty and preference for quick closure). This participant exhibits a very low need for cognitive closure, showing high tolerance for ambiguity and uncertainty. They do not feel impatient or irritated by unresolved problems and prefer to keep an open mind rather than rush to decisions. This low closure need suggests slower, more flexible decision-making and less urgency to commit quickly under uncertainty.

### Regulatory Focus (RFS)  
Score: Approximately 6.0 / 7 (highly promotion-focused). The participant strongly endorses promotion-oriented items such as creativity, achievement, and doing a good job, while rejecting prevention-oriented items like competition and tension from others’ success. This indicates a primary orientation toward achieving ideals and gains rather than avoiding losses or fulfilling obligations. Directionally, this participant is more responsive to gain-framing and opportunity-framing in decision tasks. This high promotion focus is consistent with their moderate spendthrift tendency and low present bias (see below), suggesting openness to future gains and opportunities rather than loss avoidance.

### IPIP Conscientiousness (Wave 2)  
Score: 8 / 8 (extremely high). The participant rates themselves as extremely organized, efficient, systematic, and practical, with strong agreement on positive conscientiousness traits and strong disagreement on negative traits. This score is consistent with the Big Five Conscientiousness score in background.txt (not provided here), but given the extreme self-ratings, any divergence would be notable. ## Time Preferences

### Discount Rate  
Annualized discount rate: Approximately 400% (extremely high impatience). The participant consistently prefers the sooner-smaller reward over the later-larger reward across all three multiple price lists, switching only at the highest delayed amounts or not at all. This pattern indicates extreme impatience and a strong preference for immediate rewards over future gains. Compared to typical US adult rates (20%-200%), this participant is highly impatient.

### Present Bias  
Present bias coefficient: Approximately +0.8 (strong present bias). The participant shows a strong preference for immediate rewards beyond what the base discount rate predicts, indicating present-biased preferences. This is directionally consistent with their moderate spendthrift score, as present bias often correlates with difficulty restraining spending and favoring immediate gratification.

## Summary  
This participant exhibits a moderately spendthrift consumption mode, with occasional difficulty limiting spending but not extreme. They are low in maximization, preferring satisficing and simpler decision strategies, and have a very low need for cognitive closure, indicating tolerance for ambiguity and a relaxed approach to decision speed. Their regulatory focus is strongly promotion-oriented, making them more responsive to gain-framed choices and opportunity-seeking behavior. They are extremely conscientious, suggesting disciplined and systematic decision execution. Metacognitively, they are underconfident and underestimate their cognitive abilities, which are objectively low, indicating realistic self-assessment rather than overconfidence. Their time preferences reveal extreme impatience and strong present bias, favoring immediate rewards heavily over future ones. The most predictive single feature for simulating this person on choice tasks is their extremely high discount rate combined with strong present bias, which will strongly influence their intertemporal choices and impulsivity in spending decisions.
