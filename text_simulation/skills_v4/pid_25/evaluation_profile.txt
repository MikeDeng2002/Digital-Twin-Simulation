## Risk and Loss Evaluation

### Risk Aversion  
The participant’s choices in the three gain lotteries show consistent preference for the lottery over certain amounts until the certain amount approaches the lottery’s expected value, indicating moderate risk aversion. For the $6 lottery (50% chance), the participant always prefers the lottery over certain amounts up to $6.00, switching only when the certain amount equals or exceeds the lottery value. For the $8 lottery, the participant prefers the lottery over certain amounts up to about $3.75, switching to certain amounts at $4.00 and above, indicating risk aversion. For the $10 lottery, the participant prefers the lottery over certain amounts up to about $4.75, switching at $5.00 and above. The CRRA coefficient is positive but moderate, consistent with typical risk-averse behavior.

### Loss Aversion  
In the loss lotteries, the participant consistently prefers the lottery over certain losses until the certain loss is substantially smaller than the expected loss, indicating strong loss aversion. For example, in the 50% chance of losing $6 or $0, the participant prefers the lottery over certain losses down to about -$3.00, switching only at smaller losses. Similarly, for mixed gambles with losses and gains, the participant consistently prefers to avoid sure losses, indicating strong loss aversion. The lambda coefficient is high (>1.0), showing losses are weighted more heavily than equivalent gains. Cross-referencing with the decision_profile.txt Regulatory Focus is not possible here, but the pattern of moderate risk aversion combined with strong loss aversion is consistent and coherent.

## Self-Report Reliability for This File  
The stated Values scale (A.3.3) shows a strong communal and altruistic self-presentation, with high ratings on compassion, altruism, trust, and humility. However, the behavioral economic games reveal a stark contradiction: the participant is maximally selfish in the Ultimatum and Dictator Games, shows low trust and minimal reciprocity in the Trust Game, and exhibits self-focused thoughts during these tasks. This stated-vs.-revealed gap is large and must be explicitly acknowledged. The participant’s Social Desirability score is not provided here, but the strong acquiescence in communal values combined with selfish economic behavior suggests social desirability bias in self-report. The Values scale ratings are internally consistent and not compressed, but the behavioral data should be weighted more heavily in simulations of actual decision-making, especially in fairness and trust contexts.
