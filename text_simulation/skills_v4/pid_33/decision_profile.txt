## Decision Search and Closure

Score: 3.5 / 5 (approximate average from responses). This participant tends to search moderately exhaustively for the best option but also shows some willingness to settle for good enough, indicating a balanced approach between maximization and satisficing.

## Time Preferences

### Discount Rate  
Annualized discount rate: Approximately 150%. The participant consistently preferred the sooner-smaller reward over the later-larger reward in the multiple price lists until the later-larger reward became substantially better, indicating a relatively high discount rate. This level of impatience is above average compared to typical US adult rates (20%-200%), placing them toward the more impatient end of the spectrum. They show a clear preference for immediate rewards over delayed ones, reflecting impatience in intertemporal trade-offs.

### Present Bias  
Present bias coefficient: +0.25 (positive, indicating present bias). The participant shows a moderate present bias, preferring immediate rewards more strongly than predicted by their base discount rate. This present bias aligns directionally with their moderate tightwad-spendthrift score, reflecting some tension between wanting to save/control spending and a preference for immediate gratification.

## Summary  
This participant exhibits a moderately balanced spending style, tending neither toward extreme tightwad nor spendthrift behavior, but with some difficulty in spending money. They show a high need for cognitive closure, favoring quick, decisive choices and a promotion-focused regulatory style that orients them toward gains and opportunities rather than loss avoidance. Their conscientiousness is high, supporting organized and efficient decision execution. Metacognitively, they are moderately overconfident and overplace themselves relative to peers despite below-average cognitive performance, indicating a notable miscalibration. Their time preferences reveal impatience and moderate present bias, favoring immediate rewards. The most predictive feature for simulating this person’s choice behavior is their promotion-focused regulatory orientation, which will strongly influence their responsiveness to gain-framed versus loss-framed decisions.
