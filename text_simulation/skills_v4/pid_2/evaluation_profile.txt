### Ultimatum Game  
Sender: Offered 40% of $5 to the other person (kept $2, sent $3).  
Receiver: Accepted offers when the sender kept $0, $1, $2, or $3 (i.e., offers of $5, $4, $3, $2 to receiver). Rejected offers when sender kept $4 or $5 (offers of $1 or $0 to receiver). Minimum acceptable offer: $2 (40% of total).  

This participant’s fairness norm is moderately generous as a sender, offering a fair split slightly favoring themselves. As a receiver, they enforce a fairness floor by rejecting very low offers (<20%). There is symmetry between sender generosity and receiver tolerance, indicating a fairness norm that balances self-interest with fairness enforcement.

### Trust Game  
Sender: Sent 40% of $5 ($2 sent, $3 kept).  
Receiver returns for each received amount:  
- Received $15 (sent $5 x3): returned $8 (53%)  
- Received $12 (sent $4 x3): returned $6 (50%)  
- Received $9  (sent $3 x3): returned $4 (44%)  
- Received $6  (sent $2 x3): returned $3 (50%)  
- Received $3  (sent $1 x3): returned $1 (33%)  

The participant applies a roughly proportional return rule, returning about half of the received amount except for the smallest amount where they return one-third.  

Participant's thoughts (sender): "I doubted they'd send any money after tripling," "I didn't want to give them all the money, even if that was best idea," "I don't trust the recipient," "I'd like to test the recipient with a smaller amount."  
Participant's thoughts (receiver): "I need to be more selfish in life," "Being generous does not pay," "Everyone is looking to get ahead, why not me?" "I am keeping some money for myself as insurance of getting something," "It's fair to split and if they were somewhat generous I will be generous back," "If they were cheap with me, I'll be cheap back."  

This participant shows moderate trust as sender (sending less than half), reflecting suspicion and strategic caution. As receiver, they apply a reciprocity norm based on fairness and conditional generosity, balancing self-interest with fairness. Thought listings reveal a reasoning frame of cautious reciprocity and mistrust, with strategic considerations about fairness and self-protection.

### Dictator Game  
Sent 0% of $5 (kept all $5, sent $0).  
Participant's thoughts: "The other person is MAGA," "The other person is a fascist boomer," "Everyone is out for themselves, I should be out for myself for a change," "If it was the other way around, the other person would keep the entire $5," "Don't be a chump."

Compared to the Ultimatum Game sender offer of 40%, the difference indicates strategic giving: the participant gives more when the recipient can punish (Ultimatum) but gives nothing when no punishment is possible (Dictator). This suggests fairness preferences are partly strategic rather than pure altruism.

The stated Values scale (A.3.3) shows strong communal values and altruism, but behavioral game data reveal a gap: the participant states high compassion and politeness but behaves selfishly in the Dictator Game and cautiously in Trust and Ultimatum Games. Social Desirability score is not provided here, but the participant’s thought listings and behavior suggest skepticism and mistrust, possibly reducing the reliability of stated altruism. The Values scale ratings are well spread, so acquiescence is not a concern. Given the observed stated-vs-revealed gap (high stated altruism vs. low Dictator giving and cautious trust), downstream simulation should weigh behavioral game data more heavily than stated values when conflicts arise.
