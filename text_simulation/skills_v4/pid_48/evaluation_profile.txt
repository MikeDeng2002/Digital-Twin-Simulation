### Ultimatum Game  
Sender: Offered 40% of $5 to the other person (kept $3, sent $2).  
Receiver: Rejects offers of $5, $4 (i.e., when other keeps $0 or $1), accepts offers of $3, $2, $1, and $0 (i.e., when other keeps $2, $3, $4, or $5). Minimum acceptable offer is $3 to self (i.e., rejects offers less than 60% to self).  

This participant proposes a moderately selfish split (keeping 60%), but enforces a fairness floor by rejecting very generous offers to the other (0 or 1 dollar). The asymmetry suggests a fairness norm that expects a reasonable share for self and is intolerant of offers that give the participant too little, but the sender offer is somewhat stingy compared to typical 50% splits.

### Trust Game  
Sender: Sent 20% of $5 to the other person (kept $4, sent $1).  
Receiver returns:  
- Received $15: returns $7 (47%)  
- Received $12: returns $6 (50%)  
- Received $9: returns $5 (56%)  
- Received $6: returns $3 (50%)  
- Received $3: returns $1 (33%)  

The participant applies a roughly proportional return rule, returning about one-third to one-half of the amount received, with a tendency toward returning close to half for mid-range amounts.  

Participant's thoughts (sender): "split the outcome 50:50; if 50:50 not possible, take slightly more."  
Participant's thoughts (receiver): "split evenly if possible; if even not possible, take more in the option closest to 50/50."  

This reveals a reciprocity norm focused on fairness and approximate equality, with a slight self-favoring bias. The participant trusts others modestly (sending only 20%) but expects fair reciprocation.

### Dictator Game  
Sent 40% of $5 (kept $3, sent $2). The other person had no power to reject.  
Participant's thoughts: "split money evenly; if even split not possible, take option that allows split to be as close as possible to even; in the event of uneven split, take option in my favor that minimizes amount of divergence between me and the 2nd person."  

This allocation matches the Ultimatum Game offer exactly, indicating that giving is driven by fairness preferences rather than strategic considerations. The participant is moderately altruistic, willing to share a substantial portion even when the recipient cannot punish.
