## Risk and Loss Evaluation

### Risk Aversion  
The participant’s choices in the gain lotteries reveal strong risk aversion. In three gain lotteries (50% chance of winning varying amounts), they consistently prefer the certain smaller amount over the lottery until the certain amount nearly equals the expected value, switching only at relatively high sure amounts. For example, in the $10 lottery, they switch to the sure amount only at $10 or above, indicating a cautious approach to gains. This pattern shows a clear preference for certainty over risky gains.

### Loss Aversion  
In loss lotteries, the participant consistently prefers sure smaller losses over risky larger losses, switching only at very low sure loss amounts, indicating strong loss aversion. For the mixed gamble (50% chance of losing $8, 50% chance of winning x), they reject the gamble unless the gain is at least $8, showing a roughly symmetric or slightly above symmetric loss aversion (lambda near or above 1). Cross-referencing risk and loss aversion, the participant is both strongly risk-averse and strongly loss-averse, a consistent pattern reflecting cautiousness and sensitivity to losses.

## Fairness and Social Preferences

### Mental Accounting  
The participant’s choices in the four mental accounting scenarios show a strong tendency to separate mental accounts rather than integrate gains and losses. For example, they prefer two smaller wins over one combined win of equal value (World Series lottery), are more upset by a single larger loss than by two smaller losses combined (IRS letters), and show similar patterns in lottery-plus-rug and car damage-plus-pool win scenarios. This indicates a strong mental accounting style, treating gains and losses in separate mental budgets.

### Ultimatum Game  
As sender, the participant offers $3 out of $5 (60%) to the other person, keeping $2. This is a moderately generous offer, above the typical 40-50% range, signaling fairness motivation. As receiver, they accept all offers from $0 to $5, including the most selfish possible offer ($0 to self, $5 to other). This indicates a very high tolerance for unfair offers, with no meaningful fairness floor enforced. The asymmetry between moderately generous giving and accepting all offers suggests a fairness norm that values generosity but is highly tolerant of selfishness from others.

Participant’s thoughts (sender): "share," "kind," "compassion," "fair," "nice," "caring."  
Participant’s thoughts (receiver): "kindness," "compassion," "fairness," "helping," "kind," "need."  

This reveals a reasoning frame centered on kindness, fairness, and compassion, with a norm of proportional reciprocity. The participant shows moderate trust and a clear, consistent reciprocity norm.

### Dictator Game  
The participant sent $2 out of $5 (40%) to the other person, keeping $3. The other person had no power to reject. Participant’s thoughts: "I don't want to be greedy," "I need the money but the other person might need it also," "I want to be a kind person," "I want to be a generous person," "Everyone needs more money," "I like to be fair."  

This allocation is lower than the Ultimatum Game offer (40% vs. 60%), indicating that the participant gives more strategically when the recipient can punish (Ultimatum) and less when no punishment is possible (Dictator). This suggests that fairness motivation is partly strategic rather than purely altruistic.
