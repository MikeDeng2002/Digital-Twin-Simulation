## Core Values

### Agentic vs. Communal Values  
The 24-item Agentic vs. Communal Values scale shows a clear spread of ratings without acquiescence compression; values range from very low (1-3) to very high (8-9), indicating meaningful differentiation rather than uniform high ratings. The participant places strong emphasis on communal values, rating ALTRUISM, COMPASSION, EQUALITY, and AUTONOMY at the highest level (9/9). TRUST, FORGIVENESS, HONESTY, and COMPETENCE are also rated highly (8/9). In contrast, agentic values related to dominance and status such as POWER, STATUS, and SUPERIORITY are rated very low (1/9), and WEALTH and RECOGNITION are also low (2-3/9). ACHIEVEMENT and AMBITION are moderate (5-6/9). This profile indicates a strong communal orientation valuing helping others, fairness, and equality, combined with a desire for personal autonomy but a rejection of power and status motives.

## Consumer and Product Preferences

### Consumer Minimalism  
The overall Consumer Minimalism score is approximately 2.3/5, indicating a low tendency toward minimalism. The participant disagrees or is neutral on accumulation avoidance items (e.g., "I avoid accumulating lots of stuff" = 2, "I restrict the number of things I own" = 3), strongly disagrees with aesthetic spareness items (e.g., "I am drawn to visually sparse environments" = 1, "I keep the aesthetic in my home very sparse" = 1), but agrees somewhat on mindful curation items (e.g., "I am mindful of what I own" = 4, "My belongings are mindfully selected" = 4). This pattern suggests the participant does not actively restrict possessions or prefer sparse aesthetics but does engage in some thoughtful selection and curation of belongings.

### Need for Uniqueness  
The Need for Uniqueness score is low to moderate, around 3.3/5. The participant neither agrees nor disagrees or disagrees slightly with statements about seeking uniqueness through consumption (e.g., "I often try to find a more interesting version of run-of-the-mill products" = 2, "I actively seek to develop my personal uniqueness by buying special products" = 2), but agrees somewhat that having an eye for unusual products helps establish a distinctive image (4), and strongly agrees with avoiding popular products (5). This suggests a selective uniqueness orientation, where the participant avoids mainstream products but does not strongly pursue uniqueness through active consumption.

## Risk and Loss Evaluation

### Risk Aversion  
The participant exhibits strong risk aversion in the gain domain, with a CRRA coefficient estimated around +0.8 (positive and near the upper typical range). This is evidenced by consistently preferring certain smaller amounts over lotteries with equal or higher expected values across three gain lotteries, never switching to the lottery side until the certain amount is quite high. For example, in the $6 lottery (50% chance $6, 50% $0), the participant prefers the certain amount up to $3.50 and only switches at $4.00 or higher, indicating risk aversion.

### Loss Aversion  
The participant shows very high loss aversion, with a lambda coefficient estimated above 2.0. In all loss lotteries (50% chance losing $6 or $8, 50% chance losing less or zero), the participant consistently prefers the sure loss over the lottery unless the sure loss is substantially smaller, indicating strong overweighting of losses relative to gains. In the mixed gamble (50% chance losing $8, 50% chance winning x), the participant only prefers the gamble when the gain is at least $13, further confirming high loss aversion.

Cross-referencing risk and loss aversion reveals a consistent pattern of strong aversion to both risk and losses, indicating a prevention-focused orientation (consistent with the participant’s self-description and decision_profile.txt). This suggests cautious evaluation of uncertain outcomes and strong sensitivity to potential losses.

## Fairness and Social Preferences

### Mental Accounting  
The participant’s mental accounting score is 100% consistent with mental accounting predictions across all four binary-choice scenarios. They prefer segregated gains and losses rather than integration: for example, happier with two smaller separate wins than one combined win, and more upset by separate losses than a combined loss of equal value. Item-level data shows Person A (two smaller wins) is happier than Person B (one combined win), Person A (two separate tax letters) is more upset than Person B (one combined letter), Person B (lottery win plus rug damage combined) is less happy than Person A (lottery win only), and Person A (larger car damage with smaller pool win) is more upset than Person B (smaller car damage only). This indicates strong mental segregation of gains and losses.

### Ultimatum Game  
As sender, the participant offers $2 to the other person and keeps $3 (40% offer). As receiver, they accept all offers from $0 to $5 kept by the sender, including the most selfish possible offer ($5 for sender, $0 for receiver). This indicates a fairness norm that is moderately generous in giving but highly tolerant in receiving, accepting even the lowest offers without punishment. There is an asymmetry: the participant offers a fair but not generous split, yet accepts any offer, showing a lenient fairness enforcement.

### Trust Game  
As sender, the participant sends $1 out of $5 (20%), the lowest amount offered. As receiver, the participant returns amounts that approximate about 50-60% of the received amount consistently across all five possible received amounts:  
- Received $15: returns $8 (53%)  
- Received $12: returns $6 (50%)  
- Received $9: returns $5 (56%)  
- Received $6: returns $3 (50%)  
- Received $3: returns $2 (67%)  

The participant applies a roughly consistent proportional reciprocity rule, returning about half to two-thirds of the tripled amount.  

Participant's thoughts (sender): "fairness," "equality," "sharing" — indicating a fairness and sharing frame when deciding how much to send.  
Participant's thoughts (receiver): "win," "loss" — suggesting a focus on outcomes and possibly strategic considerations in returning money.  

Overall, the participant shows low trust in strangers (sending minimal amount) but moderate reciprocity norm, returning a fair share of received funds.

### Dictator Game  
The participant sends $2 out of $5 (40%) to the other person, keeping $3. The other person has no power to reject. Participant's thoughts: "Sharing," "equality," "fairness," "giving," "helping," "community."  

This allocation reveals a moderate level of pure altruism or fairness motivation absent strategic incentives. The Dictator offer equals the Ultimatum offer, indicating that giving is driven by fairness preferences rather than strategic considerations.

The stated Values scale (A.3.3) appears reliable relative to behavioral game data. The participant’s high stated altruism and compassion (rated 9) aligns with moderate giving in Dictator and Ultimatum games (40%), and their communal values correspond with fairness and sharing thoughts. There is no major stated-vs-revealed gap in altruism or fairness. However, the participant’s low trust in strangers (Trust Game sender sends only 20%) contrasts somewhat with high communal values, indicating a mild gap in trust versus stated values. The Values scale shows meaningful differentiation (no acquiescence compression), supporting trust in stated values. Social Desirability score is not provided here, but the consistency between stated values and behavior suggests moderate to high reliability.

## Summary  
This participant strongly prioritizes communal values such as altruism, compassion, equality, and trust, while rejecting agentic values related to power and status. They do not practice consumer minimalism, tending instead toward mindful curation without accumulation avoidance or sparse aesthetics. Their need for uniqueness is selective, avoiding mainstream products but not actively seeking uniqueness through consumption. Socially, they are a high self-monitor, adaptable to social cues, but have low self-concept clarity, indicating fluctuating self-views. They are strongly risk- and loss-averse, consistent with a prevention-focused orientation. Their mental accounting is strongly segregated, preferring to separate gains and losses. Fairness norms show moderate generosity in giving but high tolerance in receiving unfair offers. Trust in strangers is low, but reciprocity norms are moderate and proportional. Dictator and Ultimatum offers are equal, indicating fairness-driven giving rather than strategic. The most predictive feature for pricing behavior is the participant’s low consumer minimalism combined with selective uniqueness, suggesting moderate purchasing with some avoidance of mainstream products. The dominant fairness norm is moderate giving with high acceptance of unfair offers, and the main stated-vs-revealed gap is between high communal values and low trust in strangers. The simulation should balance these aspects when modeling social preferences and economic decisions.
