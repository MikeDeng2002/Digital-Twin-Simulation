## Optimization strategy  
The respondent’s Maximizer scale responses indicate a moderate to high tendency toward maximizing, with agreement on having high standards for themselves (item 5 = 4) and a slight disagreement with never settling for second best (item 6 = 3). They disagree strongly with difficulty shopping for gifts or finding clothing they love (items 3 and 4 = 1), suggesting confidence in their choices. However, they only agree a little with actively seeking uniqueness by buying special products (item 3 = 4) and mostly disagree with breaking social rules or customs in purchasing (items 5-7 = 1 or 2). This suggests a domain-specific split: the respondent maximizes in personal and practical domains (e.g., work, cognitive tasks) but satisficing or conventional in social or consumer uniqueness domains. Their economic game behavior (e.g., offering a fair split in dictator and trust games) aligns with a pragmatic maximizing approach to fairness and reciprocity rather than pure self-interest.

This gap between altruistic values and strategic self-interest in behavior implies nuanced reasoning reliability: the respondent can endorse fairness but also pragmatically prioritize self-benefit when possible. Self-report trustworthiness is generally good but should be interpreted with awareness of this domain-specific gap.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent shows moderate intertemporal patience. In delayed reward tasks, they initially prefer immediate smaller rewards but switch to larger delayed rewards as the immediate option improves, indicating some discounting but willingness to wait for better payoffs. Their conscientiousness (Big Five item 38 "Makes plans and follows through" = 5) and self-reported organization support planning and patience. The respondent’s choices in intertemporal tasks show a balanced approach, neither impulsive nor overly patient.

## Bias susceptibility predictions  
Predictable biases from cognitive signals include:

- Probability matching vs. maximizing: The respondent’s cognitive style and lottery choices suggest a maximizing tendency, preferring consistent better options over matching probabilities (e.g., choosing certain amounts over lotteries at values below expected value).

- Dominator (denominator) neglect: The respondent correctly interprets probabilities and percentages in financial literacy and probability questions, indicating low susceptibility.

- Belief bias in syllogisms: Some errors in logical reasoning (e.g., accepting invalid conclusions) suggest moderate susceptibility to belief bias.

- Wason matching bias: The respondent correctly identifies cards to test conditional rules (choosing A and 3), indicating low susceptibility.

For biases weakly predictable from cognitive signals (framing effects, anchoring, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, outcome bias, sunk-cost effect, less-is-more effect, omission bias, Allais/ certainty effects, myside bias), predictive limits apply. The respondent’s conservative political views and some social attitudes may influence these biases, but cognitive data do not allow confident predictions.

Overall, the respondent’s reasoning under uncertainty is reliable but nuanced by domain-specific gaps between values and behavior.
