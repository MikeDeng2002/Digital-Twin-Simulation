## Budget constraint  
The respondent reports a total family income of $100,000 or more before taxes (income item). They are currently employed full-time and live in a household with more than four people. No further direct information about spending style or budget envelope is provided.

## Values hierarchy  
The 24-item values scale shows some rating compression, with many values rated quite high (6 to 9). The highest-rated values (rated 9) are Influence (item 4), Humility (7), Ambition (10), Loyalty (11), Honesty (16), and Equality (21). The lowest-rated value is Power (13) rated 1, and Superiority (24) rated 2. Other values such as Wealth (1) and Politeness (12) are rated moderately (5). This indicates a relative structure emphasizing social influence, humility, ambition, loyalty, honesty, and equality, with low importance placed on dominance or power.

## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
The respondent strongly agrees with minimalism-related items such as avoiding accumulating lots of stuff (1), actively avoiding excess possessions (4), being drawn to visually sparse environments (5), keeping a sparse aesthetic at home (7), preferring empty spaces (8), and being mindful of what they own (9, 11). They also agree somewhat with restricting the number of things owned (2), preferring simplicity in design (6), and carefully curating belongings (10, 12). Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking, the respondent generally disagrees or only slightly agrees with statements about seeking uniqueness or breaking social rules in consumption (items 1-12 in that scale), indicating low to moderate uniqueness-seeking and consumerism orientation.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery over certain smaller amounts when the lottery expected value is higher, but switches to the certain amount when it becomes more favorable, indicating moderate risk aversion. For example, they prefer the lottery over amounts up to about $3 but switch to certain amounts above that. They reject offers that give them nothing and accept offers with positive payoffs in ultimatum games, showing some fairness concerns but also self-interest. In loss lotteries, they prefer to avoid larger losses and accept smaller sure losses, consistent with risk aversion in losses. Overall, the respondent exhibits moderate risk aversion in gains and losses.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent’s choices in delayed reward tasks show a preference for immediate smaller rewards over delayed larger rewards in many cases, especially when the delay difference is small. For example, they choose $3 now over $6 in 1 week, and $4 now over $8 in 1 week, but switch to delayed rewards only when the delayed amount is substantially larger. This pattern indicates moderate impatience or discounting of future rewards.

## Social preferences  
In dictator and ultimatum game scenarios, the respondent proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to the other person when splitting $5, indicating a preference for fairness or generosity. As a receiver, they accept all offers except the one where they receive nothing, showing rejection of unfair offers. In the trust game with tripled amounts, they keep $3 and send $2, and as the receiver, they keep a large share (e.g., keep $13 of $15) and send back a small amount, indicating strategic self-interest tempered by some reciprocity. Their thoughts during these decisions emphasize fairness when allocating money but maximizing earnings when receiving, suggesting a balance between fairness and self-interest.

## Summary  
The respondent has a household income above $100,000 with full-time employment and a large household. Their values emphasize influence, humility, ambition, loyalty, honesty, and equality, while power and superiority are least important. They strongly endorse minimalist attitudes, avoiding excess possessions and preferring simplicity, and show low to moderate uniqueness-seeking in consumption. Risk preferences indicate moderate risk aversion in gains and losses, and intertemporal choices reveal moderate impatience. Social preferences reflect a balance between fairness and self-interest, with generous offers in dictator games and strategic behavior in trust games. Environmental concern is low, with strong disagreement on environmentally responsible behaviors.
