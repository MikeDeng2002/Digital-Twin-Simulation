## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the 12-item minimalism scale, the respondent shows moderate agreement with minimalism-related statements. They neither strongly agree nor disagree with avoiding accumulation (item 1: 3), but agree a little with restricting possessions (2: 4), actively avoiding excess possessions (4: 4), being drawn to sparse environments (5: 4), keeping home aesthetics sparse (7: 4), and being mindful of what they own (9: 4). The selection of things owned is "strongly agreed" as carefully curated (10: 5). Other items show neutral or mixed responses. Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking, the respondent agrees a little that they combine possessions to create a unique personal image (item 1: 4), break customs and rules in product use (item 5: 4), have violated social group rules about buying or owning (6: 4), and avoid products bought by the general population (10: 4) and dislike products customarily bought by everyone (11: 4). They disagree a little with actively seeking special products or brands (3: 2) and neither agree nor disagree with enjoying being original (2: 3). This indicates moderate minimalism and a mild tendency toward consumer uniqueness and rule-breaking in consumption. (Cited: Minimalism items 1-12; Consumerism/uniqueness items 1-12)

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent generally prefers the lottery over small certain amounts when the certain amount is low but switches preference to the certain amount as it approaches the expected value of the lottery, indicating moderate risk aversion. For example, for a 50% chance of $6 lottery, they prefer the lottery over $1 but switch to the certain amount at $1.25 and above. For a 50% chance of $8 lottery, they switch preference at $3.50. For a 50% chance of $10 lottery, they switch at $4.00. For lotteries involving losses, the respondent prefers the lottery over smaller losses but switches to the certain loss as the certain loss approaches the expected loss, indicating risk aversion in losses as well. For example, for a 50% chance of losing $6, they prefer the lottery over losses up to about -$3.00 but switch preference to the certain loss at -$2.75 and better. For a 50% chance of losing $8 and $2, they prefer the lottery over losses up to about -$4.50. For a 50% chance of losing $10, they prefer the lottery over losses up to about -$3.00. In a lottery with 50% chance of losing $8 and 50% chance of winning varying amounts, they always prefer the lottery over $0, indicating risk tolerance for mixed gambles with positive upside. (Cited: Lottery choice matrices)

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the larger, later reward ($6 in 6 weeks) over smaller, sooner rewards up to $5.50 in 5 weeks, switching preference to the sooner reward at $5.50 and above, indicating moderate patience with some discounting of delayed rewards. Similar patterns hold for $8 in 7 weeks and $10 in 7 weeks delayed rewards, with switching points around $7.00 and $8.50 respectively. When choosing between immediate and delayed rewards (e.g., $6 in 1 week vs. $3 now), the respondent prefers immediate rewards when the immediate amount is sufficiently close to the delayed amount, indicating some present bias but moderate patience overall. (Cited: Intertemporal choice matrices)

## Social preferences  
In dictator and ultimatum game scenarios, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person when acting as the proposer in a $5 split game. As the receiver, they accept offers down to $1 but reject offers of $0. When playing the trust game with tripled amounts, the respondent keeps $3 and sends $2 as the sender, and as the receiver, they keep a large share of the tripled amount (e.g., keep $13 of $15 when receiving $5 sent). This indicates moderate generosity as proposer but a tendency to keep a large share when in the receiver role. (Cited: Dictator/ultimatum/trust game questions)
