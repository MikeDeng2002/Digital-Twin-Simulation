The respondent rated Forgiveness (item 3), Humility (7), Altruism (9), Loyalty (11), Politeness (12), Harmony (14), Honesty (16), Compassion (17), Civility (19), and Tradition (23) at the highest level of 9, indicating these are uniquely highly valued guiding principles. Conversely, Status (18) is rated lowest at 1, and Power (13), Recognition (22), and Superiority (24) are rated low (2), indicating these are uniquely low in importance. Wealth (1) and Influence (4) are rated moderately (6), Pleasure (2), Trust (5), Achievement (8), and Excitement (15) are rated 7 or 8, showing moderate importance but not uniquely high. This pattern suggests a strong emphasis on social harmony, moral virtues, and tradition, with low valuation of dominance, status, and competition.

## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the minimalism scale (12 items), the respondent generally agrees a little (rating 4) with statements endorsing avoiding accumulation, restricting possessions, preferring simplicity, and mindful selection of belongings (items 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 11, 12). However, some items about keeping spaces visually sparse or empty (7, 8) are disagreed with slightly (rating 2), and neutral on restricting number of things owned (2) and mindfulness (9). Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking, the respondent mostly disagrees or is neutral on statements about seeking uniqueness through products, breaking social rules in consumption, or avoiding popular products (items 1-3, 5-12). The only exception is a slight agreement (4) with having an eye for interesting and unusual products to establish a distinctive image (item 4). Overall, the respondent does not strongly seek uniqueness or consumer rebellion.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery over smaller certain amounts until the certain amount approaches or exceeds the expected value, indicating moderate risk aversion. For example, in a 50% chance to win $6 or $0 lottery, the respondent prefers the lottery over amounts up to about $2.75 but switches to the certain amount at $3.00 and above. Similar patterns hold for other lotteries with varying payoffs.  

In loss lotteries (50% chance of losing $6 or $0, etc.), the respondent prefers the lottery over smaller losses but switches to the sure loss at higher amounts, again consistent with moderate risk aversion.  

In a lottery with 50% chance of losing $8 and 50% chance of winning a positive amount x, the respondent prefers the sure $0 over lotteries with x = $7 or $8, but prefers the lottery when x reaches $9 or higher, indicating a threshold for risk acceptance.  

Overall, the respondent exhibits moderate risk aversion in gains and losses, with some tolerance for risk when expected value is favorable.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the larger, later reward ($6 in 6 weeks vs. smaller amounts sooner) until the sooner amount approaches the delayed amount, indicating patience. For example, she prefers $6 in 6 weeks over $5.75 in 5 weeks but switches preference when the sooner amount equals or exceeds $6. Similar patterns hold for other delayed reward comparisons. This suggests moderate to high intertemporal patience, favoring delayed rewards unless the sooner reward is nearly equal or better.

## Social preferences  
In dictator and ultimatum game scenarios, the respondent proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to the other person when acting as the proposer. As the receiver, she accepts offers down to $1 but rejects offers of $0. In the trust game where sent amounts are tripled, she keeps $2 and sends $3 as the sender, and as the receiver, she keeps $5 and returns $10 when receiving $5 (tripled to $15), showing a willingness to share and reciprocate fairly. These choices indicate moderately generous social preferences with fairness considerations.
