## Effort regulation and cognitive style  
This individual exhibits a moderately high Need for Cognition (NFC), as indicated by their agreement with items such as "I would prefer complex to simple problems" (4/5), "I like to have the responsibility of handling a situation that requires a lot of thinking" (4/5), and "I really enjoy a task that involves coming up with new solutions to problems" (5/5). However, there is a notable ambivalence or partial avoidance of deep thinking, as they also agree with "I try to anticipate and avoid situations where there is likely chance I will have to think in depth about something" (4/5) and "I only think as hard as I have to" (4/5). This suggests a cognitive style that values intellectual engagement but balances it with effort conservation, possibly reflecting a pragmatic or selective engagement with cognitive challenges.

Cognitive test performance shows generally good logical reasoning and puzzle-solving skills, with correct answers on most logic puzzles and verbal synonym/antonym tests. However, there are some specific error patterns: for example, the individual incorrectly answered the "If you pass the person in second place, what place are you in?" question with "1" instead of the correct "2," indicating a common cognitive slip or a momentary failure in perspective-taking or sequencing. Similarly, the answer to the lily pad doubling problem ("4" days instead of the correct "47") is a major error, suggesting difficulty with exponential growth concepts or intuitive grasp of doubling time. The individual also failed to identify any valid rotation of the cube in multiple cube rotation tasks, consistently choosing "None of the cubes could be a rotation," indicating a possible weakness in spatial visualization or mental rotation.

Big Five personality responses show moderate Conscientiousness: "Does a thorough job" (4/5), "Is a reliable worker" (4/5), "Perseveres until the task is finished" (4/5), and "Makes plans and follows through with them" (4/5). The individual rates themselves as "Efficient" (9/9), "Organized" (7/9), and "Systematic" (8/9), supporting a conscientious and orderly cognitive style. However, some ambivalence is present in "Can be careless" (3/5) and "Tends to be disorganized" (3/5), suggesting occasional lapses or variability in effort regulation.

Free responses emphasize a thoughtful, self-reflective style with a strong moral and spiritual framework, focusing on virtues such as love, patience, kindness, and self-control. This suggests a reasoning style that integrates affective and value-based considerations alongside cognitive effort.

The individual shows moderate Need for Closure (NFCC), agreeing with statements like "I don’t like situations that are uncertain" (4/5), "I dislike questions which could be answered in many different ways" (4/5), and "I don’t like to go into a situation without knowing what I can expect from it" (4/5). However, they do not express strong impatience or irritation when solutions are delayed (3/5), indicating some tolerance for ambiguity.

Behaviorally, the individual’s choices in economic games and lotteries suggest a preference for fairness and predictability rather than risk-seeking or impulsivity. Their intertemporal choices show consistent preference for delayed rewards over immediate smaller rewards, indicating patience and tolerance for waiting, which aligns with moderate closure needs balanced by some openness.

Free responses do not indicate distress or discomfort with ambiguity; rather, the person seems to seek order and clarity but can tolerate some uncertainty without excessive anxiety. The low somatic anxiety symptoms and low self-reported neuroticism support this moderate closure profile.

The individual scores low to moderate on Maximizer tendencies. They disagree with "I never settle for second best" (2/5) and "No matter what I do, I have the highest standards for myself" (3/5), and agree only somewhat with "When shopping, I have a hard time finding clothing that I really love" (4/5). They strongly agree with "When I am in the car listening to the radio, I often check other stations to see if something better is playing" (5/5), indicating some tendency to seek better options but not to an extreme maximizing level.

In economic games, the person proposes a fair split ($2 for self, $3 for other) and accepts offers down to $1 for themselves but rejects the lowest offers ($1 for self, $4 for other). This suggests a fairness-driven optimization strategy that balances self-interest with equity, rather than maximizing absolute gain.

Free responses about decision thoughts emphasize fairness, selflessness, and concern for others’ needs, indicating a satisficing approach guided by social and moral values rather than pure utility maximization or exhaustive search.

They tolerate uncertainty moderately well, seeking closure but not impulsively.
