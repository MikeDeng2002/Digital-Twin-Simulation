## Effort regulation and cognitive style
The person's Need for Cognition (NFC) profile is well measured by 18 direct items. They generally show a strong preference for effortful thinking and intellectual challenge. For example, they "like to have the responsibility of handling a situation that requires a lot of thinking" (Item 2: Agree strongly), "like tasks that require little thought once I've learned them" (Item 9: Disagree strongly), and "really enjoy a task that involves coming up with new solutions to problems" (Item 11: Agree strongly). They also "prefer a task that is intellectual, difficult, and important" (Item 15: Agree strongly) and "like the notion of thinking abstractly" (Item 14: Agree strongly). They disagree strongly with statements indicating avoidance of thinking, such as "Thinking is not my idea of fun" (Item 3) and "I try to anticipate and avoid situations where there is likely chance I will have to think in depth" (Item 5). This pattern indicates a high Need for Cognition.

Regarding cognitive test items, the person completed 42 questions involving syllogisms, Wason selection tasks, analogies, and CRT-style problems. They reported answering 25 correctly out of 42, estimating that the average US adult would answer 20 correctly. Specific CRT-style items show an error: for example, the bat and ball problem was answered as "5" (the intuitive but incorrect answer), indicating susceptibility to the classic intuitive trap. On logical syllogisms, most answers were correct, except for some errors such as the conclusion "Poodles do not have four legs" (answered "No," which is correct), but the last syllogism about bicycles needing oil and concluding bicycles have motors was answered "Yes," which is logically invalid, indicating a reasoning error.

In spatial reasoning tasks involving cube rotations, the person sometimes chose "I do not know the solution" (Item with cube rotation, answer H), indicating uncertainty or difficulty. For other cube rotation tasks, they selected plausible answers (A and E). For analogy and synonym/antonym tasks, most answers were correct, showing good verbal reasoning.

## Closure and uncertainty tolerance
The Need for Closure (NFCC) scale is directly measured with 15 items. The person shows a strong preference for closure and low tolerance for uncertainty. They "don’t like situations that are uncertain" (Item 1: Agree strongly), "dislike questions which could be answered in many different ways" (Item 2: Agree strongly), and "don’t like to be with people who are capable of unexpected actions" (Item 10: Agree strongly). They also "dislike unpredictable situations" (Item 15: Agree strongly) and "dislike it when a person’s statement could mean many different things" (Item 11: Agree strongly). They agree somewhat that "a well ordered life with regular hours suits my temperament" (Item 3: Agree a little) and "establishing a consistent routine enables me to enjoy life more" (Item 12: Agree a little). They strongly endorse that "rules and regulations are helpful and necessary" (Item 2: Definitely true) and that "it is very important to carry out the obligations placed on me" (Item 3: Definitely true). They do not show impatience for quick solutions (Item 8: Disagree strongly), suggesting a nuanced profile with a desire for order but not necessarily impulsive closure seeking.

## Optimization strategy
The Maximizer scale is measured with 6 items. The person shows a strong maximizer tendency. They "never settle for second best" (Item 6: Agree strongly) and "have the highest standards for myself" (Item 5: Agree strongly). They disagree strongly with statements indicating difficulty in finding clothing they love or shopping for gifts, suggesting decisiveness rather than maximization difficulty. They neither agree nor disagree on some items about seeking uniqueness in possessions, but strongly agree with "When it comes to the products I buy and the situations in which I use them, I have broken customs and rules" (Item 5: Agree strongly), indicating a preference for distinctiveness in choices.

## Self-concept stability
Self-concept clarity is measured with 12 items. The person shows a very clear and stable self-concept. They strongly disagree with statements such as "My beliefs about myself often conflict with one another" (Item 1), "On one day I might have one opinion of myself and on another day I might have a different opinion" (Item 2), and "I spend a lot of time wondering about what kind of person I really am" (Item 3). They strongly agree that they "seldom experience conflict between the different aspects of my personality" (Item 6) and "have a clear sense of who I am and what I am" (Item 11). This indicates high self-concept clarity and stability.

## Intertemporal patience
Intertemporal choice tasks are present with multiple items offering choices between smaller-sooner and larger-later monetary rewards. The person consistently prefers the larger, later rewards over smaller, sooner ones in most cases, for example choosing $6 in 6 weeks over $3 in 5 weeks (all items 1-7), $8 in 7 weeks over smaller amounts in 6 weeks, and $10 in 7 weeks over smaller amounts in 5 weeks. They switch preference only when the immediate reward equals or exceeds the delayed reward (e.g., $6 in 6 weeks vs. $6 in 5 weeks, choosing the sooner). This pattern indicates moderate to high intertemporal patience.

In social decision tasks involving splitting money, the person chooses to keep $0 and send $5 to the other person in the ultimatum game, but as the receiver, they reject offers that are very generous to the other (rejecting $5 to self, accepting only when the other keeps $3 or more). In the trust game, they keep $2 and send $3, and as receiver, they keep moderate amounts and send back some money, indicating a balanced approach to trust and reciprocity.

## Bias susceptibility
Direct scale evidence for bias susceptibility includes:

- CRT-style intuitive trap: The bat and ball problem was answered "5" (Item: bat and ball cost), which is the classic intuitive but incorrect answer, indicating susceptibility to the "intuitive but wrong" bias on CRT items.

- Logical reasoning errors: The syllogism "If a person eats hamburgers, then they will get cancer" with the conclusion "The person will get cancer" was answered "Yes," which is logically valid given the premises, but the bicycle motor syllogism was answered incorrectly, indicating some inconsistency in logical reasoning.

- Wason selection task: Correct cards to turn over were identified (cards A and 3), indicating understanding of confirmation bias testing.

No direct scale evidence for social desirability bias or other named biases is present.
