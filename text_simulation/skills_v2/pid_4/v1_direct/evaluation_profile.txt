## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over certain smaller amounts when the lottery expected value is higher, but switches to the certain amount when it approaches or exceeds the lottery expected value, indicating risk aversion consistent with expected utility theory. For example, they prefer a 50% chance of $6 over amounts up to about $2.75 but switch to the certain amount at $3 or more. Similarly, for lotteries with higher stakes, they show similar patterns. For losses, they prefer to avoid larger losses and accept smaller sure losses over risky losses, indicating loss aversion. In social economic games (dictator and ultimatum), they propose to keep $0 and give $5 to the other person but would reject such an offer if received, rejecting offers that are very generous to the other and accepting only offers where the other keeps $3 or more. In the trust game, they would keep $2 and send $3 to the other person, and as the receiver, they keep a moderate share and return a substantial amount, indicating moderate trust and reciprocity. Overall, the respondent exhibits moderate risk aversion and social preferences favoring fairness and generosity but with some strategic acceptance thresholds.

## Intertemporal patience

For example, they prefer $6 in 6 weeks over $5.75 in 5 weeks but switch preference when the sooner amount equals or exceeds the later amount. This pattern holds across multiple time frames and amounts, indicating moderate patience and discounting consistent with normative intertemporal preferences.
