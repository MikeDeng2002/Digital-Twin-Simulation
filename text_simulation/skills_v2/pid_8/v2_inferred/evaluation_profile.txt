The respondent neither strongly minimalist nor strongly acquisitive. ## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers larger delayed rewards over smaller sooner ones until the difference becomes quite large, indicating moderate to high patience in intertemporal choice. For example, she prefers $6 in 6 weeks over $5.50 in 5 weeks but switches preference when the immediate reward approaches the delayed reward. This pattern is consistent across multiple delayed reward tasks. Cross-referenced with moderate to high Conscientiousness (organized 8, practical 8, reliable worker 5), this suggests a reasonable capacity for planning and delayed gratification.

## Risk preferences  
Lottery tasks show a tendency to prefer the lottery over small certain amounts until the certain amount approaches or exceeds the expected value, indicating moderate risk aversion. For example, the respondent prefers the lottery over $2.00 but switches to preferring certain amounts above $2.25 for a 50% chance at $6. In loss lotteries, the respondent consistently prefers the lottery over certain losses, indicating risk aversion in losses as well. The respondent’s financial literacy is moderate, with some misconceptions (e.g., incorrect mortgage interest statement, underestimation of bond returns). Overall, risk preferences align with moderate-income risk aversion, despite high family income, possibly reflecting cautious financial behavior.

## Social preferences  
The respondent shows a clear gap between family-oriented and stranger-oriented social preferences. Family collectivism is strong: high agreement with family duty and loyalty items (family members should stick together 5, duty to family 4), and willingness to sacrifice personal wants for family. However, in economic games with strangers, the respondent proposes to keep $4 out of $5 and send only $1, and rejects offers where she would receive only $1 or $0, indicating selfish stranger-allocation behavior. When receiving money, she tends to keep a large share and returns only a small amount, reinforcing a self-interested stance toward strangers. This stranger-vs-family distinction is preserved and consistent with values and behavioral data.

She values personal success, trust, humility, and politeness, with moderate concern for others but lower loyalty to groups outside family. Her consumer behavior is characterized by a preference for unique, premium products combined with moderate minimalism, leading to selective purchasing rather than accumulation. Social preferences reveal strong family collectivism but selfishness toward strangers in economic exchanges. Risk attitudes are moderately risk-averse, consistent with cautious financial behavior. Pricing and product choices will reflect a balance between mindful spending and a desire for distinctive, quality items, with minimalism tempering bulk or impulse purchases.
