## Effort regulation and cognitive style  
The respondent shows a moderate to somewhat engaged cognitive style. On the Need for Cognition scale, they "agree a little" with preferring complex problems (Item 1), enjoying deliberating hard and long (Item 6), and liking puzzles to solve (Item 13), but also "agree a little" that they prefer tasks requiring little thought once learned (Item 9) and that it is "enough that something gets the job done" (Item 17). This mixed pattern suggests a moderate cognitive effort regulation, balancing effortful thinking with some satisficing tendencies. Cognitive test performance reveals some errors and uncertainty: for example, the answer to the oscillate synonym was "recapitulate" (incorrect), and the antonym for "verbose" was chosen as "calculating" (incorrect), indicating some lexical-semantic errors. The participant correctly solved many logical syllogisms but made errors on some (e.g., incorrectly affirming that "Poodles do not have four legs" follows logically). The participant self-reports being a reliable worker (Big Five Item 13: Agree strongly) and organized (8/9 on conscientiousness-related items), which aligns with moderate conscientiousness. Overall, the cognitive style is reflective but not deeply analytical, with some lexical and reasoning errors suggesting moderate cognitive effort and occasional lapses.

Behavioral signals such as free-response length and cognitive test timing are not explicitly provided, but the participant’s moderate cognitive effort and balanced responses imply a moderate tolerance for uncertainty with a preference for some structure and closure. The participant’s reasoning on syllogisms shows some errors but also correct logical deductions, consistent with a moderate but not rigid closure style.

## Optimization strategy

However, the participant also "agrees a little" that it is enough if something just gets the job done (Need for Cognition Item 17), indicating some satisficing tendencies in certain contexts. Self-Concept Clarity scale responses show a stable and clear self-concept. The participant "disagrees strongly" or "disagrees a little" with items indicating fluctuating or conflicting self-beliefs (e.g., Items 2, 5, 6, 9, 11: mostly disagree strongly). ## Behavioral vs. stated-preference gap  
A meaningful gap exists between the participant’s stated values and their economic game behavior. They rate altruism as "quite important" (Value Item 9: 5) and express a desire to be kind and supportive in self-descriptions. However, in the dictator game, they propose keeping $4 out of $5 and sending only $1 to the other person, a relatively selfish offer. As a receiver, they reject offers where the sender keeps $4 or $5, indicating a fairness standard that is stricter than their own giving behavior. Their thoughts during the game reveal a tension between fairness and self-interest ("I should be generous, but I also need to think about my own needs"). This gap suggests a conflict between aspirational altruism and pragmatic self-interest, which may reduce the reliability of self-reported altruistic values in predicting actual behavior. No other major gaps between self-reported personality and behavior are evident.

## Intertemporal patience  
The participant shows moderate impatience in intertemporal choice tasks, consistently preferring smaller-sooner rewards over larger-later ones until the delayed reward becomes substantially better (e.g., choosing $3 now over $6 in 1 week, but switching preference when the immediate reward is closer to the delayed reward). This pattern indicates moderate discounting of future rewards. Conscientiousness-related items (organized, efficient, practical) are high, which often correlates with greater patience, but the participant’s choices suggest a moderate level of impatience. Free responses and self-descriptions do not emphasize long-term planning strongly, consistent with this moderate patience.

## Bias susceptibility predictions  
Predictable biases from cognitive signals:  
- Probability matching vs. maximizing: The participant’s lottery choices show some preference for maximizing (choosing certain amounts over lotteries when values are close), suggesting a tendency toward maximizing rather than probability matching.  
- Denominator neglect: The participant correctly estimates probabilities and percentages in lottery and disease risk questions, indicating low susceptibility to denominator neglect.  
- Belief bias in syllogisms: The participant accepts logically invalid conclusions when they align with believable content (e.g., affirming "Poodles do not have four legs" as invalid), indicating moderate belief bias.  
- Wason matching bias: The participant correctly identifies the card to turn over in the Wason selection task (choosing "3"), suggesting low susceptibility to matching bias.

Weakly predictable or not predictable from cognitive signals:  
Framing effects, anchoring, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, outcome bias, sunk-cost effect, less-is-more effect, omission bias, Allais/ certainty effects, and myside bias cannot be confidently predicted from the available data, consistent with Stanovich & West (2008) limits.

## Summary  
The respondent exhibits a moderately engaged cognitive style with some lexical and reasoning errors, balanced by conscientiousness and reliability. They show a mild preference for closure and moderate tolerance for uncertainty, with a tendency to maximize in personal achievement but satisfice in some consumer domains. Their self-concept is stable and coherent, with no major contradictions across self-descriptions. A notable gap exists between their altruistic values and economic game behavior, indicating a tension between idealism and pragmatic self-interest. Intertemporal choices reveal moderate impatience despite conscientious traits. Cognitive biases such as belief bias may be present, but denominator neglect and matching bias appear low. Overall, the participant’s reasoning under uncertainty is characterized by moderate cognitive effort, some inconsistency between values and behavior, and a generally stable self-view.
