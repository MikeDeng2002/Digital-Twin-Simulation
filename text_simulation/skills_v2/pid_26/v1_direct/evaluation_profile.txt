## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the minimalism scale, the respondent strongly disagrees with avoiding accumulation of stuff and prefers not to have sparse or simple environments (items 1, 5-8, all rated 1). However, she strongly agrees that she is mindful and thoughtful about what she owns and that her belongings are carefully curated and mindfully selected (items 9-12, all rated 5). Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking, the respondent generally disagrees or only slightly agrees with statements indicating a desire for uniqueness or breaking social norms in consumption (mostly ratings 1-2, with one 4). She does not seek to buy special products or brands to develop uniqueness, nor does she avoid popular products. This indicates low consumerism/uniqueness orientation.

## Risk preferences  
Direct responses to lottery and risk preference tasks are present. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over certain smaller amounts until the certain amount approaches the expected value of the lottery, indicating some risk aversion but willingness to accept risk at lower certain amounts. For example, she prefers the lottery over amounts up to about $4-$5 for lotteries with expected values around $5-$6. She also prefers to avoid losses and chooses safer options when lotteries involve losing money. Overall, the data show moderate risk aversion with some tolerance for risk in gains and strong aversion to losses.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers smaller, sooner rewards over larger, later rewards in all intertemporal choice tasks presented (e.g., choosing $3 now over $6 in one week, $4 now over $8 in one week, etc.). This pattern indicates relatively low patience or high present bias, preferring immediate smaller rewards to delayed larger rewards.

## Social preferences  
In dictator and ultimatum game scenarios, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person when she is the proposer. As the receiver, she accepts all offers from $0 to $5 sent to her, indicating high acceptance of offers regardless of fairness. In the trust game where money sent is tripled, she keeps $4 and sends $1 as the sender, and as the receiver, she keeps a majority of the tripled amount (e.g., keeps $8 of $15 sent). Her thoughts emphasize fairness and sharing in a close-to-fair manner. This suggests moderate generosity and fairness concerns, but also a tendency to keep a larger share when in the receiver role.
