## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over smaller certain amounts when the certain amount is substantially lower than the expected value, but switches to the certain amount when it approaches or exceeds the expected value. For example, for a 50% chance of $6 lottery, they prefer the lottery over amounts up to $3 but switch to the certain amount at $3.25 and above. Similar patterns hold for other lotteries, indicating moderate risk aversion but willingness to accept some risk.  

In loss lotteries, the respondent prefers the lottery over smaller certain losses but switches to the certain loss when it becomes less negative, indicating risk aversion in losses as well.  

In the ultimatum game as proposer, they choose to keep $4 and send $1 to the other person (keeping 80%). As responder, they accept all offers except the offer of $0 to self. This suggests moderate selfishness as proposer but acceptance of low offers as responder.  

No direct lottery or risk preference scale scores are provided beyond these choices.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the delayed larger reward ($6, $8, $10 in 6-7 weeks) over smaller sooner rewards when the sooner reward is substantially less, but switches to the sooner reward when it approaches the delayed reward in amount and timing. For example, for $6 in 6 weeks vs. $5.75 in 5 weeks, they prefer the delayed reward, but switch to the sooner reward at $6 in 5 weeks. Similar patterns hold for other amounts. This indicates moderate patience with some discounting of delayed rewards.  

In choices between money in 1-2 weeks vs. now, the respondent shows similar patterns, preferring delayed rewards when the immediate amount is substantially less, but switching to immediate rewards as amounts approach equality.  

Overall, the respondent shows moderate intertemporal patience with a discounting pattern consistent with typical behavior.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator game, the respondent proposes to keep $4 and send $1 to the other person, indicating a preference for keeping the majority of resources but giving a small positive amount. As responder in the ultimatum game, they accept all offers except the offer of $0 to self, indicating willingness to accept low offers but rejecting zero offers.  

In the trust game where money sent is tripled, the respondent chooses to keep $4 and send $1 as sender. As receiver, they keep a majority of the money sent back (e.g., keep $11 of $15 when sent $5, keep $6 of $12 when sent $4, keep $6 of $9 when sent $3), indicating a relatively selfish or self-interested allocation preference.
