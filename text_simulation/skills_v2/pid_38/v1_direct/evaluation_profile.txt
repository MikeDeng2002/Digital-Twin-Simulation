## Budget constraint  
The respondent reports a total family income of $100,000 or more before taxes (item 24). She is currently employed full-time (item 33) and lives in a household of four people (item 25). These direct income and employment items define a moderate to high budget envelope for spending decisions.

## Values hierarchy  
The 24-item values scale shows a compressed rating pattern with many values rated 6 to 8, indicating rating compression and potential acquiescence bias. Despite this, relative structure can be extracted. The highest-rated values (all rated 8) include Wealth (1), Forgiveness (3), Influence (4), Trust (5), Competence (6), Altruism (9), Ambition (10), Loyalty (11), Honesty (16), Compassion (17), Civility (19), and Autonomy (20). The lowest-rated value explicitly rated is Power (13) at 4, and Status (18) is rated 5, which are notably lower than the cluster of highest values. Tradition (23) and Humility (7) are rated 6, somewhat lower than the highest cluster. No rating was provided for Superiority (24). This pattern suggests the respondent values social and personal virtues such as trust, honesty, altruism, and autonomy highly, while valuing power and status less.

## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
The respondent consistently agrees a little (all items rated 4) with all 12 minimalism scale items (items 1-12 in the minimalism section), indicating a mild preference for minimalism, including avoiding excess possessions, preferring simplicity, and mindful ownership. Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking, the respondent mostly disagrees a little (rating 2) or neither agrees nor disagrees (rating 3) with statements about seeking uniqueness or breaking social consumption norms. However, she agrees a little (rating 4) with items expressing avoidance of products bought by the general population and a dislike for commonplace products or brands (items 10-12 in the consumerism section). This suggests a mild minimalist orientation combined with a modest tendency to avoid mainstream consumer products, but not a strong uniqueness-seeking or consumerist orientation.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over certain smaller amounts when the lottery is a gain (e.g., 50% chance to win $6 vs. certain amounts up to $5, always choosing the lottery). She also prefers the lottery over certain losses, consistently choosing the lottery with a 50% chance of losing amounts over certain smaller losses. This pattern indicates risk-seeking behavior in both gains and losses. In dictator and ultimatum games, she chooses to keep the entire amount for herself ($5 for self, $0 to other) when acting as proposer, and rejects offers where she would receive less than half, accepting only offers where she receives at least $3 out of $5. When acting as the receiver in the trust game, she keeps all the money and sends back nothing, indicating low trust or generosity in economic games. Overall, the respondent exhibits risk-seeking preferences in lotteries and a self-interested, non-cooperative stance in social economic games.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently chooses the smaller, sooner reward over larger, later rewards in intertemporal choice tasks involving monetary amounts delayed by weeks. For example, she prefers $3-$6 now over $6 in 6 weeks, and $4-$8 now over $8 in 7 weeks, consistently choosing immediate smaller rewards rather than waiting for larger delayed rewards. This pattern indicates relatively low intertemporal patience or higher present bias.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator game, the respondent chooses to keep all $5 and send $0 to the other person. As the receiver in the trust game, she keeps all the money sent and returns nothing. In the ultimatum game, she rejects offers where she would receive less than $3, accepting only offers where she receives $3 or more. These direct allocation choices indicate a self-interested social preference profile with low generosity and low willingness to share resources, combined with a threshold for fairness acceptance in ultimatum bargaining.

## Summary  
The respondent has a moderate to high budget envelope with full-time employment and a household of four. Her values show rating compression but relatively higher importance placed on social virtues such as trust, honesty, altruism, and autonomy, and lower importance on power and status. She exhibits a mild minimalist orientation with some avoidance of mainstream consumer products but does not strongly seek uniqueness. Risk preferences reveal risk-seeking behavior in lotteries and self-interested, non-cooperative behavior in economic games. Intertemporal choices indicate a preference for immediate rewards over delayed larger rewards, reflecting low patience. Social preferences are characterized by low generosity and a self-focused approach in resource allocation tasks.
