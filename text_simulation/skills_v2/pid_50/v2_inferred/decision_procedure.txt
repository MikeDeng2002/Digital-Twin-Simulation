## Optimization strategy  
The respondent identifies strongly as a maximizer, with Maximizer scale items such as "No matter what I do, I have the highest standards for myself" (Maximizer #5, 5 - Agree strongly) and "I never settle for second best" (Maximizer #6, 5 - Agree strongly). However, domain-specific behavior reveals some satisficing tendencies. For example, in the dictator game, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person, a moderately selfish split rather than a fully maximizing or fully altruistic offer. In the trust game, they keep all the money and send nothing, indicating a satisficing or self-protective approach in social-economic domains. The respondent also reports difficulty shopping for gifts (Maximizer #3, 5 - Agree strongly) and having a hard time finding clothing they love (Maximizer #4, 4 - Agree a little), which aligns with maximizing tendencies in consumer choice. Yet, they strongly disagree with statements about being on the lookout for better opportunities or switching channels when satisfied (Maximizer #1 and #2, both 1 - Disagree strongly), suggesting some domain-specific satisficing or contentment in other areas. This split indicates a maximizer orientation in personal standards and consumer choice but satisficing in social-economic interactions.

This suggests some self-concept instability related to social roles and identity, but not a fragmented or incoherent self.

## Bias susceptibility predictions  
Predictable from cognitive signals:  
- Probability matching vs. maximizing: The respondent’s high Need for Cognition and good cognitive test performance suggest a tendency toward maximizing rather than probability matching. Their lottery valuations consistently prefer the certain monetary equivalent over the lottery, indicating maximizing behavior.  
- Dominator (denominator) neglect: The respondent correctly interprets probabilities and percentages in multiple tasks (e.g., lottery winning chances, disease probabilities), suggesting low susceptibility to denominator neglect.  
- Belief bias in syllogisms: The respondent correctly answers syllogistic reasoning questions, including those that conflict with real-world knowledge (e.g., "All unemployed people are poor" premises), indicating low belief bias.  
- Wason matching bias: The respondent correctly identifies the card(s) to turn over to test conditional rules (e.g., selecting card A), indicating low Wason matching bias.

Weakly predictable or not predictable from cognitive signals:  
For biases such as framing effects, anchoring, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, outcome bias, sunk-cost effect, less-is-more effect, omission bias, Allais/ certainty effects, and myside bias, the data do not provide direct evidence. Per Stanovich & West (2008), these biases are less predictable from cognitive ability or style alone. Therefore, no confident predictions are made regarding these biases.
