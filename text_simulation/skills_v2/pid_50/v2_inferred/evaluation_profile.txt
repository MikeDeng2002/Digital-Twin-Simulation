## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers larger delayed rewards over smaller sooner ones across multiple delay discounting tasks (choosing $6 in 6 weeks over $3 in 5 weeks, $8 in 7 weeks over $4 in 6 weeks, $10 in 7 weeks over $5 in 5 weeks, etc., until the delayed and immediate amounts are equal or better). This indicates a high degree of patience and willingness to wait for larger rewards. This is supported by high Conscientiousness (Big Five items 3, 13, 28, 33, 38 all strongly agree) and strong planning and perseverance (items 38 and 28). The respondent’s cognitive test performance is above average (estimated 38/42 correct vs. 35 average), further supporting good planning capacity. Overall, intertemporal patience is high, consistent with conscientious and deliberate decision-making.

## Risk preferences  
In lottery tasks, the respondent consistently prefers the lottery over low certain amounts up to near the expected value, indicating moderate risk tolerance for gains. For example, she prefers lotteries with 50% chance of $6 over certain amounts up to $5.75, and similar patterns for $8/$2 and $10/$0 lotteries. For losses, she consistently prefers certain smaller losses over risky losses, indicating risk aversion in the loss domain. She rejects lotteries with 50% chance of losing $6 or $8 in favor of smaller certain losses. This gain/loss asymmetry is typical and consistent with moderate-income risk aversion. Her financial literacy is good, understanding mortgage, inflation, and investment risk concepts. Overall, she is moderately risk tolerant for gains but risk averse for losses, consistent with normative moderate-income behavior.

## Social preferences  
The respondent’s values show low ALTRUISM (2) and EQUALITY (2) but high LOYALTY (8) and POLITENESS (8), indicating a strong family- or in-group orientation rather than broad altruism. Behavioral game responses confirm this: in dictator/ultimatum games, she proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person, and accepts all offers including very selfish ones ($5 for other, $0 for self). When playing as the receiver in a trust game, she keeps a majority of the money sent (e.g., keeps $10 of $15, sends back $5), indicating a selfish stranger allocation. Her free responses confirm valuing solitude over family proximity and a sense of duty to family but prioritizing personal space. She agrees strongly that family members should stick together (5) and that she ought to respond to family demands (4), but also disagrees with respecting group decisions (1). This shows a gap: generous and loyal to family but selfish and cautious with strangers. This stranger-vs-family distinction is preserved.

## Product-category predictions for the pricing study  
Grocery staples: Likely to purchase at elevated prices moderately, given moderate income and mindful but non-minimalist consumption. Values of competence and harmony suggest practical choices but willingness to pay for quality.

Premium branded items: More likely to purchase at elevated prices, given strong preference for uniqueness, originality, and avoidance of mainstream brands. The respondent’s consumer orientation favors distinctive, non-mainstream products.

Bulk/party-size items: Less likely to purchase in bulk or party sizes, as the respondent values careful curation and mindfulness about possessions, and lives in a small household (2 persons). Bulk purchases may conflict with her preference for selectivity.

Indulgence/novelty items: Moderately likely to purchase indulgence items, as pleasure is rated fairly high (8), and she enjoys originality and novelty. However, minimalism is rejected, so indulgence is not avoided on principle.

Household necessities: Likely to purchase at elevated prices if quality and effectiveness are assured, consistent with high conscientiousness and competence values. She prefers practical and efficient products.

Overall, the respondent is willing to pay premiums for quality, uniqueness, and products that support her values and lifestyle, but is selective and mindful rather than impulsive or minimalist.

## Summary  
This respondent is a retired, moderately affluent older female with a strong preference for competence, honesty, harmony, and autonomy, combined with high conscientiousness and planning ability. She is not a minimalist but is highly mindful and selective about possessions, favoring unique and non-mainstream products. Socially, she is loyal and generous to family but selfish with strangers, reflecting a clear stranger-vs-family distinction. She exhibits high intertemporal patience and moderate risk tolerance for gains but risk aversion for losses. Her spending behavior is cautious but not overly restrictive, with a willingness to pay premiums for products that align with her values and support her practical, thoughtful lifestyle.
