## Effort regulation and cognitive style  
This individual exhibits a low Need for Cognition (NFC), as evidenced by strong disagreement with statements favoring complex or challenging thinking tasks (e.g., "I would prefer complex to simple problems" = strongly disagree; "I find satisfaction in deliberating hard and for long hours" = strongly disagree). They prefer routine, simple tasks ("I prefer my life to be filled with puzzles that I must solve" = strongly disagree; "I like tasks that require little thought once I've learned them" = strongly agree). Despite this, their Big Five Conscientiousness is very high (e.g., "Does a thorough job" = strongly agree; "Is a reliable worker" = strongly agree; "Makes plans and follows through" = strongly agree), indicating disciplined and systematic effort regulation. Cognitive test performance reveals multiple fundamental reasoning errors: for example, the classic bat-and-ball problem was answered incorrectly with "10" instead of the correct $0.05, and the die-roll estimate was 100 instead of the expected 500, indicating severe underestimation of probabilities and poor intuitive numeracy. Logical reasoning questions were mostly answered incorrectly, showing a tendency to reject valid logical conclusions (e.g., denying that "All animals love water; plants do not; therefore plants are not animals" follows logically). The synonym and antonym tests show frequent selection of incorrect or unrelated options, suggesting limited vocabulary or semantic reasoning deficits. Overall, the person’s cognitive style is low in analytical depth, favoring routine and simplicity, with a tendency toward fundamental reasoning errors and poor probabilistic intuition, despite high conscientiousness and effort in task completion. Free responses emphasize a focus on doing the job well and being caring, but no indication of enjoying intellectual challenge.

## Closure and uncertainty tolerance  
The individual scores very low on Need for Closure (NFCC) items that indicate discomfort with ambiguity ("I don’t like situations that are uncertain" = strongly disagree; "I dislike questions which could be answered in many different ways" = strongly disagree). However, they strongly endorse a preference for order and routine ("I find that a well ordered life with regular hours suits my temperament" = strongly agree; "I enjoy having a clear and structured mode of life" = strongly agree). They also report impatience with unresolved problems ("When I am confronted with a problem, I’m dying to reach a solution very quickly" = strongly agree; "I would quickly become impatient and irritated if I would not find a solution to a problem immediately" = strongly agree). This combination suggests a behavioral pattern of low tolerance for prolonged uncertainty but not necessarily discomfort with ambiguity per se; rather, they seek quick resolution and prefer predictable, structured environments. Free responses about the person they ought to be and actually are emphasize responsibility, caring, and hard work, consistent with a preference for clear roles and expectations. There is no evidence of anxiety or somatic symptoms related to uncertainty, supporting a stable emotional response to ambiguity.

## Optimization strategy  
The Maximizer scale responses indicate a low maximizing tendency: the person strongly disagrees with statements about never settling for second best and having the highest standards for themselves only in some respects ("No matter what I do, I have the highest standards for myself" = strongly agree, but "I never settle for second best" = strongly disagree). Consumerism and uniqueness scales show strong disagreement with all items related to seeking uniqueness or breaking social norms in consumption, indicating a satisficing or conventional approach to choices rather than maximizing or seeking distinctiveness. Economic game behavior reveals a highly self-interested and non-cooperative strategy: in the dictator game, they keep all the money for themselves, and as the receiver, they accept all offers, even the least favorable. In the trust game, they send minimal amounts and return minimal amounts, indicating low trust and low reciprocity. Free responses about decision-making in these games emphasize self-focus and not sharing with strangers. This pattern suggests a satisficing optimization strategy focused on self-interest and minimal effort in re-evaluation or seeking better options, consistent with low NFC and low maximizing.

## Self-concept stability  
Self-Concept Clarity (SCC) is very high, with strong disagreement to all items indicating instability or conflict in self-beliefs ("My beliefs about myself often conflict" = strongly disagree; "I spend a lot of time wondering about what kind of person I really am" = strongly disagree). The person reports a clear, stable, and consistent self-image ("In general, I have a clear sense of who I am and what I am" = strongly agree). Free responses about aspirational, ought, and actual selves are closely aligned, all emphasizing being caring, honest, hardworking, and family-oriented, with no notable contradictions or gaps. Big Five items show no internal contradictions either. This profile indicates a stable and coherent self-concept with little internal conflict or vacillation.

## Behavioral vs. stated-preference gap  
There is a notable gap between stated values and revealed behavior in some domains. The person rates values such as humility, altruism, forgiveness, and compassion as highly important, and self-describes as caring and loving. However, in economic games, they behave selfishly, keeping all money in the dictator game and returning minimal amounts in trust games, indicating a discrepancy between prosocial self-presentation and actual behavior toward strangers. Social desirability is not explicitly reported but can be inferred as moderate to low given the frankness in selfish economic choices and low social desirability bias in self-reports. The person’s free responses about decision-making in economic games explicitly mention self-focus and not sharing, reinforcing the revealed preference over stated values. This gap signals a self-presentation bias or aspirational prosocial values not fully enacted in anonymous economic interactions.

## Intertemporal patience  
The individual consistently prefers larger delayed rewards over smaller sooner ones, only switching preference when the delayed reward equals or exceeds the immediate reward by a substantial margin (e.g., prefers $6 in 6 weeks over $5.75 in 5 weeks, but switches at $6 in 5 weeks). This pattern indicates moderate to high patience and self-control in intertemporal choice. High Conscientiousness (e.g., "Makes plans and follows through" = strongly agree) and free responses emphasizing hard work and responsibility support this. There is no evidence of procrastination or impulsivity in free responses or behavioral tasks. Overall, the person exhibits a patient, future-oriented temporal discounting style.

## Egocentric projection tendency  
Low self-concept clarity and low empathy predict higher egocentric projection, but this person scores very high on empathy (e.g., "I often become sad when watching sad things on TV" = strongly agree; "I can usually tell when others are angry" = strongly agree) and very high on SCC, indicating low projection tendency. Political identity is moderate (Republican but politically moderate), which may moderate projection effects. Therefore, the person is likely to have a relatively low false consensus effect and less egocentric bias in judgment, given their clear self-concept and high empathy.

## Bias susceptibility predictions  
**High confidence predictions:**  
- Probability matching vs. maximizing: Likely to probability match or show inconsistent maximizing, given low NFC and fundamental reasoning errors.  
- Denominator neglect: Likely present, as shown by poor probabilistic reasoning (e.g., die roll estimate of 100 instead of 500).  
- Belief bias: Likely, given poor logical reasoning and tendency to reject valid conclusions.  
- Wason matching bias: Likely, given poor logical reasoning and misunderstanding of conditional logic.

**Moderate confidence predictions:**  
- False consensus effect: Low, due to high empathy and high SCC.  
- Risk-benefit nonseparability (affect heuristic): Moderate, as emotional empathy is high but cognitive reasoning is low.

**Low confidence predictions (acknowledging limits):**  
- Framing effects, anchoring, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, outcome bias, sunk-cost effect, less-is-more effect, omission bias, Allais/ certainty effects, myside bias: These cannot be reliably predicted from the data.

## Self-report reliability weighting  
Self-reports are moderately reliable. The social desirability bias appears low to moderate, as the person admits to selfish behavior in economic games and shows frankness in cognitive test errors. Somatic symptoms are minimal, and self-reported neuroticism is low, indicating good calibration between somatic and self-report data. However, the large gap between prosocial values and selfish economic behavior suggests some aspirational bias in value self-reports. Overall, behavioral data should be weighted more heavily than self-reports for predicting actual decision-making under uncertainty.

## Summary  
This individual exhibits a low need for cognition and a preference for routine, simple tasks, combined with high conscientiousness and effort regulation. They show fundamental reasoning errors in logic and probability, indicating poor analytical depth despite disciplined task completion. They tolerate ambiguity well but seek quick closure and prefer structured environments. Their optimization strategy is satisficing and self-interested, with a notable gap between prosocial stated values and selfish revealed behavior in economic games. Self-concept is stable and coherent, with high empathy and low egocentric projection. Intertemporal patience is moderate to high, consistent with conscientiousness. The most predictive feature is the stark discrepancy between prosocial self-description and selfish economic game behavior, signaling a complex interplay of aspirational values and pragmatic self-interest in judgment under uncertainty.
