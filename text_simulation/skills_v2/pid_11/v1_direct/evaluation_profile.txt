This indicates a low consumerism orientation and no active pursuit of uniqueness through consumption.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent shows a pattern of preferring the lottery over certain smaller amounts until the certain amount approaches or exceeds the expected value, indicating moderate risk tolerance for gains. For example, she prefers the lottery over certain amounts up to about $1.75 but switches preference at $2.00 or higher in a 50% chance of $6 lottery. For losses, she consistently prefers the sure loss over lotteries with higher expected losses, indicating risk aversion for losses. In social economic games (dictator and ultimatum), she proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to the other person and would accept offers down to $1 for herself, rejecting only the offer where she receives nothing. In the trust game with tripled amounts, she keeps $3 and sends $2, and as the receiver, she returns a fair split (e.g., keeps $7 of $15 and sends back $8). These choices indicate moderate altruism and fairness concerns in social risk contexts.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the smaller, sooner reward over the larger, later reward in all intertemporal choice tasks involving delays of 1 to 7 weeks and amounts ranging from $6 to $10. She switches preference only when the delayed reward is equal to or greater than the immediate reward (e.g., $6 in 6 weeks vs. $6 now). This pattern indicates relatively low patience or a high discount rate for delayed monetary rewards.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator game, the respondent proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to the other person. As the receiver in the ultimatum game, she accepts all offers except the one where she receives nothing. In the trust game, she sends $2 (which becomes $6) and, as the receiver, returns a roughly equal split ($7 kept, $8 sent back). These allocations reflect a preference for fairness and reciprocity, with moderate generosity and rejection of unfair offers.

## Summary  
The respondent has a moderate family income and is employed full-time in a two-person household. Her values emphasize trust, humility, loyalty, honesty, and compassion, while rejecting wealth, power, and status. She endorses minimalism and mindful ownership but does not seek uniqueness or consumerist distinction through products. She exhibits moderate risk tolerance for gains but risk aversion for losses, and shows fairness and reciprocity in social economic games. Her intertemporal choices reveal a preference for immediate rewards over delayed ones, indicating relatively low patience for delayed monetary gains.
