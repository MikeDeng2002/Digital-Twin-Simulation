## Effort regulation and cognitive style

This individual exhibits a complex and somewhat ambivalent cognitive style with respect to effort regulation. Their Need for Cognition (NFC) responses reveal a low intrinsic enjoyment of deep thinking and intellectual challenge: they strongly disagree with enjoying tasks that require new solutions or long deliberation, and they express relief rather than satisfaction after mentally effortful tasks. Yet, paradoxically, they also strongly agree with liking abstract thinking and deliberating about issues even when not personally affected, indicating some intellectual curiosity or reflective tendency. This suggests a cognitive style that is not motivated by intellectual challenge for its own sake but may engage in reflection when it aligns with personal relevance or values.

Their Big Five Conscientiousness profile is very high, with strong agreement on items like "does a thorough job," "is a reliable worker," "perseveres until the task is finished," and "makes plans and follows through." They strongly reject being careless or disorganized. This indicates a disciplined, methodical approach to tasks, likely favoring routine and efficiency over novelty or complexity. The preference for routine work and efficiency (both strongly endorsed) supports a practical, structured cognitive style rather than exploratory or creative.

Cognitive test performance shows mixed results. The participant correctly answered several logical reasoning and syllogistic tasks but made notable errors on some basic numeracy and probability questions, such as underestimating expected values (e.g., estimating 1% of 1000 as 1 instead of 10, or 20/100 as 5%). They also gave incorrect answers to classic cognitive reflection test items (e.g., the bat and ball problem answered as 10 cents instead of 5 cents). Their self-estimated correct answers on cognitive tests (5 out of 42) are drastically lower than their estimate of the average person (30 out of 42), indicating low metacognitive confidence or awareness of their own cognitive performance.

They struggled with spatial rotation tasks, often choosing "I do not know" rather than attempting a solution, suggesting either low spatial reasoning confidence or a cautious approach to uncertain problems.

Overall, the reasoning style is conscientious and practical but with limited confidence or skill in abstract or numerical reasoning. The participant prefers to avoid deep cognitive effort unless necessary and tends to rely on routine and structure. Their free responses emphasize fairness, humility, and moral approval (e.g., "I need to do what God would approve of"), indicating that moral and social values guide their reasoning more than pure analytical curiosity.

## Closure and uncertainty tolerance

The participant shows moderate to low tolerance for ambiguity and uncertainty. They neither strongly dislike nor strongly embrace uncertain situations but express discomfort with ambiguous statements and dislike questions with multiple possible answers. They strongly agree that they feel uncomfortable when they do not understand why events occur, and they dislike statements that could mean many different things. However, they do not report impatience or irritation when solutions are not immediate, nor do they dislike unpredictability strongly.

Behaviorally, their economic choices in intertemporal tasks show a strong preference for immediate or near-term rewards over delayed ones, consistently choosing smaller-sooner over larger-later amounts until the delayed reward is equal or better. This pattern suggests a low tolerance for waiting and a desire for certainty and immediacy.

Their free responses and self-descriptions emphasize responsibility, reliability, and loyalty, which may reflect a preference for stable, predictable social environments. They also disagree with being easily irritated or tense, indicating emotional stability despite some discomfort with uncertainty.

In sum, the participant prefers clarity and predictability, is somewhat averse to ambiguous or multi-interpretive information, and seeks closure but without extreme urgency or impatience. They are willing to tolerate some ambiguity but prefer structured, well-defined situations.

## Optimization strategy

The participant scores low on Maximizer tendencies. They disagree with never settling for second best and report only mild difficulty in shopping or gift selection. They strongly endorse being on the lookout for better opportunities (e.g., "No matter how satisfied I am with my job, it’s only right for me to be on the lookout for better opportunities") and frequently check alternatives (e.g., changing radio stations even when satisfied). This suggests a moderate openness to options but not a compulsive maximizer profile.

Their consumerism and uniqueness scales show neutrality or disagreement with seeking uniqueness or breaking social norms in consumption. They do not actively seek to differentiate themselves through products or brands and reject violating social rules about consumption. This indicates a satisficing approach in consumer domains, favoring conventional and socially accepted choices rather than exhaustive search or optimization.

In economic games, they propose fair splits (e.g., $2/$3 split in dictator game) and accept all offers except the most selfish, indicating a balanced approach between self-interest and fairness rather than maximizing personal gain at all costs.

Their free responses emphasize fairness, humility, and moral approval as guiding principles in decision-making, suggesting that optimization is bounded by ethical considerations rather than pure utility maximization.

They strongly reject the idea that they do not know who they are or that their personality descriptions vary day to day. They agree somewhat that they have a clear sense of who they are.

This gap between ideal and actual self is explicit in their free responses, indicating some internal tension or dissatisfaction with their current interpersonal openness.

Their Big Five responses show some contradictions: they rate themselves as emotionally stable and relaxed but also somewhat moody and aloof. They are moderately reserved and shy but neither talkative nor quiet strongly. This suggests a nuanced self-view with some ambivalence about social engagement.

Overall, the self-concept is coherent and stable but includes an aspirational desire for greater sociability and trustfulness, indicating moderate self-awareness and reflective capacity.

Yet, their self-description admits to being "not at all trusting of others and always on guard," indicating a discrepancy between idealized social values and actual interpersonal behavior.

Their social desirability score is not explicitly provided, but their willingness to admit to negative traits (e.g., taking advantage of others, feeling resentful, sometimes trying to get even) suggests moderate self-report honesty.

This gap suggests that while the participant aspires to high moral standards and fairness, their actual interpersonal trust and emotional openness are more guarded and pragmatic, likely shaped by cautious social experience.

## Intertemporal patience

The participant demonstrates low intertemporal patience, consistently preferring smaller-sooner monetary rewards over larger-later ones until the delayed reward is equal or better. This pattern is stable across multiple delay intervals and amounts, indicating a strong present bias.

Their high Conscientiousness and self-reported responsibility contrast with this impatience, suggesting that their planning and follow-through are more oriented toward task completion and reliability than long-term financial or reward optimization.

Free responses do not indicate procrastination; rather, they emphasize responsibility and duty, especially toward family. However, their economic choices reveal a preference for immediacy, possibly reflecting a pragmatic or risk-averse approach to future uncertainty rather than pure impulsivity.

## Egocentric projection tendency

The participant’s self-concept clarity is moderate to high, with low endorsement of self-belief conflict or instability, suggesting a lower tendency toward egocentric projection.

Empathy scores are mixed: they strongly agree with understanding others’ emotions and being considerate but also report some emotional distancing (e.g., "I don’t become sad when I see others crying," "Other people’s feelings don’t bother me at all"). This ambivalence suggests moderate empathy with some emotional regulation or detachment.

Their political identity is strong and conservative, which may increase projection on policy-related judgments.

Overall, the participant likely exhibits moderate false consensus effects, projecting their stable but somewhat guarded social attitudes onto others, especially in political or moral domains.

## Bias susceptibility predictions

**High confidence predictions:**

- **Probability matching vs. maximizing:** Likely to engage in probability matching or suboptimal strategies, given low numeracy and cognitive reflection test performance, and underestimation of probabilities and expected values.

- **Denominator neglect:** Likely present, as evidenced by incorrect percentage and ratio estimations (e.g., 20/100 as 5%, 1% of 1000 as 1).

- **Belief bias:** Likely to show belief bias in logical reasoning, as some syllogistic errors reflect acceptance of believable but invalid conclusions (e.g., concluding bicycles have motors because they need oil).

- **Wason matching bias:** Likely present, given selection of cards A and 3 but no mention of turning over disconfirming cards, and general low cognitive reflection.

**Moderate confidence predictions:**

- **False consensus effect:** Moderate, due to stable self-concept and strong political identity.

- **Risk-benefit nonseparability (affect heuristic):** Moderate, as emotional and moral considerations influence decisions (e.g., fairness guided by "what God would approve of").

**Low confidence predictions (acknowledging limits):**

- Framing effects, anchoring, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, outcome bias, sunk-cost effect, less-is-more effect, omission bias, Allais/ certainty effects, myside bias — these are not reliably predictable from the data.

## Self-report reliability weighting

The participant’s social desirability appears moderate; they admit to some negative traits and inconsistencies but also present themselves as conscientious and fair. The somatic symptom report shows mild physiological anxiety signs but low self-reported neuroticism, indicating some underreporting or low emotional insight.

The large gap between self-reported cognitive ability (very low) and estimated average (much higher) suggests low metacognitive accuracy.

Given these factors, self-reports on personality and values should be weighted cautiously, with behavioral and cognitive test data given more weight for reasoning simulation. The participant’s free responses and economic game behavior provide valuable calibration points.

## Summary

This participant exhibits a conscientious, practical cognitive style with low intrinsic motivation for deep thinking but moderate reflective tendencies. They prefer routine and structure, show moderate intolerance for ambiguity, and demonstrate low intertemporal patience with a strong present bias. Their reasoning under uncertainty is marked by specific cognitive errors including denominator neglect, belief bias, and low numeracy, coupled with cautious, fairness-driven social preferences that contrast with a guarded interpersonal trust stance. The most predictive feature is the pronounced gap between their aspirational social values (trust, fairness, humility) and their revealed guardedness and pragmatic fairness in behavior, indicating a reasoning profile shaped by moral considerations but constrained by skepticism and limited cognitive confidence.
