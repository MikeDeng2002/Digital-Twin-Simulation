## Intertemporal patience
The person’s intertemporal choice responses show a preference for larger, later rewards over smaller, sooner ones in most cases, indicating patience. For example, they consistently chose to wait for $6 in 6 weeks rather than smaller amounts sooner (items 1-7 in the first matrix), and similarly for $8 in 7 weeks over smaller amounts sooner, switching preference only when the sooner amount approached the delayed amount (item 5 and onward). In the second set, they preferred $10 in 7 weeks over smaller amounts sooner until the sooner amount neared $9, at which point they switched preference. In the third set, for immediate versus one-week delayed rewards, they preferred immediate smaller amounts until the immediate amount reached about $5, then switched to waiting. This pattern reflects typical discounting behavior with moderate patience.

In the dictator game scenarios, the person chose to keep all $5 for themselves when deciding how to split money (self as proposer), but when in the receiver role, they accepted all offers except the one where the proposer kept all $5 and sent $0. In the trust game, they kept $3 and sent $2 as proposer, and as receiver, they kept a majority of the tripled amount but returned a substantial portion, indicating moderate trust and reciprocity.
