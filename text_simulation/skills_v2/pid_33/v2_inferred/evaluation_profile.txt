This income level combined with single-person household suggests a moderate but limited discretionary budget for everyday purchases. ## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
Minimalism scale responses are mixed but lean toward low minimalism: she disagrees a little with avoiding accumulation and restricting possessions (items 1,2,4,5,7,8 mostly 2), but agrees a little with "less is more" and preferring simplicity in design (items 3,6,9,10,12 rated 4). She rarely seeks novelty or distinctive brands and does not challenge prevailing tastes. This, combined with moderate income and cautious spending, predicts mainstream or budget-brand preferences rather than premium or luxury brands.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers larger delayed rewards over smaller sooner ones until the delayed reward is very close in time and value (e.g., prefers $6 in 6 weeks over $5.75 in 5 weeks, but switches preference when the sooner reward is $6 in 5 weeks). This pattern indicates moderate patience with some discounting of future rewards. High Conscientiousness (Big Five items 3,13,38 rated 5) and strong planning orientation (values "makes plans and follows through" 5) support a capacity for future-oriented decision-making. Overall, she shows moderate intertemporal patience, willing to wait for better rewards but with some sensitivity to delay length.

## Risk preferences  
Lottery choices show moderate risk aversion for gains and strong aversion to losses. She prefers certain amounts over lotteries with equal or slightly higher expected values, especially for smaller amounts (e.g., prefers $2 certain over 50% chance of $6 lottery). For losses, she prefers to avoid lotteries with potential losses, choosing safer options even if the expected loss is higher. This is consistent with moderate income and typical risk aversion patterns. No strong gain/loss asymmetry beyond standard loss aversion is evident.

## Social preferences  
The respondent’s social preferences show a clear family-oriented and in-group generosity combined with selfishness toward strangers. In dictator game scenarios, she chooses to keep all money for herself when acting as proposer (6 - $5 for self, $0 for other), indicating selfish stranger allocation. However, as a receiver, she accepts all offers except the zero offer, showing some fairness expectations. Her empathy and compassion ratings are very high (Big Five helpful/unselfish 5, compassion value 9), and she reports strong emotional contagion with friends’ feelings (items 2,5,9,10,11 all 4-5). Family loyalty values are moderately high (loyalty 7, tradition 8), and she agrees somewhat with family duty items (e.g., "It is my duty to take care of my family" 4). This suggests a social preference profile of strong family collectivism but selfishness or caution in anonymous stranger interactions.

- Indulgence / novelty items: Less likely to purchase at elevated prices, given low pleasure value on spending and cautious spending habits.
- Household necessities: More likely to purchase at elevated prices, as these are essential and practical items.

## Summary  
This respondent is a moderately patient, conscientious, and somewhat anxious individual with strong values of trust, compassion, and personal autonomy. She exhibits mild minimalism and low consumerism, favoring simplicity but not strongly avoiding possessions. Her spending is cautious, with difficulty spending money despite moderate income, and she prefers mainstream or budget brands over premium or unique products. Socially, she is family-oriented and empathetic but behaves selfishly toward strangers in economic games. Risk preferences show moderate risk aversion, especially for losses. Overall, she is likely to reveal preferences consistent with budget-conscious, practical purchasing, avoiding indulgence and premium brands, and showing moderate discounting of future rewards.
