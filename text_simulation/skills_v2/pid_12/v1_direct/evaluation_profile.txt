## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over smaller certain amounts until the certain amount approaches the expected value, indicating moderate risk tolerance. For example, they prefer a 50% chance at $6 over certain amounts up to about $2.75, switching preference at $3 (lottery vs. certain money). Similar patterns hold for other lotteries with varying payoffs.  

In loss lotteries, the respondent consistently prefers the lottery over smaller certain losses, indicating risk aversion in losses. They do not accept exchanges that reduce expected losses substantially, showing loss aversion.  

In social games (dictator and ultimatum), the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 out of $5, and as a receiver, accepts all offers except the one where the sender keeps all $5. In the trust game with tripling, they send all $5 and expect the other to return a fair share (keeping $7 and sending back $8 out of $15). This indicates moderate generosity and trust.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the larger, later reward ($6 in 6 weeks) over smaller, sooner rewards down to about $5.75 in 5 weeks, switching preference only when the sooner reward equals or exceeds the later reward. This pattern holds across multiple delayed reward comparisons, indicating a relatively patient intertemporal preference with a clear discounting of earlier smaller amounts but willingness to wait for larger delayed rewards.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator/ultimatum game, the respondent offers to keep $3 and send $2 out of $5, and accepts all offers except the one where they receive nothing. In the trust game, they send the full $5 (which triples to $15) and expect the other to return a fair amount (keeping $7 and sending back $8). These choices reflect moderate fairness, trust, and reciprocity.
