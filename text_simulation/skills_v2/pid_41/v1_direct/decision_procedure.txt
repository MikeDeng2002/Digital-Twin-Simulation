## Effort regulation and cognitive style
The respondent’s Need for Cognition (NFC) profile is directly measured by 18 items. They generally agree a little with statements indicating a preference for complex problems and thinking hard (e.g., items 1, 2, 6, 10, 11, 13, 14, 15, 18 all answered with "Agree a little" or similar), and disagree a little with statements indicating avoidance of thinking or preference for simple tasks (items 3, 4, 5, 7, 8, 9, 12, 16, 17 answered mostly "Disagree a little"). This pattern suggests a moderate but consistent engagement with cognitive effort and enjoyment of thinking.

Regarding cognitive test items, the respondent completed 42 questions including syllogisms, Wason selection tasks, analogies, and CRT-style items. They answered 31 correctly and estimated that the average US adult would answer 28 correctly, indicating above-average performance. Specific CRT-style items were answered correctly: for example, the bat and ball problem was answered with 0.1 (correct), and the lily pad doubling problem with 47 days (correct). Logical syllogisms were mostly answered correctly, except for a few errors such as the conclusion about poodles (item answered "No" when logically it should be "Yes") and the conclusion about plants (correctly answered "Yes"). The respondent also correctly identified the Wason card to turn over (card A) and solved several spatial rotation and pattern completion puzzles with mostly correct answers (e.g., puzzle grid answers D and C, cube rotation answers G, C, and A). No intuitive-trap errors on CRT items were explicitly noted.

## Closure and uncertainty tolerance
The Need for Closure (NFCC) scale is present with 15 items. The respondent shows moderate tolerance for uncertainty and some desire for order. They neither strongly agree nor disagree with disliking uncertainty (item 1: neither agree nor disagree), but agree a little that a well-ordered life suits their temperament (item 3), feel uncomfortable when not understanding reasons for events (item 4), dislike not knowing what to expect (item 6), and feel relieved when making decisions (item 7: agree strongly). They also express a strong desire to reach solutions quickly (items 8 and 9: agree strongly and agree a little). They do not strongly dislike unpredictable situations or ambiguous statements (items 10, 11, 15: neither agree nor disagree). Overall, the profile suggests moderate need for closure with some impatience for unresolved problems and a preference for structure.

## Self-concept stability
Self-concept clarity is measured with 12 items. The respondent agrees a little with statements indicating some instability or conflict in self-beliefs (items 1, 2, 8: agree a little), but disagrees a little with statements indicating confusion or lack of clarity (items 4, 5, 6, 9, 10: mostly disagree a little). They agree a little that they have a clear sense of who they are (item 11). They neither agree nor disagree on spending a lot of time wondering about their identity (item 3) and difficulty making up their mind (item 12). This suggests moderate self-concept clarity with some minor fluctuations or conflicts but overall a reasonably stable self-view.

## Intertemporal patience
Intertemporal choice tasks are present with multiple items offering choices between smaller-sooner and larger-later monetary rewards. The respondent consistently prefers the smaller-sooner option when the difference is small (e.g., $6 in 6 weeks vs. $3-$5.75 in 5 weeks: chose $6 in 6 weeks), switching to the larger-later option only when the delayed amount equals or exceeds the immediate amount (e.g., $6 in 6 weeks vs. $6 or more in 5 weeks: chose the later). This pattern holds across different amounts and delays, indicating a moderate level of patience but a preference for immediacy unless the delayed reward is clearly better.

In social discounting tasks involving money splitting and return decisions, the respondent chooses to keep a moderate share for themselves and send a fair amount to the other person (e.g., keeps $2 and sends $3 out of $5). When in the receiver role, they tend to keep about half or slightly more of the tripled amount and send back the rest, indicating a balance between self-interest and fairness.

- Cognitive biases related to Need for Closure and Maximizing may influence impatience and decision-making, but no direct bias scales (e.g., confirmation bias, anchoring) are present.

- No direct evidence for susceptibility to specific cognitive biases beyond what is measured by Need for Closure and Maximizing scales.

## Summary
The respondent exhibits a moderate Need for Cognition, enjoying thinking and engaging with complex problems, supported by above-average performance on cognitive tests including CRT-style items and logical reasoning. Their Need for Closure is moderate, with a preference for order and quick resolution but tolerance for some uncertainty. They show some maximizing tendencies, holding high personal standards without extreme perfectionism. Self-concept clarity is moderate, with some minor fluctuations but overall a stable self-view. Intertemporal choice behavior indicates moderate patience, favoring immediate rewards unless delayed rewards are substantially better, and social decision-making reflects a balance between fairness and self-interest. Bias susceptibility is limited to moderate social desirability and self-monitoring tendencies, with no direct evidence for other biases.
