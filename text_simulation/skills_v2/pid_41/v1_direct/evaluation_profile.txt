## Budget constraint  
The respondent reports a total family income before taxes in the range of $50,000 to $75,000 (item 24). She is employed full-time (item 28) and lives alone in a household of one person (item 25). These responses provide a direct indication of her budget envelope without any inference about spending style.

## Values hierarchy  
The 24-item values scale shows some compression in ratings, with many values rated between 5 and 9, indicating acquiescence bias. Despite this, relative structure can be extracted. The highest-rated values are Pleasure (item 2) and Ambition (item 10), both rated 9, indicating these are uniquely important guiding principles. Other relatively high values include Competence (item 6), Altruism (item 9), Politeness (item 12), Harmony (item 14), Excitement (item 15), Compassion (item 17), and Civility (item 19), all rated 8. The lowest-rated values are Influence (item 4), Power (item 13), and Superiority (item 24), each rated 3, indicating these are less important to her. Several values are rated moderately (5-7), such as Wealth (5), Status (18), Autonomy (20), Recognition (22), and Tradition (23).

## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the minimalism scale (12 items), the respondent generally disagrees or is neutral with statements endorsing minimalism. She disagrees a little with avoiding accumulating stuff (item 1), restricting number of things owned (item 2), actively avoiding excess possessions (item 4), and preferring empty spaces (item 8). She agrees a little only with preferring simplicity in design (item 6) and being mindful of belongings (item 9, strongly agrees). She neither agrees nor disagrees on some items related to sparse aesthetics (items 3, 5, 7, 11). Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking scales (12 items), she mostly disagrees a little or neither agrees nor disagrees with statements about seeking uniqueness or breaking social rules in consumption. For example, she disagrees a little with actively seeking to develop personal uniqueness by buying special products (item 3), breaking customs (item 5), violating social group rules (item 6), and disliking common products (items 9-12). This indicates a low orientation toward consumer uniqueness or rebellious consumption.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent’s choices indicate moderate risk aversion. For example, in lotteries with a 50% chance of winning various amounts, she prefers the lottery over low certain amounts up to about $2.00-$2.25 but switches preference to the certain amount as it increases beyond that. She accepts lotteries with moderate risk but prefers certain amounts when they approach or exceed expected values. In loss lotteries, she prefers to avoid larger losses and chooses safer options as losses increase. In social economic games (dictator and ultimatum), she proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to the other person, and as a receiver, she accepts offers down to $1 but rejects offers of $0. In the trust game with tripled amounts, she keeps $3 and sends $2 as sender, and as receiver, she returns a fair share but keeps a moderate portion herself, indicating balanced trust and reciprocity. These direct responses show moderate risk tolerance and social fairness preferences.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the larger, later monetary rewards over smaller, sooner rewards across multiple intertemporal choice tasks involving delays of 1 to 7 weeks and amounts ranging from $3 to $11. She chooses the delayed $6 in 6 weeks over amounts up to $5.75 in 5 weeks, switching only when the sooner amount equals or exceeds the delayed amount. Similar patterns hold for $8 and $10 delayed rewards. This indicates relatively high patience and willingness to wait for larger rewards rather than preferring immediate smaller gains.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator and ultimatum games, the respondent proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to the other person, indicating a preference for fairness and generosity. As a receiver, she accepts all offers except the one where the sender keeps all $5 and sends $0. In the trust game where amounts sent are tripled, she keeps $3 and sends $2 as sender, and as receiver, she returns a substantial portion but keeps some for herself, showing balanced reciprocity and trust. These allocations reflect moderate altruism and fairness in anonymous social interactions.

## Summary  
The respondent has a moderate budget envelope with full-time employment and a family income between $50,000 and $75,000, living alone. Her values show some rating compression but highlight Pleasure and Ambition as uniquely important, with low emphasis on Power and Influence. She exhibits low minimalism orientation and low consumer uniqueness-seeking tendencies. Risk preferences indicate moderate risk aversion with balanced social fairness in economic games. She demonstrates relatively high intertemporal patience, preferring larger delayed rewards over smaller immediate ones. Social preferences reveal fairness and reciprocity in sharing and trust decisions.
