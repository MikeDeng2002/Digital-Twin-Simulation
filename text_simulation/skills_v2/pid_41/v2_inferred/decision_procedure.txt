## Closure and uncertainty tolerance  
The Need for Closure scale responses indicate moderate preference for order and predictability: the respondent agrees that "I find that a well ordered life with regular hours suits my temperament" (NFCC #3), "I feel uncomfortable when I don’t understand the reason why an event occurred" (NFCC #4), and "I don’t like to go into a situation without knowing what I can expect" (NFCC #6). They strongly agree with "When I have made a decision, I feel relieved" (NFCC #7) and "When confronted with a problem, I’m dying to reach a solution very quickly" (NFCC #8), indicating a tendency toward quick commitment once a decision is made. However, they neither agree nor disagree with statements about disliking uncertainty or unpredictability (NFCC #1, #10, #15), suggesting some tolerance for ambiguity. Behavioral evidence from free responses shows thoughtful balancing of competing goals and recognition of overthinking tendencies, indicating some discomfort with uncertainty but not extreme intolerance. Decision consistency is high, as seen in economic games and cognitive tasks, supporting a stable closure style with moderate uncertainty tolerance.

## Optimization strategy  
The Maximizer scale responses show a tendency toward high standards and maximizing behavior: the respondent "never settles for second best" (Maximizer #6, agree a little), has "highest standards for myself" (Maximizer #5, agree strongly), and finds "it difficult to shop for a gift for a friend" (Maximizer #3, agree a little). However, they neither strongly endorse nor reject maximizing tendencies in all domains, indicating some flexibility. For example, they neither agree nor disagree with "I often try to find a more interesting version of run-of-the-mill products" (Maximizer #2) and "I actively seek to develop my personal uniqueness by buying special products" (Maximizer #3). This suggests a split between personal and social domains: they maximize on personal achievement and standards but are more moderate or satisficing in consumer uniqueness or social signaling. Economic game behavior (proposing a fair split, accepting fair offers) aligns with a balanced optimization approach rather than strict maximizing.
