## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over certain smaller amounts until the certain amount approaches or exceeds the expected value, indicating risk aversion. For example, they prefer a 50% chance of $6 over certain amounts up to about $3 but switch preference at $5. They also show risk aversion in loss lotteries, preferring to avoid lotteries with potential losses by choosing better certain outcomes.  

In the ultimatum game, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person. As a receiver, they reject offers that are more generous to the other person than to themselves (rejecting offers where the other keeps less than or equal to $2), but accept the offer where the other keeps $3 and they get $2. This indicates a preference for fairness or at least a minimum acceptable share.  

In the trust game, when sending money that will be tripled, the respondent keeps $3 and sends $2. When receiving money, they keep a moderate share and send back a substantial portion, indicating moderate trust and reciprocity.  

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent’s choices in delayed reward tasks show a preference for smaller-sooner rewards over larger-later rewards when the delay difference is small or the amount difference is small, but they switch to larger-later rewards as the amount difference increases. For example, they prefer $3 in 5 weeks over $6 in 6 weeks but switch preference when the immediate amount increases. This pattern suggests moderate patience with some present bias.  

## Social preferences  
In the dictator game, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and give $2 out of $5, indicating a moderate level of generosity. In the ultimatum game as a receiver, they reject offers that are less favorable to themselves (rejecting offers where they get $3 or more but the other keeps less), accepting only the offer where the proposer keeps $3 and they get $2. This suggests a preference for fairness and some willingness to punish unfair offers.  

In the trust game, the respondent sends $2 (out of $5) to be tripled and returns a substantial portion of the tripled amount when receiving money, indicating moderate trust and reciprocity.
