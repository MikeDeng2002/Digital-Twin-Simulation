## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
Minimalism scale responses show disagreement with restricting possessions or actively avoiding excess (items 2, 3, 4 rated low), but strong agreement with preference for visually sparse environments and simplicity in design (items 5 and 6 rated 5). This mixed pattern suggests a mild aesthetic minimalism rather than strict minimalism. Consumerism and uniqueness items are mostly disagreed with (mostly 1s and 2s), indicating low interest in uniqueness or breaking social norms via consumption. The respondent is unlikely to seek premium or niche brands for status or uniqueness. Combined with moderate income and cautious spending, this points to mainstream or budget-brand preferences with some appreciation for simplicity and order in possessions.

## Intertemporal patience  
The delayed-reward tasks show a preference for smaller-sooner rewards over larger-later rewards until the delayed reward becomes sufficiently larger (e.g., choosing $3 now over $6 in 1 week, but switching to $5.50 or more in 5 weeks). This pattern indicates moderate patience with some discounting of future rewards. High Conscientiousness (agree strongly on thoroughness, reliability, planning) supports capacity for planning and delayed gratification. Overall, the respondent exhibits moderate intertemporal patience consistent with conscientious and practical personality traits.

## Risk preferences  
Lottery tasks show a strong preference for certain money over lotteries with equal or higher expected value, indicating risk aversion in gains. For example, the respondent prefers $1.00 or less over a 50% chance at $6, and only switches to accepting lotteries at higher sure amounts. In losses, the respondent consistently prefers smaller sure losses over lotteries with equal or higher expected loss, showing risk aversion in losses as well. This pattern is typical for moderate-income individuals. The respondent’s moderate income and risk aversion align well, with no evidence of gain/loss asymmetry beyond standard risk aversion.

## Social preferences  
The respondent’s social preferences show a clear family-oriented collectivism combined with selfish stranger behavior. In the dictator game, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person (60% to self), and as receiver, rejects offers less than or equal to $2, accepting only the $3-for-me/$2-for-other split or better. This indicates moderate generosity to strangers but a preference for keeping a majority share. Values for Loyalty (7), Politeness (7), Compassion (8), and statements agreeing with family duty and sticking together (items 14, 15, 16 all “Agree a little”) support family collectivism. The respondent’s free-response about aspiring to be thoughtful and kind but admitting to not acting on helping the poor suggests some ambivalence in altruism. Overall, the respondent is moderately altruistic but prioritizes family and self-interest over strangers.

## Product-category predictions for the pricing study  
Given moderate income, mild aesthetic minimalism, and risk aversion, the respondent is likely to be cautious about elevated prices. Grocery staples and household necessities are likely to be purchased even at elevated prices due to necessity and practicality. Premium branded items and indulgence/novelty items are less likely to be purchased at elevated prices, given low consumerism, low uniqueness seeking, and moderate income. Bulk/party-size items may be less favored due to smaller household size and preference for simplicity. Indulgence items like candy or ice cream may be purchased occasionally but not at premium prices. Overall, the respondent will favor mainstream, practical, and reasonably priced products, with some preference for simplicity and minimal clutter.
