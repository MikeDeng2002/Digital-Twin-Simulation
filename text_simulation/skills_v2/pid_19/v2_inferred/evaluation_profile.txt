Given her income, she is likely to prefer mainstream or quality brands but not necessarily premium or luxury brands.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers larger delayed rewards over smaller sooner ones, choosing to wait for $6 in 6 weeks rather than $5.75 in 5 weeks, and similar patterns across multiple delay discounting items (Intertemporal choice). This indicates moderate to high patience. This is consistent with her moderate to high Conscientiousness (e.g., “Makes plans and follows through” rated 4-5, “Does things efficiently” rated 5). She shows a preference for planning and delayed gratification, suggesting good planning capacity and self-control.

## Risk preferences  
Lottery choices show a strong preference for the lottery over certain smaller amounts up to near the expected value, indicating moderate risk tolerance for gains. However, for losses, she prefers certain smaller losses over risky larger losses, indicating risk aversion in the loss domain. She also shows loss aversion by rejecting lotteries with a 50% chance of losing $10 in favor of certain smaller losses. This pattern is typical for moderate-income individuals and consistent with her income and financial concerns. She also prefers the single larger lottery win over multiple smaller wins, indicating a preference for larger, less fragmented gains.

## Social preferences  
The respondent’s values rate Loyalty, Compassion, and Tradition very highly (9), indicating strong family- and group-oriented social preferences. Behavioral game responses show she proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to an anonymous stranger in the ultimatum game, a somewhat selfish offer, and rejects offers where she would receive $2 or less, indicating moderate selfishness toward strangers. As a receiver, she rejects low offers ($2 or less) and accepts fairer offers, showing some fairness concern but also self-interest. In the trust game, she keeps all the money and sends nothing back when receiving tripled amounts, indicating low reciprocity toward strangers. This reveals a gap: generous and loyal toward family and group values but more self-interested and less generous toward strangers. This stranger-vs-family distinction is preserved.

Given the respondent’s moderate to high income, mindful but non-minimalist consumer orientation, and moderate risk aversion, she is likely to be moderately price sensitive but willing to pay for quality and necessity. Overall, she is less likely to indulge in premium or bulk purchases at elevated prices but will maintain necessary purchases with some quality preference.
