## Budget constraint  
The respondent reports a total family income of $100,000 or more before taxes (income item). She is currently employed full-time and lives in a household of two people. These direct income and employment items define a moderate to high budget envelope without further inference about spending style.

## Values hierarchy  
The 24-item values scale shows evidence of rating compression, with many values rated highly (6 to 9 on a 9-point scale). Given this, only the relative structure is extracted. The highest-rated values (rated 9) include Competence (item 6), Humility (7), Achievement (8), Loyalty (11), Honesty (16), Compassion (17), Civility (19), Equality (21), and Tradition (23). The lowest-rated values include Recognition (22) rated 1, Status (18) rated 2, Power (13) rated 3, and Superiority (24) rated 4. This pattern indicates a strong emphasis on mastery, moral integrity, social harmony, and equality, with low importance placed on status, dominance, and fame.

## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the 12-item minimalism scale, the respondent neither agrees nor disagrees with most minimalism statements but strongly disagrees with actively avoiding excess possessions, preferring sparse environments, and keeping a sparse aesthetic (items 4, 5, 7). She strongly agrees that she is mindful of what she owns and that her belongings are carefully curated and thoughtfully selected (items 9-12). This suggests a mindful but not minimalist consumer orientation. Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking scales, the respondent mostly neither agrees nor disagrees with statements about seeking uniqueness or breaking social rules around consumption, except for a slight agreement with breaking customs and rules in product use (item 5). She strongly disagrees with avoiding common products or brands (items 10, 12), indicating low uniqueness-seeking or consumer rebellion.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over certain smaller amounts when the lottery offers a chance to win money (e.g., 50% chance of $6 vs. certain amounts up to $5.75), indicating risk tolerance in gains. She also prefers the certain option over lotteries involving losses (e.g., 50% chance of losing $10 vs. certain smaller losses), indicating risk aversion in losses. In the ultimatum game, she proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person and would reject offers where she receives $2 or less, showing moderate fairness concerns. In the trust game, she would keep all the money sent to her and send nothing back, indicating low trust or reciprocity in this context.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers larger delayed rewards over smaller sooner rewards across multiple intertemporal choice tasks (e.g., $6 in 6 weeks over $5.75 in 5 weeks, $8 in 7 weeks over $7.75 in 6 weeks, $10 in 7 weeks over $9.75 in 5 weeks, and similar patterns for choices involving immediate rewards). This pattern indicates relatively high patience and willingness to wait for larger future rewards.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator/ultimatum game, the respondent offers to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person and would reject offers where she receives $2 or less, indicating a preference for fairness and some willingness to punish unfair offers. In the trust game, she would keep all money sent and return nothing, indicating low trust or reciprocity. No other economic game data are present.
