## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery over certain smaller amounts when the certain amount is substantially less than the expected value, but switches preference to the certain amount as it approaches or exceeds the expected value. For example, for a 50% chance of $6 and 50% chance of $0, she prefers the lottery over amounts up to $2.75 but prefers the certain amount at $3.00 and above. For a 50% chance of $8 and 50% chance of $2, she prefers the certain amount at all tested values ($2.50 and above). For a 50% chance of $10 and 50% chance of $0, she prefers the lottery at lower certain amounts but switches to the certain amount at $4.25 and above.  

For lotteries involving losses, she prefers the lottery over smaller losses but switches to preferring the certain loss as the certain loss amount approaches the expected loss. For example, for a 50% chance of losing $6 and 50% chance of $0, she prefers the lottery over losses up to -$3.50 but prefers the certain loss at -$3.25 and above. For a 50% chance of losing $8 and 50% chance of losing $2, she prefers the lottery over losses up to -$6.00 but switches to the certain loss at -$5.75 and above. For a 50% chance of losing $10 and 50% chance of $0, she prefers the lottery over losses up to -$5.00 but switches at -$4.50 and above.  

In a mixed lottery with a 50% chance of losing $8 and 50% chance of winning various amounts, she consistently prefers the lottery over a sure $0 for all tested positive values of x ($7 to $20).  

These choices indicate moderate risk aversion for gains and losses, with a tendency to accept fair or slightly unfavorable gambles but to prefer sure amounts when close to expected values. She also shows loss aversion by preferring to avoid certain losses when possible.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent completed multiple intertemporal choice tasks involving choices between smaller-sooner and larger-later monetary rewards. She consistently prefers the larger, later reward over the smaller, sooner reward when the difference is moderate to large, switching preference only when the sooner reward is close in value or only slightly smaller. For example, she prefers $6 in 6 weeks over $3 to $5.25 in 5 weeks but switches to preferring the sooner reward at $5.50 and above. Similar patterns hold for $8 in 7 weeks and $10 in 7 weeks. For choices involving immediate rewards (now or in 1-2 weeks), she shows similar patterns, preferring delayed rewards unless the immediate reward is close to or exceeds the delayed amount.  

This pattern indicates moderate to high patience and willingness to wait for larger rewards, with some discounting of delayed rewards but not extreme impatience.

## Social preferences  
In dictator and ultimatum game scenarios, the respondent proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to the other person when acting as the proposer in a $5 split, indicating a preference for a fair or slightly generous split. As the receiver, she accepts all offers from $0 to $5 kept by the proposer, including the most selfish offer ($5 for proposer, $0 for receiver), indicating high acceptance and low rejection of unfair offers.  

In the trust game where money sent is tripled, she chooses to keep $2 and send $3 as the sender, and as the receiver, she returns a substantial portion of the money received, keeping $7 and sending $8 back when receiving $15, and similarly generous splits for smaller amounts. This indicates a reciprocal and fair-minded social preference, valuing fairness and cooperation.
