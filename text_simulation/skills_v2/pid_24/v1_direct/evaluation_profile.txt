## Budget constraint  
The respondent reports a total family income from all sources last year as "Less than $30,000." Employment status is "Unemployed." Household size is 2 people. These direct income and employment items indicate a limited budget envelope.

## Values hierarchy  
The 24-item values scale shows some rating compression, with many values rated moderately high (e.g., several 7s and 8s). Explicitly, the highest-rated values are Ambition (item 10) and Power (item 13), both rated 9 (Highly Important). Other relatively high values include Pleasure (item 2), Competence (item 6), and Achievement (item 8), each rated 8. The lowest-rated values are Forgiveness (item 3), Status (item 18), Equality (item 21), and Recognition (item 22), each rated 1 (Not Important). Thus, the respondent places strong relative importance on ambition, power, and achievement, and low importance on forgiveness, status, equality, and recognition.

## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the minimalism scale (12 items), the respondent shows moderate agreement with minimalism-related statements such as "Less is more" (item 3, agree a little), actively avoiding excess possessions (item 4, agree a little), drawn to visually sparse environments (item 5, agree a little), and strong agreement with preferring simplicity in design (item 6, agree strongly) and leaving spaces visually empty over filling them (item 8, agree strongly). However, the respondent disagrees or is neutral on mindful ownership and curation items (items 2, 7, 9, 10, 11, 12 mostly disagree a little or strongly).  

Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking scales, the respondent strongly disagrees with all items measuring uniqueness-seeking and consumerism orientation (items 1-4 all "disagree strongly"), indicating no preference for uniqueness or breaking social consumption norms.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the certain monetary amounts over lotteries when the certain amount is close to or slightly less than the expected lottery value, indicating risk aversion. For example, in lotteries with 50% chance of winning $6 or $0, the respondent prefers certain amounts starting at about $1.75 and above. Similarly, for lotteries with 50% chance of winning $8 or $2, the respondent prefers certain amounts starting at about $7.50 and above. For lotteries involving losses, the respondent prefers to avoid lotteries with large potential losses, choosing safer options. This pattern indicates moderate to strong risk aversion in both gains and losses domains.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent’s choices in intertemporal choice tasks show a preference for smaller-sooner rewards over larger-later rewards when the delay difference is small, but switches to larger-later rewards when the delayed amount is sufficiently higher. For example, the respondent prefers $6 in 6 weeks over $5.25 in 5 weeks but switches to preferring $5.25 in 5 weeks over $6 in 6 weeks at a certain threshold. This pattern suggests moderate patience with some present bias. The respondent also prefers immediate smaller amounts over delayed larger amounts in short delays (e.g., $6 in 1 week vs. $5 now, prefers $5 now), indicating some impatience for near-term rewards.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator and ultimatum games, the respondent proposes to keep all $5 for themselves and send $0 to the other person when acting as the proposer, indicating a fully selfish allocation. When acting as the receiver, the respondent accepts all offers except the one where the proposer keeps all $5 and sends $0, which is rejected. In the trust game where money sent is tripled, the respondent sends all $5 to the other person (maximal trust) but when receiving, keeps all the money and sends back $0, indicating distrust or selfishness in return. These choices show a pattern of selfishness in allocation and low reciprocity in social economic games.

## Summary  
The respondent has a limited budget with low income and unemployment status. Their values emphasize ambition, power, and achievement, while forgiveness, equality, and recognition are least important. They show moderate endorsement of minimalism, especially in design simplicity and visual sparsity, but do not seek uniqueness or consumerism. Risk preferences indicate moderate to strong risk aversion in both gains and losses. Intertemporal choices reveal moderate patience with some present bias, favoring immediate rewards when delays are short. Social preferences are characterized by selfish behavior in economic games, proposing to keep all money and rejecting unfair offers, with low reciprocity when trusted by others.
