## Effort regulation and cognitive style  
This individual exhibits a strong preference for engaging in effortful cognitive activity, as evidenced by their high Need for Cognition (NFC) scores: they "like to have the responsibility of handling a situation that requires a lot of thinking" (5 - agree strongly), "find satisfaction in deliberating hard and for long hours" (5), and "really enjoy a task that involves coming up with new solutions to problems" (5). They also "usually end up deliberating about issues even when they do not affect me personally" (5), indicating a deep, reflective thinking style. Their Big Five Conscientiousness facets align with this: they rate themselves as extremely organized (9), reliable (5), efficient (8), and systematic (8), and strongly agree that they "always try to make my work as accurate and error-free as possible" (7). They also "make plans and follow through with them" (5) and "persevere until the task is finished" (4), showing good effort regulation and persistence.

Cognitive test performance shows generally solid reasoning with some specific error patterns. They correctly answered classic trick questions (e.g., "How many cubic feet of dirt in a hole?" = 0; "If you pass the person in second place, you are in second place"), indicating good attention to detail and avoidance of common heuristic errors. However, in logical reasoning tasks, they made some errors, such as incorrectly judging that "Plants are not animals" does not follow logically from "All animals love water" and "Plants do not love water," which is a classic conditional reasoning error (answering "No" when "Yes" is correct). They also incorrectly answered that "Roses are flowers" does not follow from "All flowers have petals" and "Roses have petals," showing difficulty with affirming the consequent or misunderstanding logical syllogisms. This pattern suggests some susceptibility to belief bias or confusion in conditional logic.

In spatial reasoning tasks (cube rotations, pattern completions), the participant showed mixed performance, sometimes choosing "I do not know" or incorrect options, indicating some difficulty with mental rotation or spatial visualization under uncertainty. Their vocabulary and synonym/antonym tests were mostly correct, showing good verbal reasoning and lexical knowledge.

Overall, the profile suggests a highly conscientious, effortful, and analytical thinker who enjoys deep cognitive engagement but may occasionally struggle with formal logical reasoning and spatial tasks, possibly due to overthinking or cognitive overload. Their free responses describe themselves as "very organized and logical," "obsessive problem solver," and "prefer to be in control," which aligns with the observed cognitive style.

They also "dislike questions which could be answered in many different ways" (3 - neutral), but "dislike it when a person’s statement could mean many different things" (4 - agree a little), reinforcing a preference for clarity and unambiguous information.

## Optimization strategy

They "never settle for second best" is rated as neither agree nor disagree (3), but they "have the highest standards for myself" (5 - agree strongly) and "are on the lookout for better opportunities" (5). They "often find it difficult to shop for a gift for a friend" (4), indicating some indecision or difficulty in choice, a hallmark of maximizers.

However, they "disagree a little" with "I prefer to work without instructions" (2) and "like to do things in a new way" (4 - neither agree nor disagree), suggesting some openness to routine and conventional approaches rather than constant novelty seeking.

Their consumerism and uniqueness scales are strongly low: they "disagree strongly" with all statements about seeking uniqueness or breaking social rules in consumption, indicating a satisficing approach in social domains, preferring conformity and simplicity.

In economic games, they propose to keep $3 and send $2 in the dictator game, reflecting a balance between self-interest and fairness, and when receiving, they tend to keep about two-thirds of the money and send back one-third, showing pragmatic fairness but also self-protection. Their thoughts during these decisions emphasize fairness but also concern about reciprocity and fairness from the other party.

Overall, the participant appears to optimize with a strong internal standard but is willing to settle for "good enough" in social and consumer domains, balancing fairness and self-interest without excessive re-evaluation or perfectionism.

## Intertemporal patience  
The participant shows moderate intertemporal patience. In delayed reward tasks, they prefer immediate smaller rewards over delayed larger ones when the delay difference is small (e.g., prefer $3 now over $6 in 1 week), but switch to preferring delayed rewards as the immediate amount gets closer to the delayed amount (e.g., prefer $5.25 now over $6 in 6 weeks, but $6 now over $8 in 1 week). This pattern suggests a typical hyperbolic discounting curve with some patience for larger delays.

Their Big Five Conscientiousness and free responses emphasize planning, organization, and follow-through, which support moderate patience and goal-directed behavior.

They describe themselves as "organized and logical," "prefer to be in control," and "obsessive problem solver," which aligns with a tendency to plan and delay gratification when worthwhile.

- Denominator neglect: Low likelihood, given good understanding of probabilities and base rates in numeric tasks.  
- Belief bias: Moderate susceptibility, as seen in logical reasoning errors where belief interfered with logical validity judgments.  
- Wason matching bias: Likely low, as they correctly identified cards to test rules (A and 3), showing good conditional reasoning.

**MODERATE CONFIDENCE**

- Risk-benefit nonseparability (affect heuristic): Moderate, as they show some emotional responses but also deliberate cognitive style.

**LOW CONFIDENCE**  
- Framing effects, anchoring, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, outcome bias, sunk-cost effect, less-is-more effect, omission bias, Allais/ certainty effects, myside bias: These biases are not reliably predictable from cognitive ability or personality alone, so no strong predictions.
