## Optimization strategy  
The respondent shows a mixed but generally satisficing approach rather than strict maximizing. They disagree strongly or somewhat with maximizer tendencies such as never settling for second best or always having the highest standards (Maximizer items 5, 6). They do not report difficulty shopping for gifts or clothing, nor do they seek out unique or unusual products to establish a distinctive image (Maximizer items 1-4, 9-12). Their economic game behavior supports a pragmatic rather than perfectionist approach: in dictator and trust games, they propose and accept moderately fair splits (e.g., keeping $3 and sending $2 in the dictator game, sending $3 and expecting a roughly even split back in the trust game). This suggests a balance between fairness and self-interest rather than exhaustive search for the optimal outcome. There is no evidence of domain-specific maximizing; rather, the respondent appears to satisfice consistently across domains.

They do not experience significant internal conflict or uncertainty about their identity. However, their free-response self-descriptions reveal some tension between their aspirational self (more patient, kinder, accepting help) and their actual self (imperfect, sometimes losing their cool, resisting help). The respondent’s self-concept is coherent but acknowledges areas for personal growth.

## Behavioral vs. stated-preference gap  
A meaningful gap exists between the respondent’s stated values and their behavioral tendencies. They endorse highly prosocial and altruistic values (Values items: Altruism 9, Compassion 9, Politeness 9, Civility 9, Trust 8, Harmony 9), and express a desire to be kinder and more giving in their free responses. They also report tendencies to refuse help and insist on doing things themselves, despite recognizing this as unwise. In economic games, they behave fairly but not maximally altruistically, keeping a majority share in dictator games and expecting fair but not generous returns in trust games. This gap implies that while the respondent aspires to prosocial and patient behavior, their actual behavior and emotional responses sometimes contradict these ideals. This discrepancy suggests moderate self-report trustworthiness but also indicates that reasoning under uncertainty may be influenced by emotional and interpersonal tensions, reducing reliability in social or affect-laden decisions.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent shows relatively low intertemporal patience, consistently preferring smaller-sooner rewards over larger-later ones across multiple delayed reward tasks. They choose immediate or near-immediate payoffs even when delayed rewards are only slightly larger, indicating a preference for immediacy. This is consistent with their self-reported need to be more patient and their recognition of health and aging concerns. Their high conscientiousness (Big Five) and planning tendencies (agreeing strongly with "makes plans and follows through") suggest that this impatience is not due to lack of planning but may reflect affective or motivational factors. Overall, the respondent exhibits a tendency toward impatience in intertemporal choice despite conscientiousness and planning abilities.

## Bias susceptibility predictions  
Predictable biases from cognitive signals:  
- Probability matching vs. maximizing: The respondent’s cognitive style and lottery choices suggest a tendency toward maximizing rather than probability matching, as they correctly estimate expected values and choose consistent lottery exchanges.  
- Denominator neglect: The respondent shows good understanding of probabilities and percentages (e.g., correctly converting 1 in 1,000 to 0.1%), indicating low susceptibility to denominator neglect.  
- Belief bias in syllogisms: The respondent correctly accepts logically valid conclusions and rejects invalid ones, except for one error in a syllogism involving animals and plants, suggesting moderate susceptibility to belief bias but generally good logical reasoning.  
- Wason matching bias: The respondent correctly identifies the cards to turn over in the Wason selection task, indicating low susceptibility to matching bias.

Weakly predictable or not predictable biases (acknowledging limits):  
Framing effects, anchoring and adjustment, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, outcome bias, sunk-cost effect, less-is-more effect, omission bias, Allais/ certainty effects, and myside bias cannot be confidently predicted from the available data. The respondent’s cognitive and personality profile does not provide strong evidence to over-claim predictive power for these biases.

## Summary  
This respondent exhibits a high need for cognition and conscientiousness, engaging effortfully and systematically with complex cognitive tasks. They show moderate need for closure, balancing a desire for order with thoughtful deliberation. Their optimization strategy is generally satisficing rather than maximizing, consistent across domains. The self-concept is stable and coherent, though there is a notable gap between aspirational kindness and actual interpersonal irritability and resistance to help. Intertemporal patience is relatively low despite conscientious planning. Cognitive test performance predicts low susceptibility to denominator neglect, belief bias, and Wason matching bias, with a maximizing approach to probability. The behavioral vs. stated-preference gap suggests some emotional and social tensions that may affect reasoning reliability in interpersonal contexts.
