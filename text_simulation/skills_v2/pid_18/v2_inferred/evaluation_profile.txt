## Budget constraint  
The respondent is a 65+ year-old male living in the Western United States, married, with a household size of two, and currently employed full-time. His reported family income is in the $30,000-$50,000 range, which is modest for a two-person household with full-time employment. There are no explicit free-response signals indicating financial stress, but the income level combined with household size suggests a moderate budget constraint for everyday purchases. The respondent also identifies as a tightwad (difficulty spending money) and often has trouble spending money even when needed (Minimalism item 1: Tightwad; Minimalism item 2: Often trouble spending money). This implies a relatively low spending ceiling for discretionary purchases, with a strong tendency to limit expenditures.

The respondent agrees strongly or moderately with minimalism items indicating active avoidance of excess possessions and mindful ownership (e.g., item 4: strongly agree to actively avoid acquiring excess possessions; items 1, 2, 6, 9, 11, 12: agree a little). ## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers delayed larger rewards over smaller sooner rewards until the delayed reward equals or exceeds the immediate reward (e.g., choosing $6 in 6 weeks over $5.75 in 5 weeks, but switching preference at $7 in 5 weeks). This pattern indicates moderate to high patience and willingness to wait for better outcomes. This is consistent with high Conscientiousness (items 3, 13, 28: strongly agree on thoroughness, reliability, perseverance) and self-reports of wanting to be more patient. The respondent’s cognitive test performance (33/42 correct) and financial literacy also support reasonable planning capacity.

## Risk preferences  
In lottery tasks, the respondent prefers the lottery over certain amounts only when the certain amount is substantially lower than the expected value, indicating moderate risk aversion for gains. For losses, the respondent prefers certain smaller losses over risky larger losses, again consistent with risk aversion. The respondent’s choices show some loss aversion but not extreme. The respondent’s income level and age align with typical moderate risk aversion. The respondent’s answers to lottery happiness and upset scenarios also suggest sensitivity to losses and gains framing.

## Social preferences  
The respondent’s values emphasize altruism, compassion, loyalty, and politeness highly, and behavioral game choices show moderate generosity: in the dictator game, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person, indicating some selfishness but also willingness to share. As the receiver, the respondent accepts all offers except the zero-share offer, showing fairness expectations. In the trust game with tripling, the respondent sends $3 (out of $5) and returns roughly half of received amounts, indicating moderate reciprocity and fairness. The respondent agrees moderately that family duty and loyalty are important (items 14, 15: agree a little), but not strongly collectivist. The social preference profile is moderately family-oriented with some selfishness toward strangers, consistent with the stranger-vs-family distinction.

## Product-category predictions for the pricing study  
Given the respondent’s modest income, strong minimalism, and tightwad spending habits, the likelihood of purchasing grocery staples at elevated prices is moderate to low; staples are necessary but spending is cautious. Premium branded items are less likely to be purchased at elevated prices due to low emphasis on wealth, status, and consumerism, and a preference for mindful, minimal ownership. Bulk/party-size items are unlikely to be favored given the small household size and minimalism. Indulgence/novelty items may be purchased occasionally but not at premium prices, as pleasure is valued but spending is constrained. Household necessities will be purchased but with price sensitivity. Overall, the respondent is predicted to favor budget or mainstream brands, avoid premium pricing, and limit purchases to essentials or meaningful items.

## Summary  
This respondent is a modest-income, older male with a strong minimalist orientation and tightwad spending habits, leading to cautious and limited purchasing behavior. He values competence, altruism, politeness, and social harmony highly, aligning with moderate conscientiousness and agreeableness. His intertemporal patience is moderate to high, supporting planning and delayed gratification, while his risk preferences show moderate risk aversion consistent with his income and age. Social preferences reveal moderate generosity toward family and strangers but with a pragmatic self-interest. Pricing and product choices will reflect a preference for essential, budget-conscious purchases, avoidance of premium brands, and limited indulgence spending despite valuing simple pleasures and social fairness.
