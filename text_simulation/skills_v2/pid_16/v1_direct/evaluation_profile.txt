## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery tasks with choices between lotteries and certain amounts. The respondent generally prefers the lottery over low certain amounts but switches preference to certain amounts as the sure payoff increases, indicating some risk aversion. For example, for a 50% chance to win $6 vs. certain amounts, they prefer the lottery over amounts up to about $1.75 but prefer certain amounts above $2. For higher stakes lotteries (e.g., 50% chance to win $10), they prefer certain amounts over the lottery starting at about $9.50. For lotteries involving losses, the respondent prefers the sure loss over lotteries with larger potential losses, indicating risk aversion in losses as well. Overall, the respondent exhibits moderate risk aversion in both gains and losses.

## Intertemporal patience  
In intertemporal choice tasks involving delayed monetary rewards, the respondent consistently prefers the larger, later reward over smaller, sooner rewards when the difference is moderate to large, indicating patience. For example, they prefer $6 in 6 weeks over $5.75 in 5 weeks, and only switch to preferring the sooner reward when it equals or exceeds the delayed reward (e.g., $6 in 6 weeks vs. $6 or $7 in 5 weeks). Similar patterns hold for other amounts and delays. This suggests a relatively patient time preference with a willingness to wait for larger delayed rewards.

## Social preferences  
In dictator and ultimatum game scenarios, the respondent proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to the other person when acting as the proposer in a $5 split, indicating a moderately generous offer. When acting as the receiver, they accept offers down to $1 but reject offers of $0, showing some fairness sensitivity. In the trust game where money sent is tripled, the respondent chooses to send the full $5 as the sender, indicating high trust or generosity. As the receiver, they keep a majority share of the tripled amount (e.g., keep $8 of $15, send back $7), showing moderate reciprocity but also self-interest. Their thoughts during these decisions emphasize equality, sharing, and partnership when receiving, and unemotional, stoic, and solid attitudes when sending.
