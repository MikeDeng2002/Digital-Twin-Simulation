This suggests a cognitive style that is neither strongly effortful nor avoidant. Cognitive test performance shows mixed accuracy: the respondent correctly answered many logical syllogisms and analogies but made errors on some classic cognitive reflection test (CRT)-style items (e.g., the bat and ball problem answered as 9 instead of 5 cents). The self-estimated number of correct cognitive test answers (22/42) matches their estimate of the average US adult, indicating moderate metacognitive insight. Conscientiousness items from Big Five show mild to moderate agreement with being thorough, reliable, and persevering (items 3, 13, 28: “agree a little”), consistent with a somewhat conscientious cognitive style that supports sustained effort but not extreme diligence. Error patterns include typical CRT errors and some logical reasoning errors (e.g., endorsing invalid conclusions in syllogisms), indicating moderate but not high cognitive reflection and analytic reasoning.

The respondent does not show impatience for quick solutions (NFCC item 8: “disagree a little”) and does not feel relief strongly upon decision (item 7: “neither agree nor disagree”), indicating a balanced approach to closure rather than impulsive commitment.

## Optimization strategy  
The Maximizer scale responses indicate a generally low to moderate maximizing tendency. The respondent disagrees or is neutral on items reflecting high standards and never settling for second best (items 5, 6: “neither agree nor disagree” or “disagree a little”). They do not strongly endorse maximizing behaviors or perfectionism. Shopping behavior and gift selection items also show mild disagreement with maximizing tendencies. However, in economic games, the respondent proposes a moderately generous split ($2 keep / $3 send) in the dictator game and accepts fair offers but rejects very unfair ones, indicating a pragmatic approach to optimization that balances fairness and self-interest. There is no evidence of domain-specific maximizing; rather, the respondent appears to satisfice moderately across domains.

Self-Concept Clarity (SCC) scale responses show strong agreement with having a clear and stable self-concept (item 11: “agree strongly”) and strong disagreement with statements about conflicting or changing self-beliefs (items 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 8, 9: mostly “disagree strongly” or “disagree a little”). ## Behavioral vs. stated-preference gap  
There is a notable gap between the respondent’s stated values and their economic game behavior. The respondent rates altruism, compassion, and trust as moderately to highly important values (values scale items 5, 7, 9, 16, 17: mostly 7 or 8 out of 9), and their free-response thoughts during the trust game emphasize equality, sharing, and partnership. However, in the dictator game, the respondent chooses to keep $3 and send $2, a less generous split than their altruistic values might predict. Similarly, as the receiver in the trust game, they keep a majority share of the tripled amount (keeping $8 of $15), which is more self-interested than the altruistic values suggest. This gap implies that while the respondent aspires to be altruistic and cooperative, their actual economic decisions reflect a pragmatic or self-protective stance. This discrepancy suggests moderate reliability in self-report but some social desirability or aspirational bias in values and self-descriptions relative to behavior.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers larger, later rewards over smaller, sooner ones in intertemporal choice tasks, choosing delayed payments even when the immediate alternative is close in value (e.g., preferring $6 in 6 weeks over $5.75 in 5 weeks). This pattern indicates moderate to high patience. Conscientiousness items (e.g., “makes plans and follows through,” “does things efficiently”) support this patience and planning orientation. The respondent’s choices reflect a willingness to wait for better outcomes, consistent with a future-oriented and self-controlled decision style.

## Bias susceptibility predictions  
Predictable from cognitive signals:  
- Probability matching vs. maximizing: The respondent’s lottery choices show a tendency to prefer certain amounts over risky lotteries even when expected values are similar, suggesting a probability maximizing tendency rather than matching.  
- Denominator neglect: The respondent correctly interprets probabilities and percentages in multiple tasks (e.g., lottery winning chances, disease probabilities), indicating low susceptibility to denominator neglect.  
- Belief bias in syllogisms: The respondent accepts logically invalid conclusions (e.g., “All eastern countries are communist; China is not eastern; therefore China is not communist” answered “Yes”), indicating moderate belief bias in syllogistic reasoning.  
- Wason matching bias: The respondent correctly identifies the card to turn over to test a conditional rule (choosing “A” card), suggesting low Wason matching bias.

Weakly predictable or not predictable from cognitive signals:  
- Framing effects, anchoring and adjustment, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, outcome bias, sunk-cost effect, less-is-more effect, omission bias, Allais/certainty effects, and myside bias cannot be confidently predicted from the available data. The respondent’s moderate cognitive reflection and balanced personality suggest moderate susceptibility, but no strong claims can be made.

## Summary  
The respondent exhibits a moderate cognitive engagement style with some typical reasoning errors, including CRT mistakes and belief bias in syllogisms, but generally good probabilistic reasoning. They show a balanced tolerance for uncertainty and a preference for structured routines without strong intolerance of ambiguity. Their optimization strategy is pragmatic, with moderate satisficing rather than maximizing tendencies. Self-concept clarity is high and stable, with consistent self-descriptions. A meaningful gap exists between altruistic values and economic game behavior, indicating some aspirational bias in self-report relative to actual choices. Intertemporal patience is moderate to high, supported by conscientiousness and planning. Bias susceptibility predictions indicate low denominator neglect and Wason matching bias but moderate belief bias in syllogistic reasoning.
