## Budget constraint

## Values hierarchy  
The 24-item values scale shows evidence of rating compression, with many values rated highly (mostly 5 to 9). Despite this, the respondent rated WEALTH (item 1), STATUS (item 18), AUTONOMY (item 20), and SUPERIORITY (item 24) at the highest level of 9, indicating these are relatively more important guiding principles. Other highly rated values include PLEASURE (8), ACHIEVEMENT (8), POLITENESS (8), and RECOGNITION (8). The lowest rated value is TRADITION (item 23) at 4, which is notably lower than most others. Several values such as TRUST (5), POWER (5), and EQUALITY (5) are rated moderately. This pattern suggests a strong emphasis on personal success, independence, and status, with less importance placed on tradition.

## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the minimalism scale (12 items), the respondent generally disagrees with statements about avoiding accumulation and restricting possessions (items 1-4 rated 2 - disagree a little), indicating a low preference for minimalism. However, she agrees somewhat with preferring simplicity in design (item 6 rated 5 - agree strongly) and is somewhat drawn to visually sparse environments (item 5 rated 4 - agree a little). Other items about mindful ownership and curation are mostly neutral or slightly disagreeing. This suggests a moderate appreciation for simplicity in aesthetics but not a strong minimalist lifestyle.

Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking, the respondent strongly agrees with statements reflecting a desire for uniqueness and originality in products and consumption (items 2, 3, 4, 6, 7 rated 5 - agree strongly; items 1, 5, 8 rated 4 - agree a little). She disagrees somewhat with avoiding common products or brands (items 10-12 rated 2 - disagree a little), indicating she does not strongly avoid popular products but values personal distinctiveness. Overall, she exhibits a strong orientation toward consumer uniqueness and originality.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over smaller certain amounts when the certain amount is substantially less than the expected lottery value, indicating some risk tolerance. For example, she prefers the lottery over amounts up to about half the expected value but switches to preferring certain amounts when they approach or exceed the expected lottery value. She also rejects offers in the ultimatum game that leave her with nothing and keeps all money when acting as the proposer, indicating a self-interested but not spiteful stance. No explicit direct risk preference scale is reported beyond these lottery choices.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers larger delayed rewards over smaller sooner rewards in all intertemporal choice tasks presented, even when the delay difference is small. For example, she chooses $6 in 6 weeks over $5.75 in 5 weeks, $8 in 7 weeks over $7.75 in 6 weeks, and $10 in 7 weeks over $9.75 in 5 weeks. She switches to preferring the sooner reward only when the amounts are equal or the sooner reward is larger. This pattern indicates relatively high patience and willingness to wait for larger delayed rewards.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator and ultimatum games, the respondent chooses to keep the entire $5 for herself when acting as the proposer and sends $0 to the other person. When acting as the receiver, she accepts all offers except the one where she would receive $0, which she rejects. In the trust game where amounts sent are tripled, she again keeps all money sent to her and sends nothing back. Her thoughts during these decisions indicate a pragmatic, self-interested approach with little concern for the other anonymous person, reflecting low altruism or trust in anonymous social exchanges.

## Environmental concern  
The respondent strongly disagrees with all six items measuring environmental concern, including the importance of products not harming the environment, considering environmental impact in decisions, and willingness to be inconvenienced for environmental reasons. This indicates very low environmental concern.

## Summary  
The respondent has a moderate household budget with self-employment income between $50,000 and $75,000 and lives with one other person. Her values emphasize wealth, status, autonomy, and superiority, with less importance on tradition. She shows low minimalism but a strong consumer orientation toward uniqueness and originality. She exhibits moderate risk tolerance in lottery tasks and high patience in intertemporal choices. Social preferences reveal a self-interested stance with minimal sharing or trust in anonymous interactions. Environmental concern is very low. Overall, she prioritizes personal success and independence, values distinctiveness in consumption, and prefers to maximize personal gain in social-economic exchanges.
