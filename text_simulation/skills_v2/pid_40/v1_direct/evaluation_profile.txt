## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over certain smaller amounts when the certain amount is substantially less than the expected value, indicating some risk tolerance. For example, they prefer a 50% chance of $6 over certain amounts up to $3.50, switching to preferring the certain amount only at $4 or more. Similar patterns hold for other lotteries with different payoffs. For losses, the respondent prefers to avoid larger losses, switching preference to the certain loss option as the certain loss amount improves. This pattern suggests moderate risk aversion for losses and moderate risk tolerance for gains. The respondent also chooses to keep a larger share in dictator and trust games, indicating cautiousness in social risk. Overall, the data show moderate risk tolerance for gains and risk aversion for losses.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the larger, later reward ($6 in 6 weeks) over smaller, sooner rewards down to $5.75 in 5 weeks, switching preference only when the sooner reward equals or exceeds the later reward. This pattern is consistent across multiple delayed reward tasks with varying amounts and delays. For immediate versus delayed rewards (e.g., $6 in 1 week vs. $5.75 now), the respondent prefers the immediate reward, indicating some present bias or impatience for very short delays. Overall, the respondent shows moderate patience for delayed rewards over weeks but prefers immediate rewards when delays are very short.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator game where the respondent proposes a split of $5, they choose to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person. As the receiver, they accept all offers from $0 to $5 kept by the sender, including the least favorable ($0 for sender, $5 for receiver) and the most favorable ($5 for sender, $0 for receiver). In the trust game where money sent is tripled, the respondent keeps all $5 and sends $0 as sender, indicating no trust in the other person. As receiver, they keep $5 and send back $10 when receiving $5 (tripled to $15), and keep large shares when receiving smaller amounts, indicating some reciprocity but a preference for keeping a majority. Their thoughts during these decisions emphasize distrust of others and preference for securing their own payoff. This pattern indicates moderate selfishness combined with some willingness to share when it benefits them.
