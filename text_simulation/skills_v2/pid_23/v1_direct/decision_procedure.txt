## Effort regulation and cognitive style
The person's Need for Cognition (NFC) profile is directly measured by 18 items. They generally agree a little or strongly with statements indicating a preference for effortful thinking and intellectual challenge. For example, they "agree a little" with items such as "I would prefer complex to simple problems" (Item 1), "I like to have the responsibility of handling a situation that requires a lot of thinking" (Item 2), and "I find satisfaction in deliberating hard and for long hours" (Item 6). They "disagree a little" with statements indicating avoidance of thinking, such as "Thinking is not my idea of fun" (Item 3) and "I try to anticipate and avoid situations where there is likely chance I will have to think in depth about something" (Item 5). They also "agree a little" that they "usually end up deliberating about issues even when they do not affect me personally" (Item 18). This pattern suggests a moderate to somewhat high need for cognition.

Regarding cognitive test items, the person answered 42 questions and estimated they answered 36 correctly, which is above their estimate of the average US adult (20 correct). Specific CRT-style items were answered correctly: for example, the bat and ball problem was answered as 0.05 dollars, which is the correct analytic answer. The lily pad doubling problem was answered as 47 days, also correct. The person correctly solved syllogistic reasoning problems, such as "All animals love water; plants do not love water; therefore plants are not animals" (answered "Yes" correctly). They also correctly identified cards to turn over in the Wason selection task (cards A and 3). However, some synonym and antonym items were answered incorrectly or with less precise choices, e.g., "Oscillate" was answered as "irradiate" (incorrect synonym), and "Indolent" was answered as "contrite" (incorrect synonym). This suggests some errors in verbal reasoning or vocabulary knowledge. Overall, the person demonstrates good analytic reasoning and effortful cognitive style, with minor errors in vocabulary items.

## Closure and uncertainty tolerance
The Need for Closure (NFCC) scale is present with 15 items. The person’s responses mostly indicate a moderate to low need for closure and moderate tolerance for uncertainty. For example, they "neither agree nor disagree" or "disagree a little" with statements such as "I don’t like situations that are uncertain" (Item 1: 3), "I dislike questions which could be answered in many different ways" (Item 2: 3), and "I feel uncomfortable when I don’t understand the reason why an event occurred in my life" (Item 4: 3). They "disagree a little" with "I don’t like to go into a situation without knowing what I can expect from it" (Item 6: 2) and "When I have made a decision, I feel relieved" (Item 7: 2). They also "disagree a little" with impatience-related items such as "When I am confronted with a problem, I’m dying to reach a solution very quickly" (Item 8: 2) and "I would quickly become impatient and irritated if I would not find a solution to a problem immediately" (Item 9: 2). However, they "agree a little" that they "dislike it when a person’s statement could mean many different things" (Item 11: 4) and "enjoy having a clear and structured mode of life" (Item 13: 4). This pattern suggests a moderate preference for order and structure but not a strong intolerance of ambiguity or uncertainty.

For example, they "agree a little" that they "often find it difficult to shop for a gift for a friend" (Item 3), "have a hard time finding clothing that I really love" (Item 4), and "have the highest standards for myself" (Item 5). They "agree strongly" with "I never settle for second best" (Item 6). They also "agree a little" with statements about seeking uniqueness and originality in products (e.g., Items 1-5). This indicates a tendency toward maximizing behavior, with high standards and a preference for optimal choices rather than satisficing.

## Self-concept stability
Self-concept clarity is measured with 12 items. The person strongly disagrees with statements indicating instability or confusion about self-concept, such as "My beliefs about myself often conflict with one another" (Item 1), "On one day I might have one opinion of myself and on another day I might have a different opinion" (Item 2), and "I spend a lot of time wondering about what kind of person I really am" (Item 3). They strongly agree that they "seldom experience conflict between the different aspects of my personality" (Item 6) and "have a clear sense of who I am and what I am" (Item 11). ## Intertemporal patience
Intertemporal choice tasks are present with multiple items comparing smaller-sooner vs. larger-later monetary rewards. The person consistently prefers the larger, later reward over smaller, sooner amounts in most cases, for example choosing $6 in 6 weeks over $3 in 5 weeks (all such items 1-8), $8 in 7 weeks over $4 in 6 weeks, and $10 in 7 weeks over $5 in 5 weeks, until the amounts become equal or the sooner amount is substantially larger. They switch preference to the sooner reward only when the immediate amount is equal or greater than the delayed amount (e.g., $6 in 1 week vs. $6 now, choosing $6 now). This pattern indicates moderate patience and willingness to wait for larger rewards, with some discounting of delayed rewards but not extreme impulsivity.
