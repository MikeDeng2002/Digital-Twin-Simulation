The respondent’s moderate conscientiousness and low preference for delayed rewards suggest limited long-term financial planning or saving behavior.

The respondent’s financial literacy is moderate, with some errors (e.g., incorrectly identifying highest return asset as savings account), which may influence risk attitudes.

## Social preferences  
The respondent’s social preferences are strongly self-oriented and low in altruism. In dictator and ultimatum games, the respondent proposes to keep $2 and give $3 to the other person but rejects offers where the other keeps $4 or $5, indicating moderate selfishness toward strangers. When in the receiver role, the respondent keeps all money and sends nothing back, showing distrust and selfishness. The respondent rates low on altruism, compassion, loyalty, and family collectivism values (all 1), and behavioral responses confirm low generosity to strangers. There is no evidence of family-oriented social preferences; the respondent disagrees with family duty and collectivism items. This indicates a consistent pattern of selfish stranger-oriented social preferences and weak family collectivism.

They exhibit moderate minimalism, preferring simplicity and avoiding excess possessions, and show risk-averse and present-biased financial preferences. Socially, they are selfish toward strangers and show little family collectivism. Their consumer behavior is likely to be cautious and budget-oriented, avoiding premium or indulgent purchases. Pricing and product choices will reflect a preference for essential, mainstream items with sensitivity to cost and minimal interest in social or status-driven consumption.
