## Effort regulation and cognitive style  
The respondent exhibits a low need for cognition, as indicated by strong agreement with items such as "Thinking is not my idea of fun" (Need for Cognition, item 3 = 5) and "I only think as hard as I have to" (item 7 = 5), and strong disagreement with items reflecting enjoyment of complex or abstract thinking (items 1, 2, 5, 10, 11, 13, 14, 15 all = 1). This low motivation to engage in effortful thinking is consistent with their cognitive test performance, which is average to below average: they self-estimate 21 correct out of 42 cognitive questions, below the population average estimate of 25. Their answers to cognitive puzzles and syllogisms show some correct logical reasoning but also errors (e.g., incorrectly judging some syllogisms as invalid). The respondent’s conscientiousness is moderate to neutral (Big Five item 3 "Does a thorough job" = 3; item 13 "Is a reliable worker" = 3; item 28 "Perseveres until the task is finished" = 3), indicating neither strong nor weak effort regulation. Overall, the respondent appears to engage in minimal cognitive effort, preferring simple, routine tasks over complex or novel problems, consistent with their self-reported low curiosity and low openness to experience (e.g., "Is original" = 1, "Is curious about many different things" = 3, "Is ingenious" = 1).  

## Closure and uncertainty tolerance  
The respondent strongly prefers closure and order, as shown by high agreement with "I don’t like situations that are uncertain" (NFCC item 1 = 5), "I dislike questions which could be answered in many different ways" (item 2 = 5), "I feel uncomfortable when I don’t understand the reason why an event occurred" (item 4 = 5), and "I don’t like to go into a situation without knowing what I can expect" (item 6 = 5). They also strongly dislike unpredictability in others (item 10 = 5) and ambiguous statements (item 11 = 5). They show some impatience with unresolved problems ("I would quickly become impatient and irritated if I would not find a solution immediately" = 4). However, they do not report feeling relief upon making decisions (item 7 = 1), suggesting that while they seek closure, they may not experience comfort from it. Behavioral signals such as relatively quick decision-making in intertemporal choice tasks (preferring smaller-sooner rewards until the delayed reward is substantially better) and consistent acceptance/rejection patterns in ultimatum and dictator games indicate a preference for decisiveness and consistency. Free-response length is limited, and the respondent’s self-descriptions emphasize skepticism and introversion, which may reflect a cautious approach to uncertainty. Overall, the respondent shows a strong intolerance of uncertainty and a preference for quick closure.  

## Optimization strategy  
The respondent scores low on maximizing tendencies, disagreeing strongly with statements like "No matter what I do, I have the highest standards for myself" and "I never settle for second best" (Maximizer items 5 and 6 = 1). They report difficulty shopping for gifts and clothing (items 3 and 4 = 5), which may indicate some maximizing behavior in social or consumer domains. However, their free responses and economic game behavior reveal a pragmatic, self-interested approach rather than striving for optimal outcomes for others. For example, in the dictator and trust games, they keep all money for themselves when in the receiver role and offer a moderate split when proposer, but their thoughts reveal distrust and cynicism about others’ generosity. This suggests a satisficing strategy in social-economic domains, prioritizing self-interest and minimal effort rather than maximizing social outcomes. There is no evidence of domain-specific maximizing; rather, the respondent appears to generally satisfice or avoid effortful optimization.  

## Self-concept stability  
The respondent reports a very stable and clear self-concept, strongly agreeing with "In general, I have a clear sense of who I am and what I am" (SCC item 11 = 5) and strongly disagreeing with items indicating self-concept conflict or fluctuation (items 1, 2, 3, 4, 8, 9 all = 1). There are no contradictions between their self-descriptions and scale responses. Their free responses consistently describe themselves as skeptical, introverted, and self-reliant, with a stable identity centered on independence and bluntness. This coherence between self-report and free text indicates high self-concept clarity and stability.  

## Behavioral vs. stated-preference gap  
A meaningful gap exists between the respondent’s stated values and their economic game behavior. They rate altruism, compassion, forgiveness, and helping others as not important (Values items 3, 9, 17 = 1), and their self-reports indicate low helpfulness and unselfishness (Big Five item 7 = 1). Yet, in the dictator game as proposer, they offer a moderate share ($3 out of $5) to the other person, which is more generous than their stated low altruism would predict. However, as the receiver in the trust game, they keep all money and send nothing back, reflecting distrust and self-interest. Their free-response thoughts during economic games reveal cynicism about others’ generosity and a self-protective, antisocial stance ("People are greedy," "I hate society so I will only look out for myself"). This gap implies that while the respondent claims low altruistic values and distrusts others, they still behave somewhat generously when in control of the offer, possibly as a strategic or social norm compliance behavior rather than genuine altruism. This inconsistency suggests limited reliability of self-report for social preferences and a reasoning style that is guarded and skeptical, potentially leading to unpredictable social decision-making.  

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent shows moderate impatience in intertemporal choice tasks, consistently preferring smaller-sooner rewards over larger-later rewards until the delayed reward is substantially better (e.g., preferring $6 in 6 weeks over $5.75 in 5 weeks, but switching preference at $5.75). This pattern indicates a discounting of future rewards, consistent with moderate temporal impatience. Their Conscientiousness is neutral (Big Five item 3 = 3), and they report neither strong planning nor systematic behavior (e.g., "Makes plans and follows through" = 3). Their free responses do not emphasize long-term planning or delayed gratification. Overall, the respondent exhibits moderate intertemporal impatience with no strong signals of high self-control or planning.  

## Bias susceptibility predictions  
Predictable biases from cognitive signals:  
- Probability matching vs. maximizing: Given the respondent’s low need for cognition and moderate cognitive performance, they are likely to engage in probability matching rather than maximizing in probabilistic tasks. Their moderate cognitive test accuracy and low engagement with complex thinking support this.  
- Denominator neglect: The respondent’s moderate numeracy and some errors in logical reasoning suggest moderate susceptibility to denominator neglect, especially given their difficulty with abstract or complex problems.  
- Belief bias in syllogisms: The respondent shows mixed performance on syllogistic reasoning, sometimes accepting invalid conclusions, indicating moderate belief bias susceptibility.  
- Wason matching bias: Given their low cognitive engagement and some errors in conditional reasoning tasks, they are likely susceptible to Wason matching bias.  

Weakly predictable or not predictable from cognitive signals:  
Framing effects, anchoring, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, outcome bias, sunk-cost effect, less-is-more effect, omission bias, Allais/ certainty effects, and myside bias are not reliably predictable from the available cognitive and personality data. The respondent’s skeptical and self-focused attitude may influence some of these biases, but predictive limits apply.  

## Summary  
This respondent exhibits a low need for cognition and a preference for simple, routine tasks, avoiding complex or abstract thinking. They strongly prefer closure and certainty, showing intolerance for ambiguity and unpredictability. Their optimization strategy is generally satisficing and self-interested, with some inconsistency between stated low altruism and moderate generosity in economic games, revealing a behavioral vs. stated-preference gap rooted in distrust and cynicism. Their self-concept is stable and clear, centered on independence and bluntness. They show moderate impatience in intertemporal choices and moderate susceptibility to cognitive biases related to reasoning under uncertainty. Overall, their reasoning style is cautious, skeptical, and effort-averse, with potential unpredictability in social decision-making due to conflicting attitudes and behaviors.
