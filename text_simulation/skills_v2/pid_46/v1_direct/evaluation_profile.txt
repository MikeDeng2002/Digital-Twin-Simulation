## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the minimalism scale (12 items), the respondent generally agrees moderately with minimalist tendencies: they "agree a little" to avoiding accumulating lots of stuff (item 1), actively avoiding excess possessions (4), being drawn to visually sparse environments (5), preferring simplicity in design strongly (6), preferring leaving spaces visually empty (8), and mindfully selecting belongings (12). They only "agree a little" that they have broken customs/rules in product use (item 5), but otherwise mostly disagree or neither agree nor disagree with statements about violating social group rules or seeking distinctive images through products. They strongly disagree with avoiding popular products or brands. This indicates a low consumer orientation toward uniqueness or consumer rebellion.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery over smaller certain amounts when the certain amount is low, but switches preference to the certain amount as it approaches the expected value of the lottery. For example, for a 50% chance of $6 vs. certain amounts, they prefer the lottery over $0.50 but prefer certain amounts above $1.00. Similar patterns hold for other lotteries with varying payoffs. For lotteries involving losses, the respondent prefers the lottery over smaller losses but switches to preferring smaller certain losses as the amounts improve.  

In ultimatum and dictator games, the respondent proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to the other person when acting as proposer, indicating a moderately generous split. However, when acting as the receiver, they accept offers down to $1 but reject offers of $1 or $0, showing some fairness concerns. In the trust game with tripled amounts, the respondent chooses to keep all the money themselves and send nothing to the other person, and as receiver, they keep all money sent and return nothing. This indicates low trust and reciprocity in economic games involving trust and return.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the smaller, later reward ($6 in 6 weeks) over larger, sooner rewards up to $5.50 in 5 weeks, switching preference only at $5.75 and above. Similar patterns hold for other delayed reward choices, indicating moderate patience but a preference for sooner rewards when the difference is small. For immediate vs. delayed rewards (e.g., $6 in 1 week vs. $5.75 now), the respondent prefers immediate rewards once the immediate amount is close to the delayed amount, indicating some present bias but moderate patience overall.

## Social preferences  
In dictator and ultimatum games, the respondent proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to the other person, showing moderate generosity. As receiver, they accept offers down to $1 but reject offers of $1 or $0, indicating fairness concerns. In the trust game with tripled amounts, the respondent keeps all money themselves and sends nothing back when receiving money, and when sending money, they keep all for themselves and send nothing, indicating low trust and low reciprocity. Their thoughts during these decisions reveal a cynical view of others' generosity and a self-protective, individualistic stance.
