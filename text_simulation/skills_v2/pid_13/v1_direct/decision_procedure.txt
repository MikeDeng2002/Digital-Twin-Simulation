However, some items related to abstract thinking and novelty (Items 13 and 14) are disagreed with or only slightly agreed, suggesting a nuanced cognitive style.

Regarding cognitive test items, the person answered 30 out of 42 questions correctly and estimated that the average US adult would answer 22 correctly, indicating above-average performance. They correctly solved various logical syllogisms and reasoning tasks, such as the syllogism "All the XAR's are YOF's" with correct conclusions (e.g., Item: "If a glock is a XAR, it is certain that the glock is a YOF"). This suggests some susceptibility to intuitive traps in spatial reasoning but solid performance in verbal and logical reasoning.

The person strongly disagrees with statements such as "When I am in the car listening to the radio, I often check other stations to see if something better is playing" (Item 1, Disagree strongly) and "No matter how satisfied I am with my job, it’s only right for me to be on the lookout for better opportunities" (Item 2, Disagree strongly). ## Intertemporal patience
The intertemporal choice tasks show a preference for larger, later rewards over smaller, sooner ones, indicating moderate patience. For example, the person consistently chooses to wait for $6 in 6 weeks rather than smaller amounts sooner (Items 1-5 in the first matrix), switching to preferring the sooner option only when the immediate amount approaches the delayed amount (Item 6 and beyond). Similar patterns are observed for $8 in 7 weeks and $10 in 7 weeks lotteries. When choosing between $6 in 1 week and smaller amounts now, the person switches preference to the immediate option only when the immediate amount is close to the delayed amount (Item 4 and beyond). This pattern is consistent across multiple matrices, showing a reasonable discounting of future rewards but a willingness to wait for better payoffs. In the lottery exchange tasks, the person consistently prefers the certain monetary amounts over lotteries with equal or higher expected values, indicating risk aversion or cautiousness in uncertain gains and losses. In social economic games, the person proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to another, and when receiving, tends to keep a fair share but also returns a substantial portion, reflecting fairness and reciprocity considerations.
