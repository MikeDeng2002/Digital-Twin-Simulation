## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the 12-item minimalism scale, the respondent strongly agrees with avoiding accumulating lots of stuff (item 1), actively avoiding excess possessions (item 4), and endorses "less is more" in owning things (item 3) and being thoughtful about what she owns (item 11). She somewhat agrees with restricting the number of things owned (item 2) and keeping a sparse aesthetic at home (item 7). Other items show neutral or mixed responses. Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking, the respondent disagrees somewhat with statements about combining possessions to create a unique image (item 1) and challenging prevailing tastes (item 8). She agrees a little that she likes more interesting versions of products (item 2), that having an eye for unusual products helps establish a distinctive image (item 4), and that she has sometimes violated social rules about what to buy or own (item 6). She neither agrees nor disagrees with avoiding common products (items 9-11), but agrees a little that she is less interested in commonplace products (item 12). Overall, she shows a moderate minimalist orientation with some interest in uniqueness but limited consumerism or rebelliousness.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery over smaller certain amounts when the certain amount is substantially less than the expected value, indicating moderate risk tolerance. For example, she prefers a 50% chance of $6 over certain amounts up to $1.75 but switches to preferring certain amounts at $2 or more. Similar patterns hold for other lotteries with varying payoffs. In loss lotteries, she prefers the lottery over smaller certain losses but switches preference as the certain loss approaches the expected loss, indicating loss aversion. In social economic games (dictator and ultimatum), she proposes to keep $3 and send $2 out of $5 and accepts all offers as the receiver, including the lowest offers. In the trust game, she sends $2 and returns a substantial portion of the tripled amount, indicating moderate trust and reciprocity. Overall, the respondent exhibits moderate risk tolerance with some loss aversion and a fair, reciprocal social preference.

## Intertemporal patience  
In intertemporal choice tasks involving delayed monetary rewards, the respondent consistently prefers the larger, later reward ($6 in 6 weeks vs. smaller amounts sooner, $8 in 7 weeks vs. smaller amounts sooner, $10 in 7 weeks vs. smaller amounts sooner) until the immediate amount approaches or exceeds the delayed amount. She chooses the delayed reward over immediate amounts up to about $5.75-$7.75 but switches to immediate rewards when they equal or exceed the delayed amount. This pattern indicates moderate patience and discounting consistent with typical delay discounting behavior.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator and ultimatum games, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 out of $5 and accepts all offers as the receiver, including the lowest offers ($0). In the trust game, when sending money that is tripled, she chooses to send $2 out of $5 and, as the receiver, returns a substantial portion of the tripled amount (e.g., keeps $9 and sends $6 back when receiving $15). These choices indicate a preference for fairness and reciprocity, with willingness to share but also to keep a reasonable share. She shows no rejection of low offers, suggesting low spite or punishment motives.

## Summary  
The respondent has a relatively high household income with part-time employment and a larger household size. Her values emphasize social harmony, trust, humility, compassion, and equality, while placing low importance on power, status, and tradition. She exhibits a strong minimalist orientation, actively avoiding excess possessions and valuing thoughtful ownership, with moderate interest in uniqueness but limited consumerism. Her risk preferences show moderate risk tolerance and loss aversion. She demonstrates moderate patience in intertemporal choices, preferring larger delayed rewards unless immediate amounts are close in value. Social preferences reveal fairness and reciprocity, proposing equitable splits and accepting all offers in economic games. Overall, her directly measured preferences suggest a socially oriented, fair-minded individual with moderate risk and time preferences and a minimalist consumer style.
