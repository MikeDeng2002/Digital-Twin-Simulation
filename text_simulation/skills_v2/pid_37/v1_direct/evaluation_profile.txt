## Values hierarchy  
The 24-item values scale shows some rating compression but with meaningful variation. The highest-rated values (rated 9) are Loyalty (Item 11), Harmony (Item 14), Excitement (Item 15), and Autonomy (Item 20). The lowest-rated values (rated 1 or 2) include Power (Item 13), Tradition (Item 23), and Superiority (Item 24) rated 1, and Wealth (Item 1), Ambition (Item 10), Status (Item 18) rated 2. Other values such as Trust, Competence, Politeness, Compassion, Civility, and Equality are rated relatively high (mostly 8). Achievement is rated moderately low (4). This indicates a preference for social harmony, loyalty, personal freedom, and excitement, with low importance placed on power, status, and traditional values.

## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the minimalism scale (12 items), the respondent strongly agrees with avoiding accumulating lots of stuff (Item 1), actively avoiding excess possessions (Item 4), and keeping a sparse aesthetic in their home (Item 7). They also agree somewhat with restricting the number of things owned (Item 2) and being thoughtful and mindful about possessions (Items 9-12). They neither agree nor disagree on some aesthetic preferences (Items 5, 6, 8). Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking, the respondent neither agrees nor disagrees with statements about creating a unique personal image through possessions (Item 1), but agrees a little with enjoying originality (Item 2), violating social norms about products (Item 6), and challenging prevailing tastes (Item 8). They generally do not strongly seek uniqueness by buying special products or brands (Item 3). Overall, the respondent values minimalism and is somewhat inclined to challenge social norms in consumption but does not strongly seek uniqueness through products.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent shows moderate risk aversion in gains: for lotteries with 50% chance to win amounts between $6 and $10, they prefer the certain amounts once these reach about $2 or more, indicating a willingness to accept sure gains over risky prospects at relatively low thresholds. For lotteries involving losses, the respondent prefers to avoid larger losses, accepting sure smaller losses over risky larger losses. They also accept exchanging lotteries with mixed gains and losses for certain amounts, indicating risk-averse behavior in losses and mixed gambles. In the ultimatum and dictator games, the respondent proposes to keep $2 and send $3 to the other person and would accept all offers as a receiver, including the most selfish ($5 for other, $0 for self). In the trust game with tripled amounts, they keep $2 and send $3, and as receiver, they keep roughly half to slightly more than half of the tripled amount and return the rest, indicating moderate trust and reciprocity. Overall, the respondent exhibits moderate risk aversion and fairness preferences consistent with balanced social preferences.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the smaller, sooner reward over the larger, later reward in all intertemporal choice tasks where the delayed reward is $6 to $10 in 6-7 weeks versus smaller amounts 1-2 weeks earlier or immediately. They only switch preference when the immediate amount equals or exceeds the delayed amount, indicating relatively low patience or high discounting of future rewards over delays of a few weeks.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator/ultimatum games, the respondent offers to keep $2 and send $3 out of $5, showing a preference for sharing more than half with the other person. As a receiver, they accept all offers, including the most selfish. In the trust game with tripled amounts, they keep $2 and send $3, and as receiver, they keep about half to slightly more than half and return the rest, indicating moderate trust and reciprocity. Their thoughts during these decisions emphasize splitting money roughly evenly and considering how much the other person has left. This suggests a social preference for fairness and cooperation, with moderate generosity and trust.

## Summary  
The respondent has a modest budget constraint with family income between $30,000 and $50,000, currently unemployed, and living alone. Their values emphasize loyalty, harmony, autonomy, and excitement, while rejecting power, status, and tradition. They strongly endorse minimalism and mindful ownership, with some inclination to challenge social norms in consumption but without strong uniqueness-seeking. Risk preferences indicate moderate risk aversion in gains and losses, with social preferences favoring fairness, moderate generosity, and trust. Intertemporal choices reveal relatively low patience for delayed rewards over short time horizons. Overall, the respondent values social harmony and autonomy, prefers simplicity in possessions, and exhibits balanced risk and social preferences.
