## Budget constraint  
The respondent reports a total family income from all sources before taxes in the range of $50,000 to $75,000 (Question: Last year, total family income). Employment status is part-time employment. Household size is three people including the respondent. These data provide a moderate budget envelope without any inference about spending style.

## Values hierarchy  
The 24-item values scale shows a compressed rating distribution, with many values rated 7 or 8 on a 9-point scale, indicating acquiescence bias. Despite this compression, the highest-rated value is TRUST (item 5) rated 9 (Highly Important). Other relatively high values rated 8 include WEALTH (1), PLEASURE (2), FORGIVENESS (3), INFLUENCE (4), COMPETENCE (6), AMBITION (10), LOYALTY (11), POLITENESS (12), POWER (13), HONESTY (16), COMPASSION (17), STATUS (18), and CIVILITY (19). The lowest relative value is HARMONY (14) rated 6, which is notably lower than most others. Thus, TRUST stands out as uniquely highest, and HARMONY as relatively low.

## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
The respondent strongly agrees with minimalism-related items, such as avoiding accumulating lots of stuff (item 1), restricting the number of things owned (2), and endorsing "less is more" (3), all rated 5 (Agree strongly). Other minimalism items mostly range from 4 to 5, indicating a strong minimalist orientation. Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking, the respondent generally disagrees or is neutral on statements about seeking uniqueness through products (items 1-4 on consumerism scale rated mostly 2 or 3), indicating low consumerism and uniqueness-seeking. However, there is slight agreement (4) with challenging prevailing tastes by buying something others would not accept (item 8), and mild agreement (4) with disliking products customarily bought by everyone (items 11 and 12). Overall, the respondent is strongly minimalist and shows low to moderate consumerism/uniqueness-seeking.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over certain smaller amounts only when the certain amount is substantially lower than the expected lottery value, indicating moderate risk aversion. For example, in a 50% chance to win $6 or $0 lottery, the respondent prefers the lottery over amounts up to about $3 but switches preference at $3.25 and above. In lotteries with higher stakes, the respondent shows similar patterns, often preferring the lottery at lower certain amounts but switching to certain amounts as they increase. For losses, the respondent consistently prefers the sure loss over lotteries with higher expected losses, indicating risk aversion in losses. Overall, the respondent exhibits moderate risk aversion in gains and losses.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the smaller, sooner reward over the larger, later reward in all intertemporal choice tasks until the delayed reward equals or exceeds the immediate reward, at which point she switches preference to the delayed reward. This pattern indicates a relatively high discount rate and low patience for delayed rewards, preferring immediate smaller gains over delayed larger gains unless the delayed gain is clearly superior.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator game where the respondent proposes how to split $5, she chooses to keep all $5 for herself (offer: $5 for self, $0 for other). However, when playing as the receiver, she accepts all offers from $0 to $5 sent to her, including the offer where the sender keeps all $5 and sends $0. In the trust game where money sent is tripled, the respondent chooses to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person as sender, indicating some willingness to share. As receiver, she tends to keep a majority of the money sent back but returns a substantial portion (e.g., keeps $10 of $15 and sends back $5). These choices indicate a self-interested but somewhat reciprocal social preference, with a tendency to maximize own payoff but some willingness to share and reciprocate.

## Summary  
The respondent has a moderate budget with part-time employment and a household of three. Her values show a compressed pattern but highlight TRUST as uniquely important and HARMONY as relatively less important. She strongly endorses minimalism and shows low to moderate consumerism and uniqueness-seeking. Risk preferences indicate moderate risk aversion in both gains and losses. Intertemporal choices reveal low patience for delayed rewards, favoring immediate smaller payoffs. Social preferences reflect self-interest with some reciprocity and sharing in trust situations. Overall, the respondent values trust and minimalism, is moderately risk-averse, impatient with delayed rewards, and socially self-interested but not entirely selfish.
