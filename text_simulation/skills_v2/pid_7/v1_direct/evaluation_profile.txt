## Budget constraint  
The respondent reports a total family income before taxes in the range of $75,000 to $100,000 (Question: Last year, total family income). Employment status is unemployed at the time of the survey. Household size is two people. No further direct income or employment details are provided.

## Values hierarchy  
The 24-item values scale shows evidence of rating compression, with many values rated highly (7 to 9). Despite this, relative structure can be extracted. The highest-rated values (rated 9) include Pleasure (item 2), Influence (4), Competence (6), Achievement (8), Ambition (10), Honesty (16), Compassion (17), Status (18), Autonomy (20), and Recognition (22). The lowest-rated value is Power (13), rated 1. Other relatively low values (rated 4) include Altruism (9), Harmony (14), Tradition (23), and Superiority (24). This indicates a strong emphasis on personal success, influence, and integrity, with low importance placed on dominance or control over others.

## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the minimalism scale (12 items), the respondent generally disagrees with minimalist statements, indicating low endorsement of minimalism. For example, "I avoid accumulating lots of stuff" is strongly disagreed with (1), and "I am drawn to visually sparse environments" is strongly disagreed with (1). However, the respondent agrees strongly (5) that their belongings are mindfully selected and carefully curated (items 10, 11, 12). This suggests a preference for thoughtful ownership rather than minimalism per se.

Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking, the respondent strongly agrees (5) with all four statements about seeking uniqueness and originality in products (items 1-4), such as creating a personal image that cannot be duplicated and buying special products or brands. They also agree somewhat (4) with statements about breaking social customs and challenging prevailing tastes (items 5-8). The respondent tends to avoid products popular among the general population to some extent (items 9-12 mostly rated 4, except item 12 rated 2). This indicates a strong consumer orientation toward uniqueness and nonconformity.

## Risk preferences  
Direct responses to lottery and risk preference tasks are present. The respondent generally prefers the lottery option over small certain amounts only when the certain amount is relatively low, indicating moderate risk aversion. For example, for a 50% chance of $6 vs. certain amounts, the respondent prefers the lottery over amounts up to about $2.25 but switches preference to the certain amount at $2.50 and above. Similar patterns hold for other lotteries with varying payoffs. For lotteries involving losses, the respondent consistently prefers the lottery over smaller certain losses, indicating risk aversion in losses as well. Overall, the respondent shows moderate risk aversion in gains and losses.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent’s choices in delayed-reward tasks show a preference for immediate smaller rewards over delayed larger rewards when the difference is small, but switches to delayed rewards when the immediate reward is substantially smaller. For example, for $6 in 6 weeks vs. $5 in 5 weeks, the respondent prefers the delayed $6; for $6 in 6 weeks vs. $4.50 in 5 weeks, the respondent prefers the delayed $6; but for $6 in 6 weeks vs. $5.00 in 5 weeks, the respondent prefers the sooner $5. This pattern suggests moderate patience with some present bias. Similar patterns are observed across other delayed reward comparisons.

## Social preferences  
In dictator and ultimatum game scenarios, the respondent chooses to keep the entire amount for themselves when acting as the proposer (keeping $5 and sending $0). When acting as the receiver, the respondent accepts all offers, including the offer of $0 to themselves. In the trust game where money sent is tripled, the respondent again keeps all the money for themselves and sends nothing to the other person, and as the receiver, keeps all the money and sends nothing back. This indicates a highly self-interested social preference profile with minimal altruism or trust in anonymous others.

## Summary  
The respondent has a moderate family income but is currently unemployed, living in a two-person household. Their values emphasize personal achievement, influence, competence, honesty, compassion, and autonomy, while placing very low importance on power and dominance. They do not endorse minimalism but prefer to own possessions that are carefully and mindfully selected. They strongly seek uniqueness and nonconformity in consumer products. Risk preferences indicate moderate risk aversion in both gains and losses. Intertemporal choices reveal moderate patience with some present bias. Social preference measures show a self-interested stance, keeping all money in economic games and accepting all offers as a receiver.
