## Effort regulation and cognitive style  
This individual demonstrates a moderately high Need for Cognition (NFC), consistently agreeing "a little" or "strongly" with statements reflecting enjoyment of complex problems, intellectual challenge, and deep thinking (e.g., "I would prefer complex to simple problems" = 4, "I like to have the responsibility of handling a situation that requires a lot of thinking" = 4, "I find satisfaction in deliberating hard and for long hours" = 4). They reject avoidance of effortful thinking and show a preference for analytical engagement. Cognitive test performance supports this: they correctly answered most cognitive puzzles, including logical syllogisms and spatial rotation tasks, with only minor errors (e.g., some ambiguity in cube rotation tasks, selecting plausible but not always perfect rotations). Their vocabulary and verbal reasoning are strong, as shown by synonym and antonym tests with mostly correct answers, indicating good crystallized intelligence.  

Big Five Conscientiousness is high, with strong agreement on being organized, efficient, systematic, practical, and careful (scores mostly 8-9 on these traits). Free responses reveal a self-critical but conscientious thinker who "wants to be nicer" and recognizes personal flaws, indicating reflective metacognition. The individual’s self-description as "a little harsh and callous" but "wishing to change" suggests a capacity for self-monitoring and effortful self-regulation.  

Overall, the person exhibits a deliberate, systematic cognitive style with moderate to high effort regulation, favoring thoroughness and accuracy over speed or impulsivity. They are not prone to careless errors but may occasionally overthink or struggle with changing ingrained habits.

## Closure and uncertainty tolerance  
The Need for Closure (NFCC) is very high, with strong agreement on disliking uncertainty, ambiguity, and unpredictability (e.g., "I don’t like situations that are uncertain" = 5, "I dislike unpredictable situations" = 5, "I feel irritated when one person disagrees with what everyone else believes" = 5). They prefer structured, ordered environments and feel relief upon making decisions, indicating a low tolerance for ambiguity and a strong desire for cognitive closure.  

Behaviorally, this is reflected in their intertemporal choices, where they consistently prefer the smaller-sooner reward over larger-later rewards until the amounts become equal or better, showing impatience and a preference for certainty. Their free responses emphasize wanting to be dependable and responsible, which aligns with a preference for predictability and order.  

This person likely experiences discomfort with ambiguous or complex uncertain situations and seeks to resolve uncertainty quickly, potentially leading to premature closure or impatience in decision-making under uncertainty.

## Optimization strategy  
The individual scores low to moderate on Maximizer tendencies, disagreeing or only slightly agreeing with statements about never settling for second best or having the highest standards (e.g., "I never settle for second best" = 2, "No matter what I do, I have the highest standards for myself" = 3). They do not report difficulty shopping or gift selection, suggesting a practical rather than perfectionistic approach to choices.  

In economic games, they propose fair splits (e.g., offering $2 to self and $3 to other in dictator game) and accept all offers as receiver, indicating a cooperative but not overly self-sacrificing stance. Their thoughts during these tasks emphasize kindness, fairness, and compassion, but also a pragmatic sense of self-interest ("I need the money but the other person might need it also").  

They show some openness to re-evaluation (agreeing "I often end up deliberating about issues even when they do not affect me personally" = 4), but also a tendency to commit once decisions are made (high NFCC). This suggests a satisficing rather than maximizing strategy, balancing fairness and efficiency without excessive indecision or regret.

However, their self-reports of kindness and fairness in decision-making contrast with some self-admitted harshness, indicating a complex self-image with some aspirational bias.  

Behavioral economic choices align more with stated values of fairness and trust than with self-critical harshness, suggesting that in practice, the person acts more prosocially than their self-judgment might imply.

## Intertemporal patience  
The individual shows low intertemporal patience, consistently preferring immediate smaller rewards over delayed larger rewards until the amounts are equal or better. This pattern indicates a strong present bias and impatience for delayed gratification.  

Conscientiousness is high, which often correlates with better planning and patience, but here the impatience in monetary choices suggests a domain-specific impulsivity or a pragmatic preference for certainty over delayed gains. Free responses do not mention procrastination but do reveal some difficulty changing self, which may reflect challenges in long-term self-regulation.  

Overall, the person is likely to discount future rewards steeply, favoring immediate outcomes and showing limited willingness to wait, especially when delays are short and rewards only moderately larger.

- Denominator neglect: Low likelihood, as person shows good numeracy and base-rate understanding.  
- Belief bias: Moderate, as logical reasoning is generally sound but some errors in syllogisms suggest occasional influence of belief content.  
- Wason matching bias: Moderate to low, given good performance on Wason selection tasks.

**MODERATE CONFIDENCE**

- Risk-benefit nonseparability (affect heuristic): Moderate, as emotional responses (e.g., compassion) influence decisions but cognitive control is strong.

**LOW CONFIDENCE**

They adopt a satisficing rather than maximizing strategy, balancing fairness and pragmatism in decisions. Their self-concept is fairly stable but marked by aspirational tension, especially regarding compassion and kindness. Behavioral choices in economic games reveal more prosociality than their self-critical free responses suggest, indicating a gap between self-judgment and actual behavior. Cognitive test performance is strong, with minor logical errors but good numeracy and reasoning. The most predictive feature of their reasoning profile is the pronounced need for closure combined with moderate cognitive ability, which shapes their impatience and preference for quick, decisive judgments under uncertainty.
