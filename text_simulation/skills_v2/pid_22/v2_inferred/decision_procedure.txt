## Effort regulation and cognitive style  
The respondent shows a moderately high need for cognition, agreeing strongly with items such as enjoying complex problems (NFC 13, 14, 15, 18) and preferring intellectual challenges, while disagreeing strongly with statements indicating avoidance of thinking (NFC 3, 4, 5, 12, 16, 17). This is consistent with their self-description as ingenious, original, curious, and a deep thinker (Big Five 5, 10, 15, 20, 25). Cognitive test performance is solid but not perfect: they correctly answered many logic and spatial reasoning puzzles but made some errors (e.g., selecting answer 3 for the question about the 15-year mortgage’s total interest, and some logical syllogism errors such as denying the conclusion that a Honda is expensive). They estimated their own cognitive test performance as 30/42, below the population average estimate of 35/42, indicating some modest underconfidence or realistic self-assessment. The respondent perseveres until tasks are finished (Big Five 28 = 5) and values doing things efficiently (33 = 5), aligning with conscientiousness traits (Big Five 3 = 4, 13 = 5). Overall, the respondent exhibits a reflective, effortful cognitive style with a preference for deep thinking and problem-solving, supported by conscientiousness but with some modest cognitive test errors and realistic self-appraisal.

## Closure and uncertainty tolerance  
The respondent’s Need for Closure (NFCC) profile is mixed but leans toward moderate closure: they neither strongly dislike uncertainty nor strongly seek immediate closure (NFCC 1 = 3, 6 = 3, 8 = 3, 9 = 4, 11 = 2). They agree a little that they dislike ambiguous statements and prefer a well-ordered life (NFCC 2, 3, 4, 11, 12, 13 = 4), but do not become impatient quickly (NFCC 9 = 2). Their free responses indicate awareness of life’s uncertainties and a realist perspective that “anything is possible at any moment,” suggesting a cognitive style that tolerates uncertainty but remains vigilant. Decision consistency is moderate; they show some deliberation but do not rush to closure. They also report putting off decisions more than before (depression-related item), but generally make decisions as well as before. Overall, the respondent tolerates uncertainty moderately well, prefers some structure, but does not rush to closure or avoid ambiguity.

## Optimization strategy  
The respondent’s Maximizer scale responses indicate a tendency away from maximizing: they disagree strongly with searching for better options when satisfied (Maximizer 1 = 1), and disagree a little with never settling for second best (6 = 2). They agree a little that they have high standards for themselves (5 = 4) and sometimes find it hard to find clothing they love (4 = 4), but overall do not strongly endorse maximizing behavior. Their purchasing behavior is consistent with a preference for uniqueness and nonconformity (e.g., disagreeing strongly with liking run-of-the-mill products, and strongly agreeing with avoiding popular brands and breaking social rules about products). This suggests a selective maximizing strategy focused on personal distinctiveness rather than broad maximization. In economic games, they propose keeping $3 of $5 and sending $2, indicating a moderate fairness orientation but not full generosity. In the trust game, they keep $4 and send $1, showing more cautious or satisficing behavior in trust contexts. Thus, the respondent maximizes selectively in domains related to personal identity and creativity but satisficing in social-economic exchanges.

## Self-concept stability  
The respondent reports strong self-concept instability and conflict: they agree strongly that their beliefs about themselves often conflict (SCC 1 = 5), that their self-opinions vary day to day (2, 8, 9 = 5), and that they spend a lot of time wondering who they really are (3 = 5). They also feel sometimes not like the person they appear to be (4 = 5) and have difficulty recalling past self-consistency (5 = 5). However, they also agree a little that they have a clear sense of who they are (11 = 4) and strongly disagree that they find it hard to make up their mind (12 = 1). Their free responses confirm this tension: they describe themselves as “sad, inconsistent but mostly reliable,” “a realist,” and struggling with a “broken compass” regarding purpose. This indicates a self-concept that is both reflective and conflicted, with ongoing internal dialogue and some instability but not complete fragmentation.

## Behavioral vs. stated-preference gap  
There is a notable gap between the respondent’s stated values and their economic game behavior. They rate altruism and compassion as highly important values (Values 9 and 17 = 9), and emphasize fairness, sharing, and community in their thoughts during economic decisions. However, in the dictator game, they keep $3 and send $2, which is moderately generous but not fully altruistic. In the trust game, they keep $4 and send $1, showing more self-protective behavior. This gap suggests that while the respondent aspires to be fair and altruistic, their actual behavior is more cautious and self-interested, possibly reflecting realism or protective pragmatism. This gap implies that their reasoning under uncertainty may be influenced by a tension between idealistic values and pragmatic self-interest, reducing the reliability of self-reports as pure indicators of behavior.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent shows moderate intertemporal patience. In delayed reward tasks, they prefer immediate smaller rewards over delayed larger ones when the difference is small (e.g., choosing $3 now over $4 in 5 weeks), but switch to preferring delayed rewards as the difference grows. This pattern indicates a typical discounting curve with some patience but also sensitivity to reward magnitude. Their conscientiousness is moderately high (Big Five 3, 13 = 4-5; Conscientiousness self-ratings 7 = 7), and they report making plans and following through (Big Five 38 = 4). However, their free responses and depression symptoms suggest some inconsistency in motivation and energy. Overall, the respondent is moderately patient with future rewards but may experience fluctuations in self-regulation.

## Bias susceptibility predictions  
Predictable biases:  
- Probability matching vs. maximizing: Given the respondent’s moderate cognitive reflection and preference for deliberation, they are likely to maximize rather than probability match in repeated choice tasks. Their cognitive style and performance on CRT-like items support this.  
- Denominator neglect: Their good understanding of probabilities and percentages (e.g., correctly converting 20/100 to 20%) suggests low susceptibility to denominator neglect.  
- Belief bias in syllogisms: The respondent made some logical errors (e.g., incorrectly judging the conclusion about Honda cars), indicating moderate susceptibility to belief bias.  
- Wason matching bias: They correctly identified cards to test the rule (A and 7), indicating low susceptibility to Wason matching bias.

Not predictable or weakly predictable biases:  
Framing effects, anchoring, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, outcome bias, sunk-cost effect, less-is-more effect, omission bias, Allais/ certainty effects, and myside bias are not strongly predictable from the available cognitive and personality data. The respondent’s moderate openness and reflective style may reduce some biases, but no strong claims can be made.

## Summary  
The respondent exhibits a reflective and effortful cognitive style with a preference for complex problem-solving and intellectual engagement, supported by conscientiousness. They tolerate uncertainty moderately well, preferring some structure but not rushing to closure. Their optimization strategy is selective, maximizing in domains related to personal identity and creativity but satisficing in social-economic decisions. Their self-concept is unstable and conflicted, with frequent internal contradictions. A meaningful gap exists between their altruistic values and more self-protective economic behavior, suggesting tension between ideals and pragmatism. They show moderate intertemporal patience and are likely to maximize in probability tasks, with moderate susceptibility to belief bias but low susceptibility to Wason matching bias.
