## Budget constraint  
The respondent reports a total family income of $100,000 or more before taxes (item 24). She is self-employed (item 29) and lives in a household of three people (item 25). These responses provide a direct indication of her budget envelope without inference about spending style.

## Values hierarchy  
The 24-item values scale shows some rating compression, with many values rated moderately high. Explicitly, the respondent rated ALTRUISM (helping others in need, item 9), COMPASSION (caring for others, item 17), AUTONOMY (independence, item 20), and EQUALITY (human rights and equal opportunity, item 21) as the highest-rated values at 9 ("Highly Important to me"). The lowest-rated values, all rated 1 ("Not Important to me"), include POWER (control over others, item 13), STATUS (high rank, item 18), and SUPERIORITY (defeating competition, item 24). WEALTH (financial success, item 1) and RECOGNITION (fame, item 22) are also rated very low (2). This pattern indicates a strong relative preference for prosocial and egalitarian values and a low valuation of dominance, status, and material success.

## Minimalism and consumer orientation  
On the minimalism scale (12 items), the respondent shows low endorsement of minimalism. She disagrees or only slightly agrees with statements about avoiding accumulation or preferring sparse environments (items 1, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 all rated 1 or 2). However, she agrees somewhat that she is mindful and thoughtful about what she owns and that her belongings are carefully curated (items 9-12 rated 4). This suggests a moderate mindful consumer orientation but not a strong minimalist preference.

Regarding consumerism and uniqueness-seeking, the respondent generally disagrees or is neutral about seeking uniqueness through products (items 1-3 rated 2 or 3). She agrees somewhat that having an eye for unusual products helps establish a distinctive image (item 4 rated 4) and strongly agrees that she has violated social group rules about what to buy or own (item 6 rated 5). She also strongly agrees with avoiding products popular among the general population (items 9-11 rated 5), indicating a preference for uniqueness and nonconformity in consumption. She disagrees strongly with breaking customs in product use (item 5 rated 1), suggesting selective rule-breaking.

## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over smaller certain amounts only when the certain amount is substantially lower than the expected value, indicating risk aversion. For example, in a 50% chance to win $6 lottery, she prefers the lottery over certain amounts up to about $3.50 but switches to the certain amount at $4.00 and above. Similar patterns hold for other lotteries with varying payoffs. She also prefers to avoid lotteries with losses, consistently choosing the safer or less negative option. This pattern indicates moderate risk aversion in gains and strong aversion to losses.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent’s choices in intertemporal choice tasks show a preference for immediate smaller rewards over delayed larger rewards when the difference is small but switches to delayed rewards when the difference increases. For example, she prefers $3 now over $6 in 1 week but switches to $5 or more in 5 weeks over $6 in 6 weeks. This pattern suggests moderate patience with some present bias, valuing sooner rewards unless the delayed reward is sufficiently larger.

## Social preferences  
In dictator and ultimatum game scenarios, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person when deciding the split (item 1 in the final section). When acting as the receiver, she accepts all offers from $0 to $5 sent to her, including the least favorable ($0). In the trust game with tripled amounts, she chooses to keep $4 and send $1 to the other person as sender, and as receiver, she returns a substantial portion of the money received (e.g., keeps $8 and sends $7 back when receiving $15). These choices indicate a moderately generous and reciprocal social preference, balancing self-interest with fairness and cooperation.

## Summary  
The respondent has a household budget consistent with a family income above $100,000 and self-employment status. Her values emphasize altruism, compassion, autonomy, and equality, while she devalues power, status, and material wealth. She shows low minimalist tendencies but a preference for mindful consumption and uniqueness, often avoiding mainstream products. Her risk preferences reveal moderate risk aversion for gains and strong aversion to losses. Intertemporal choices indicate moderate patience with some present bias. Social preference tasks show moderate generosity and reciprocity, proposing fair splits and accepting all offers. Overall, her directly measured preferences suggest a prosocial, mindful consumer with cautious risk attitudes and balanced social fairness concerns.
