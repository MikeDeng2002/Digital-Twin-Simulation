## Effort regulation and cognitive style
The person’s Need for Cognition (NFC) profile is well measured by 18 direct items. They generally agree with statements indicating a preference for effortful thinking and intellectual challenge. For example, they "agree strongly" with liking puzzles to solve (item 13), thinking abstractly (item 14), preferring intellectual and difficult tasks (item 15), and deliberating about issues even when not personally affected (item 18). They "disagree strongly" with statements that downplay thinking effort, such as "thinking is not my idea of fun" (item 3), "I would rather do something that requires little thought" (item 4), and "I usually end up deliberating only as much as I have to" (item 7). This pattern indicates a relatively high NFC.

Regarding cognitive test items, the person answered 42 questions with a self-reported correct count of 30. They correctly solved several syllogistic reasoning problems, such as affirming valid conclusions (e.g., "All things that are smoked are good for health" leading to "Cigarettes are good for health" - answered Yes). They also correctly identified logical inconsistencies (e.g., rejecting the conclusion that "China is not communist" given the premises). However, they made an error on the syllogism "If a car is a Honda, then it is expensive. A car is a Honda. Conclusion: The car is expensive," answering No, which is logically incorrect (affirming the antecedent is valid). This suggests some difficulty with conditional reasoning.

On CRT-style items, the person correctly answered the bat and ball problem with 0.05, and the lily pad doubling problem with 47 days, indicating good analytic reasoning. They also correctly answered the die roll expected value (500) and lottery probability questions. In the cube rotation tasks, the person’s answers were mixed, including selecting "None of the cubes could be a rotation" (item 8 in one cube task), indicating some uncertainty or difficulty with spatial reasoning.

Overall, the person shows a strong preference for cognitive effort and generally good performance on reasoning tasks, with some minor errors in conditional logic and spatial rotation problems.

They "agree a little" that they dislike questions with many possible answers (item 2), prefer a well-ordered life (item 3), feel uncomfortable when not understanding reasons for events (item 4), and feel relieved when making decisions (item 7). However, they "neither agree nor disagree" or "disagree a little" with items indicating impatience or intolerance for ambiguity, such as becoming irritated if no immediate solution is found (item 9) or disliking unpredictable people (item 10). They also "agree strongly" that they do not usually consult many opinions before forming views (item 14), suggesting some decisiveness.

In sum, the person shows a moderate need for closure, valuing order and clarity but not strongly intolerant of ambiguity or unpredictability.

## Intertemporal patience
Intertemporal choice tasks are present with multiple items offering choices between smaller-sooner and larger-later monetary rewards. The person tends to prefer immediate smaller rewards when the delayed reward is only slightly larger (e.g., choosing $3 now over $6 in 1 week), but switches to preferring delayed rewards when the difference is more substantial (e.g., choosing $6 in 6 weeks over $4 in 5 weeks). This pattern is consistent across different amounts and delays, showing moderate patience with some discounting of future rewards.

In social economic games involving splitting money, the person proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to another person, and as a receiver, accepts all offers including the least generous ones. In a trust game with tripled amounts, they keep $4 and send $1, and as receiver, keep a majority of the tripled amount but send back some money, indicating moderate trust and reciprocity.
