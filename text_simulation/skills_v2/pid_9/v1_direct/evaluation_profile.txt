## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over smaller certain amounts when the certain amount is substantially lower than the expected lottery value, but switches preference to the certain amount as it approaches or exceeds the expected lottery value. For example, in a 50% chance to win $6 lottery, they prefer the lottery over certain amounts up to about $2.75, then prefer the certain amount beyond that. Similar patterns hold for other lotteries with varying payoffs. For lotteries involving losses, the respondent prefers the lottery over smaller certain losses but switches preference as the certain loss becomes less severe.

In the ultimatum game, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person and accepts offers down to $1 sent to them but rejects offers of $1 or $0 to them. In the trust game, when receiving multiplied amounts, they keep a majority share but send back a substantial portion (e.g., keep $8 of $15 and send back $7). These choices indicate moderate risk tolerance and a balance between self-interest and fairness in social risk contexts.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the larger delayed reward ($6 in 6 weeks vs. smaller amounts in 5 weeks, $8 in 7 weeks vs. smaller amounts in 6 weeks, $10 in 7 weeks vs. smaller amounts in 5 weeks, etc.) until the immediate amount approaches the delayed amount, at which point they switch preference to the immediate reward. This pattern indicates moderate patience with delayed rewards, showing willingness to wait for larger payoffs but discounting future rewards as the delay or amount difference decreases.

## Social preferences
