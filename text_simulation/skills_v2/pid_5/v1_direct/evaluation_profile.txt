## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over certain smaller amounts when the certain amount is substantially less than the expected lottery value, indicating some risk tolerance. For example, in a 50% chance to win $6 vs. certain amounts, they prefer the lottery over amounts up to about $3.50, switching preference to the certain amount at $4.00 and above. Similar patterns hold for other lotteries with different payoffs. For losses, the respondent prefers the lottery over smaller certain losses, indicating risk aversion in losses but with some tolerance.

In the ultimatum game, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person and would accept all offers from $0 to $5 sent to them, indicating acceptance of all splits. In the dictator game with tripled amounts, the respondent keeps all $5 and sends $0, and as receiver, keeps a majority of the money sent back, indicating self-interest in this context.

Overall, the respondent shows moderate risk tolerance in gains, some risk aversion in losses, and self-interested behavior in economic games.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the larger delayed reward ($6 in 6 weeks) over smaller sooner rewards up to about $5.50 in 5 weeks, switching preference to the sooner reward at $5.75 and above. Similar patterns hold for other delayed reward tasks with different amounts and delays. This indicates moderate patience with some discounting of delayed rewards but willingness to wait for moderately larger amounts.

## Social preferences  
In the ultimatum game, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 to the other person and would accept all offers from the other person, including offers where the other keeps $5 and sends $0. In the dictator game with tripled amounts, the respondent keeps all $5 and sends $0. When receiving money in the tripled game, the respondent tends to keep a majority share and send back a smaller portion, indicating a self-interested but somewhat reciprocal approach. This suggests moderate fairness concerns but a preference for self-interest in social allocation decisions.
