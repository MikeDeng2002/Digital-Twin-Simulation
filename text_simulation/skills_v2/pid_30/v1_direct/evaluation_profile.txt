## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over smaller certain amounts until the certain amount approaches or exceeds the expected value of the lottery, indicating moderate risk tolerance. For example, they prefer a 50% chance at $6 over certain amounts up to about $3.50, switching preference to certain amounts at $4 or more. Similar patterns hold for other lotteries with different payoffs. For losses, the respondent prefers to avoid larger losses, accepting smaller certain losses over lotteries with higher expected losses, indicating risk aversion in losses.  

In ultimatum and dictator games, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 out of $5, and as a receiver, accepts offers down to $1 but rejects offers of $0. In the trust game with tripled amounts, they send the full $5 and expect the other to return about half of the tripled amount, indicating moderate trust and fairness concerns.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers the larger, later reward ($6 in 6 weeks) over smaller, sooner rewards down to $5.75 in 5 weeks, switching preference only when the sooner reward equals or exceeds the later reward. This pattern is consistent across multiple delayed reward tasks with varying amounts and delays. Similarly, for immediate versus delayed rewards, they prefer immediate smaller amounts only when they equal or exceed the delayed amount. This indicates moderate to high patience and willingness to wait for larger delayed rewards.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator/ultimatum games, the respondent offers a moderately fair split ($3 for self, $2 for other) and accepts all offers except the zero offer. In the trust game, they send the full amount to the other person and expect about half of the tripled amount back, indicating a fair and trusting social preference. Their thoughts during these decisions emphasize fairness, reciprocity, and kindness.  

No direct family-versus-stranger context is tested, so no inference is made.
