## Risk preferences  
The survey includes multiple lottery and risk preference tasks. The respondent consistently prefers the lottery option over smaller certain amounts until the certain amount approaches the expected value, indicating moderate risk aversion. For example, in a 50% chance to win $6 lottery, they prefer the lottery over amounts up to about $2.75 but switch to the certain amount at $3.00 and above. Similar patterns hold for other lotteries with varying payoffs. In loss lotteries, they prefer the lottery over smaller losses but switch preference as losses become smaller, indicating risk aversion in losses as well.

In social economic games (dictator and ultimatum), the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 out of $5 (ultimatum proposer) and accepts offers down to $1 (ultimatum responder), rejecting only the offer where they get $0. In the trust game, when sending money that is tripled, they keep $4 and send $1, and when receiving, they tend to keep a majority but send back a substantial portion (e.g., keep $8 and send $7 when receiving $15). These choices indicate moderate generosity and fairness concerns, with some self-interest.

## Intertemporal patience  
The respondent consistently prefers smaller, sooner rewards over larger, later rewards in all intertemporal choice tasks presented. For example, they choose $3 in 5 weeks over $6 in 6 weeks, and similar patterns hold for other amounts and delays. This indicates relatively low patience or high discounting of future rewards.

## Social preferences  
In the dictator and ultimatum games, the respondent proposes to keep $3 and send $2 out of $5, and accepts offers as low as $1, rejecting only $0 offers. In the trust game, they send $1 out of $5 (which is tripled) and when receiving, they keep a majority but return a substantial amount (e.g., keep $8 and send $7 out of $15). These allocations show moderate fairness and reciprocity preferences, balancing self-interest with concern for others.
